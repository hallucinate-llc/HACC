# Oregon Article I, Section 9 Privacy Research Note

## Purpose

This note collects the strongest currently verified Oregon Article I, section 9 privacy authorities that may help frame the privacy dimension of HACC's proposed living-room sleeping arrangement.

The key caution is that these are search-and-seizure cases, not housing-accommodation cases. They do not directly decide whether a housing authority violates Article I, section 9 by structuring a voucher or occupancy decision in a way that forces private life into common space. Their value is narrower: they articulate Oregon's strongest constitutional privacy principles, especially the idea that Article I, section 9 protects freedom from government-imposed scrutiny and is not limited by formal property or contract labels.

## Strongest Oregon Article I, Section 9 Authorities

### 1. State v. Campbell, 306 Or 157, 759 P2d 1040 (1988)

Campbell is the foundational Oregon case for the proposition that Article I, section 9 protects privacy as freedom from certain forms of government scrutiny rather than only physical trespass or formal property interests.

Best propositions:

- Article I, section 9 protects privacy to which a person has a right, not merely privacy that a court later labels a reasonable expectation.
- A privacy interest under Article I, section 9 is an interest in freedom from particular forms of scrutiny.
- The fundamental constitutional question is whether the government practice, if left wholly to government discretion, would significantly impair the people's freedom from scrutiny.

Best quoted language:

"A privacy interest, as that phrase is used in this court's Article I, section 9, opinions, is an interest in freedom from particular forms of scrutiny."

"In deciding whether government practices that make use of these developments are searches, we must decide whether the practice, if engaged in wholly at the discretion of the government, will significantly impair 'the people's' freedom from scrutiny, for the protection of that freedom is the principle that underlies the prohibition on 'unreasonable searches' set forth in Article I, section 9."

Why it helps here:

Campbell is the best source for the broad Oregon privacy principle. If HACC insists that one adult must sleep in the living room, the argument is not that HACC conducted a literal search. The argument is that Oregon constitutional privacy law is especially sensitive to government conduct that forces private life into greater exposure and scrutiny. Campbell supplies the governing concept of freedom from scrutiny.

Limitation:

Campbell is still a warrantless electronic-surveillance case. It provides the principle, not a direct housing holding.

### 2. State v. Lien/Wilverding, 364 Or 750, 441 P3d 185 (2019)

Lien/Wilverding is the cleanest modern Oregon case for two propositions useful here: first, that Article I, section 9 privacy is not defined by private property or contractual rights; and second, that the Campbell freedom-from-scrutiny framework remains controlling.

Best quoted language:

"In Oregon, the right to privacy - the individual freedom from government scrutiny - protected by Article I, section 9, is not defined by private property or contractual rights, although such rights may inform the analysis in a given case."

"This court also has stated that the fundamental question underlying an Article I, section 9, search case is whether the government's conduct, 'if engaged in wholly at the discretion of the government, will significantly impair 'the people's' freedom from scrutiny, for the protection of that freedom is the principle that underlies the prohibition on 'unreasonable searches' set forth in Article I, section 9.'"

Why it helps here:

This is the best authority against a purely formal response from HACC. If HACC argues that voucher size, subsidy size, or bedroom-assignment rules are merely formal occupancy or administrative categories, Lien/Wilverding helps answer that Oregon privacy analysis is not controlled by such labels. The inquiry is functional and privacy-protective.

Limitation:

Lien/Wilverding is still about a police search of garbage. The analogy is strongest on method and principle, not on facts.

### 3. State v. Simons, 375 Or 70 (2026)

Simons is the newest and most useful modernization case. It reaffirms Campbell and Lien/Wilverding while applying Article I, section 9 to long-term digital monitoring even where the defendant used a public Wi-Fi network and accepted terms of service.

Best quoted language:

"As we have said, Article I, section 9, is not 'defined by private property or contractual rights, although such rights may inform the analysis in a given case.'"

"This focus enables us to frame the fundamental question underlying an Article I, section 9, challenge as follows: whether the government's conduct, 'if engaged in wholly at the discretion of the government, will significantly impair 'the people's' freedom from scrutiny, for the protection of that freedom is the principle that underlies the prohibition on 'unreasonable searches' set forth in Article I, section 9.'"

Why it helps here:

Simons is especially useful because it rejects the idea that privacy disappears merely because someone uses a network or arrangement that includes terms, conditions, or some reduced formal control. That is the best recent analogue to a likely housing-authority position that the living-room arrangement is permissible because the household can technically choose how to arrange itself within the voucher.

Limitation:

Again, Simons is a government-monitoring case, not a housing case. It is best used to reinforce the privacy principle, not as direct housing precedent.

## Additional Oregon Authorities Worth Marshaling

### 4. State v. Smith, 327 Or 366, 963 P2d 642 (1998)

Lien/Wilverding quotes Smith for the proposition that Article I, section 9 traditionally protects privacy interests, meaning the individual's interest in freedom from certain forms of governmental scrutiny.

Use:

Smith is worth citing as a reinforcement case when you want to show that Campbell's formulation was not isolated and was later reaffirmed.

### 5. State v. Newcomb, 359 Or 756, 375 P3d 434 (2016)

Lien/Wilverding quotes Newcomb for the proposition that the right to privacy under Article I, section 9 is freedom from scrutiny as determined by social and legal norms of behavior, such as trespass laws and conventions against eavesdropping.

Use:

Newcomb helps connect Article I, section 9 privacy to social and legal norms about what kinds of exposure and observation society treats as intrusive.

### 6. State v. Louis, 296 Or 57, 672 P2d 708 (1983)

Louis is useful for home-privacy framing. Campbell quotes Louis for the proposition that living quarters are the quintessential domain protected by the constitutional guarantee against warrantless searches.

Use:

Louis is one of the best cases for stressing that Oregon gives especially strong protection to private living space. That helps rhetorically when the state actor's solution is to push one adult's sleeping and intimate life out of a bedroom and into common space.

### 7. State v. Tanner, 304 Or 312, 745 P2d 757 (1987)

Campbell relies on Tanner for the proposition that the privacy protected by Article I, section 9 is the privacy to which one has a right.

Use:

Tanner helps if you want to emphasize that Oregon's framework is rights-based rather than limited to fluctuating notions of reasonableness or administrative custom.

## How To Marshal These Authorities

### Best ordering

If these authorities are used in a revised memorandum or attorney packet, the best sequence is:

1. Campbell for the foundational freedom-from-scrutiny test.
2. Lien/Wilverding for the modern statement that privacy is not defined by private property or contract labels.
3. Simons for the newest reaffirmation, especially against arguments based on consent-like terms or public-facing arrangements.
4. Louis and Tanner as older Oregon home-privacy and rights-based support.
5. Smith and Newcomb as reinforcement citations showing doctrinal continuity.

### Strongest way to state the point

The safest formulation is:

"These Oregon Article I, section 9 authorities do not directly decide the housing-accommodation issue presented here. They are relevant because they show that Oregon constitutional privacy law focuses on government conduct that significantly impairs freedom from scrutiny, is not controlled by formal property or contractual labels, and treats living quarters as a core zone of privacy. Those principles support treating HACC's proposed living-room sleeping arrangement as a genuine government-created privacy harm rather than a mere preference dispute."

### Overstatement to avoid

Avoid saying:

- Article I, section 9 directly forbids HACC's voucher decision.
- The denial letter itself is a search or seizure.
- The Oregon cases prove a standalone constitutional claim without further development.

Those formulations overread the cases.

## Relationship To ORS 163.467

ORS 163.467 helps in a different way. It does not convert the housing dispute into a criminal case. But it is useful because it reflects Oregon's legal judgment that a residence is a place where another person may have a reasonable expectation of privacy and that certain nonconsensual exposure there can trigger criminal liability.

The best way to connect the statute to the Article I, section 9 cases is:

- Campbell, Lien/Wilverding, and Simons show Oregon's constitutional concern with government-imposed scrutiny and exposure of private life.
- ORS 163.467 shows Oregon's positive law also treats residential privacy as serious enough to support criminal sanctions when exposure occurs under the statute's elements.
- Therefore, HACC's proposal that one adult should simply sleep in the living room is not a trivial comfort issue. It predictably heightens privacy conflict and exposure risk in a setting Oregon law treats as highly private.

## Interaction With The Existing Federal Accommodation Authorities

The Oregon Article I, section 9 authorities should not replace the current federal stack. They should be layered on top of it.

Best federal stack:

1. 24 C.F.R. § 982.316(a)
2. 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(b)(6), (8)
3. HUD Notice PIH 2014-25
4. HUD 2025 HCV Guidebook
5. Wren v. City of Cherryvale
6. Huynh v. Sanchez
7. Hardaway v. District of Columbia Housing Authority

Best way to combine them:

- Federal law answers the core accommodation question: whether the live-in-aide arrangement must work in practice.
- Oregon Article I, section 9 cases answer the privacy-value question: why living-room sleeping imposed by a government actor is not a minor inconvenience but a serious intrusion into protected privacy interests.
- ORS 163.467 adds concrete Oregon statutory context showing that residential privacy conflicts around unwanted exposure are legally consequential.

## Bottom Line

The strongest Oregon Article I, section 9 authorities for this issue are Campbell, Lien/Wilverding, and Simons. Louis and Tanner add useful home-privacy and rights-based support. The cases do not directly decide a housing-accommodation claim, but they materially strengthen the argument that HACC's proposed living-room sleeping arrangement creates a real government-imposed privacy harm under Oregon law and should not be minimized as a mere bedroom-preference dispute.
