# Memorandum Re Section 504 Accommodation for Two-Bedroom Unit With Live-In Aide

## Purpose

This memorandum is a draft for attorney review regarding a request for a two-bedroom unit as a reasonable accommodation under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act for a disabled tenant who needs a live-in aide. It is written to help counsel evaluate, refine, and potentially convert the argument into an appeal, demand letter, administrative complaint, or court filing.

## Question Presented

Whether a housing authority that receives federal financial assistance may satisfy its obligations under Section 504 by approving a live-in aide in principle while limiting the household to a one-bedroom unit, where the tenant's doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation and a one-bedroom layout would force the aide into the living room and destroy the functional privacy, sleep, caregiving, workability, and household separation needed for the arrangement to operate in practice.

## Short Answer

The stronger argument is no. Under Section 504, a federally assisted housing provider may not deny a qualified person with a disability the benefits of the program or provide a disability-related benefit in a form that is ineffective in practice. Where a disabled tenant needs a live-in aide, the regulations require approval of the aide when needed as a reasonable accommodation, and the accommodation must make the program accessible to and usable by the disabled family member. On these facts, a one-bedroom arrangement is vulnerable to challenge as an ineffective accommodation because it forces the aide to sleep in the living room, eliminates ordinary adult privacy, interferes with caregiving and remote work, and turns the mother's private bedroom into overflow space for conflicts involving visitors and the household dog. A two-bedroom unit is the minimum reasonable accommodation supported by the medical recommendation and the practical operation of the household.

## Facts For Counsel Review

The current record, as presently described, is:

- The tenant is a person with a disability whose housing is subject to Section 504 because the housing authority receives federal financial assistance.
- The tenant needs a live-in aide.
- The tenant's doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation.
- The housing authority has taken the position that a one-bedroom unit is sufficient.
- In a one-bedroom unit, the live-in aide would be forced to sleep in the living room rather than in a separate bedroom.
- HACC's denial letter expressly states that one member of the household can use the bedroom and the second member can use the living room for sleep.
- The live-in aide also works remotely and uses work equipment including a standing desk, chair, printers, and storage.
- The household includes a dog that may bark or become agitated when people knock, visit, or perform work in the unit.
- When the dog is alerting, the mother's bedroom should not have to function as the overflow containment room.
- A one-bedroom arrangement would substantially reduce ordinary adult privacy and dignity because intimate aspects of the aide's personal life would be exposed to the shared household environment.
- The mother's mobility needs, including scooter use if applicable, require safe circulation and should not be compromised by overflow sleeping or congested common areas.

These facts should be conformed to declarations, medical verification, voucher records, accommodation-request forms, hearing records, and any housing-authority correspondence before filing.

## Governing Authorities

### 1. Section 504

Section 504 provides that no otherwise qualified individual with a disability shall, solely by reason of disability, be excluded from participation in, denied the benefits of, or subjected to discrimination under a program or activity receiving federal financial assistance. 29 U.S.C. § 794(a).

### 2. HUD's Section 504 framework

HUD states that Section 504 and its implementing regulations at 24 C.F.R. parts 8 and 9 prohibit disability discrimination in federally assisted housing and require recipients to provide reasonable accommodations. HUD also explains that recipients cannot deny qualified persons with disabilities the opportunity to participate in or benefit from housing, or provide different or separate benefits unless they are equally effective, and that recipients must provide reasonable accommodations and administer programs in the most integrated setting appropriate to the person's needs.

### 3. Live-in aide regulation

HUD's voucher regulation states that a family including a disabled person may request approval of a live-in aide and that the PHA must approve a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation in accordance with 24 C.F.R. part 8 to make the program accessible to and usable by the family member with a disability. 24 C.F.R. § 982.316(a).

HUD's general definition of a live-in aide is a person who resides with a person with disabilities, is essential to that person's care and well-being, is not obligated to support the person, and would not be living in the unit except to provide necessary supportive services. 24 C.F.R. § 5.403.

### 4. HUD/DOJ guidance

The HUD/DOJ Joint Statement on Reasonable Accommodations explains that housing providers receiving federal financial assistance are also subject to Section 504, and that although Section 504 can impose greater obligations than the Fair Housing Act, the same reasonable-accommodation principles generally apply to requests for accommodations to rules, policies, practices, and services.

## Analysis

### A. The issue is not merely whether a live-in aide was nominally approved

The core Section 504 question is whether the housing program is accessible to and usable by the disabled tenant in practice. A housing authority does not satisfy that obligation simply by acknowledging that a live-in aide may exist while refusing the housing configuration necessary for the aide to function as a live-in aide. If the tenant needs a live-in aide and the only offered unit forces that aide into the living room, the housing authority is exposed to the argument that it has approved the aide in name while denying the accommodation in effective form.

### B. The doctor-supported two-bedroom request is modest and concrete

This is an important framing point for counsel. The household is not asking for an open-ended expansion of space. The requested accommodation is a two-bedroom unit, and that request is supported by medical verification. That makes the case cleaner than one in which the requested configuration is less defined or less clearly tied to disability-related need.

The strongest presentation is:

- The mother needs a live-in aide.
- The doctor supported two bedrooms.
- One bedroom forces the aide into the living room.
- Therefore one bedroom is not an effective accommodation and two bedrooms is the minimum reasonable accommodation.

### C. Privacy is part of functional use and enjoyment, not a luxury add-on

The one-bedroom arrangement is vulnerable not only because of sleep disruption, but because it destroys the ordinary privacy and dignity that allow adults to live in a home rather than in a forced common-space arrangement. If the aide must sleep and function in the living room, then common space becomes sleeping space, work space, and intimate personal space all at once. That collapse of functions exposes the household to continuous privacy loss and makes the arrangement substantially less usable.

This privacy point is especially strong where:

- the mother should have her own bedroom for rest, privacy, and disability-related use
- the aide should not be required to conduct all sleeping and private life in the living room
- the mother's bedroom should not become the substitute room for dog containment, visitor management, or overflow household conflicts

The legal inference is that privacy here is part of equal use and enjoyment and part of whether the accommodation works in reality.

There is also an Oregon-specific privacy overlay that counsel may want to evaluate. HACC's stated position that the second household member can sleep in the living room does not merely reduce comfort. It forces intimate adult life out of a bedroom and into a common area visible to another household member. That matters under ORS 163.467 because Oregon's private-indecency statute applies to exposure in a place where another person has a reasonable expectation of privacy, including a residence, when the remaining statutory elements are met. The point is not that the current record already proves a criminal violation. The point is that a state housing authority should not force a living arrangement that creates avoidable risk of exposure, accusation, or conflict under a criminal privacy statute by eliminating ordinary bedroom privacy.

The Oregon constitutional cases make the same privacy concern more concrete. Campbell states that a privacy interest under Article I, section 9 is an interest in freedom from particular forms of scrutiny and asks whether the government's conduct, if left wholly to its own discretion, will significantly impair the people's freedom from scrutiny. State v. Campbell, 306 Or 157, 170-71 (1988). Lien/Wilverding then restates that Oregon privacy is not defined by private property or contractual rights, although those rights may inform the analysis, and again ties Article I, section 9 to freedom from government scrutiny. State v. Lien/Wilverding, 364 Or 750, 760 (2019). Simons is the newest reaffirmation of that same framework and is useful because it rejects the idea that privacy necessarily disappears just because the arrangement includes terms, conditions, or reduced formal control. State v. Simons, 375 Or 70, 80-81 (2026). Those cases do not directly decide the housing-accommodation issue here, but they reinforce the broader Oregon privacy concern with government-imposed arrangements that force private life into unnecessary exposure.

### D. The dog and visitor-management facts help show why one bedroom fails in practice

The dog facts should not be framed as a separate claim to extra space for a pet. They are better used as practical proof that a one-bedroom layout does not work. If the dog barks when someone knocks, visits, or performs work in the unit, a one-bedroom layout leaves the household with no workable separation. If the dog cannot remain in the shared space, the pressure falls on the mother's bedroom. That is precisely the sort of spillover that shows the one-bedroom arrangement is not genuinely usable.

This is especially important if the mother's room would otherwise be turned into:

- the dog's temporary confinement room
- the only remaining private room in the home
- the overflow space for conflicts created by the one-bedroom layout

That is a strong practical explanation for why the second bedroom is not excess space, but the minimum separation needed to make the living arrangement function.

### E. Remote-work and equipment facts support effectiveness, not lifestyle preference

The aide's remote-work setup should be framed carefully. The best argument is not that Section 504 guarantees a home office. The stronger argument is that forcing the aide to sleep in the same living room that must also serve as common area and work area demonstrates that the one-bedroom configuration is not a workable live-in-aide arrangement. The work facts therefore reinforce the broader point that the common room cannot realistically serve as bedroom, office, private space, circulation area, and family room at the same time.

### F. Mobility and circulation considerations reinforce the need for functional separation

If the mother's mobility needs include a scooter, walker, cane, or other assistive device, a one-bedroom setup becomes even less defensible. Overflow sleeping in the living room and congestion from combined sleeping, work, and dog-management functions can interfere with circulation and safe use of the dwelling. That supports the argument that the offered arrangement is not meaningfully accessible to and usable by the disabled tenant.

### G. The best theory is ineffective or constructive denial of the accommodation

Counsel may choose among several overlapping theories, but the strongest current formulation appears to be that the housing authority has effectively denied the requested accommodation by offering a configuration that does not function in practice. In plain English, approving a live-in aide while denying the separate bedroom needed for that aide to live in the unit as an aide is approval on paper only.

Depending on the forum, this can be described as:

- denial of reasonable accommodation under Section 504
- ineffective accommodation
- constructive denial
- denial of equal use and enjoyment

## Draft Position Statement For Counsel

The tenant requests a two-bedroom unit as a reasonable accommodation under Section 504. The tenant's doctor supported that request because the tenant needs a live-in aide and the arrangement must work in practice. A one-bedroom unit is not an effective accommodation because it forces the live-in aide to sleep in the living room, destroys ordinary adult privacy, interferes with caregiving and remote work, compromises visitor and dog management, and risks turning the mother's bedroom into the overflow room for conflicts that exist only because the second bedroom has been denied. Under Section 504 and HUD's live-in-aide regulation, the question is whether the program is accessible to and usable by the disabled tenant. On these facts, the stronger argument is that it is not.

## What Counsel Should Confirm Before Filing

- The exact program involved and whether the housing authority is acting as a PHA, voucher administrator, or in another federally assisted housing role.
- The precise wording of the doctor's recommendation.
- The exact wording of the accommodation request and the authority's response.
- Whether the authority formally denied a two-bedroom voucher, denied a bedroom increase, or simply insisted on a one-bedroom unit.
- Whether there are administrative-plan provisions, occupancy standards, or subsidy-size standards that need to be addressed directly.
- Whether the dog is a pet, service animal, or assistance animal.
- Whether the mother currently uses a scooter or other mobility device and what documentation exists.
- Whether Oregon privacy theories should be pled or reserved, including the relevance of ORS 163.467 and Article I, section 9 to a state-created living-room sleeping arrangement.
- Whether state-law claims, Fair Housing Act claims, ADA Title II claims, or local administrative claims should be joined with the Section 504 claim.

## Bottom Line

For attorney-review purposes, the case can be framed cleanly: the doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation for a disabled tenant who needs a live-in aide, and the housing authority's one-bedroom position does not work in practice. The better legal theory is that the authority cannot satisfy Section 504 by recognizing the need for a live-in aide while denying the minimum bedroom configuration required for that aide arrangement to be accessible, usable, and effective.
