# Memorandum of Law

## Re: Section 504 Reasonable Accommodation Request for Two-Bedroom Unit for Disabled Tenant With Live-In Aide

**Prepared for:** Prospective Counsel  
**Prepared by:** Benjamin Barber and Jane K. Cortez Household  
**Date:** 2026-04-06

## Issue Presented

Whether a federally assisted housing authority may satisfy Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act by recognizing that a disabled tenant needs a live-in aide, while denying the minimum bedroom configuration needed for that aide arrangement to function in practice, where the tenant's doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation and the authority insists on a one-bedroom unit.

## Brief Answer

No. Section 504 prohibits a recipient of federal financial assistance from excluding a qualified person with a disability from participation in, denying that person the benefits of, or subjecting that person to discrimination under the program. 29 U.S.C. § 794(a). HUD's voucher regulation further provides that a PHA must approve a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation, in accordance with 24 C.F.R. part 8, to make the program accessible to and usable by the family member with a disability. 24 C.F.R. § 982.316(a). And HUD's subsidy-standard regulation states that an approved live-in aide must be counted in determining family unit size, while allowing exceptions justified by age, sex, health, handicap, relationship, or other personal circumstances. 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(b)(6), (8). Where a disabled tenant needs a live-in aide and the doctor supported a two-bedroom unit, a one-bedroom arrangement that forces the aide into the living room is vulnerable to challenge as an ineffective accommodation because it collapses sleeping, privacy, caregiving, work, and household management into shared space and deprives the disabled tenant of a genuinely usable dwelling arrangement.

## Facts

For purposes of attorney review, the relevant facts are as follows:

1. The tenant is a person with a disability.
2. The housing authority receives federal financial assistance and is therefore subject to Section 504.
3. The tenant needs a live-in aide.
4. The tenant's doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation.
5. The housing authority has taken the position that a one-bedroom unit is sufficient.
6. In a one-bedroom unit, the live-in aide would be required to sleep in the living room rather than in a separate bedroom.
7. The live-in aide also works remotely and uses equipment including a standing desk, desk chair, printers, and storage.
8. The household includes a dog that may bark or become agitated when people knock, visit, or work in the unit.
9. The mother's bedroom should not have to serve as the overflow room for dog separation, visitor management, or conflicts created by the lack of a second bedroom.
10. A one-bedroom layout would substantially reduce ordinary adult privacy and dignity by forcing intimate and private aspects of life into shared common space.
11. The mother's mobility needs, including scooter use if applicable, require safe circulation that should not be obstructed by overflow sleeping and combined common-room functions.

These facts should be conformed to the client's declarations, physician verification, voucher-size records, accommodation-request materials, and correspondence from the housing authority.

## Governing Law

### I. Section 504

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act provides:

"No otherwise qualified individual with a disability in the United States . . . shall, solely by reason of her or his disability, be excluded from the participation in, be denied the benefits of, or be subjected to discrimination under any program or activity receiving Federal financial assistance." 29 U.S.C. § 794(a).

### II. HUD/DOJ guidance tying housing accommodations to Section 504

The jointly issued HUD/DOJ reasonable-accommodation statement says that housing providers receiving federal financial assistance are also subject to Section 504, that Section 504 and 24 C.F.R. Part 8 prohibit disability discrimination, and that recipients of federal financial assistance must provide reasonable accommodations to applicants and residents with disabilities. The same statement also says that the Fair Housing Act reasonable-accommodation principles discussed there generally apply to requests for reasonable accommodations to rules, policies, practices, and services under Section 504.

### III. Live-in aide regulation

HUD's voucher regulation states:

"The PHA must approve a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation in accordance with 24 CFR part 8, to make the program accessible to and usable by the family member with a disability." 24 C.F.R. § 982.316(a).

HUD defines a live-in aide as a person who resides with one or more elderly persons, near-elderly persons, or persons with disabilities and who is essential to the care and well-being of the person, is not obligated for support, and would not be living in the unit except to provide necessary supportive services. 24 C.F.R. § 5.403.

### IV. Subsidy standards and family unit size

HUD's subsidy-standard regulation says that the PHA must establish subsidy standards that determine the number of bedrooms needed for families of different sizes and compositions, must provide for the smallest number of bedrooms needed to house a family without overcrowding, must count any approved live-in aide in determining family unit size, and may grant an exception justified by age, sex, health, handicap, relationship, or other personal circumstances. 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(a), (b)(1), (b)(6), (b)(8).

### V. HUD guidance on subsidy-standard exceptions for disability-related care

HUD Notice PIH 2014-25 specifically addresses reasonable-accommodation requests affecting voucher bedroom size. The notice explains that a family may request a reasonable accommodation to the PHA's subsidy standards and gives, as an example, a request for an additional bedroom to permit disability-related overnight care and allow the family equal use and enjoyment of the unit. The notice further explains that the PHA must consider such requests case by case and grant the exception as a reasonable accommodation when the exception is necessary because of the person's disability.

HUD's June 2025 Housing Choice Voucher Guidebook repeats the same principle. The Guidebook explains both that an identified live-in aide may justify an additional bedroom under the ordinary live-in-aide rules, and that a family may separately request a reasonable-accommodation exception to subsidy standards where disability-related overnight care and equal use and enjoyment require an additional bedroom. The 2025 Guidebook therefore confirms that PIH 2014-25 remains current operational guidance rather than an outdated historical notice.

### VI. Additional legal research supporting the memorandum

The directly retrieved Ninth Circuit authorities help frame the accommodation theory more precisely. The memorandum uses these FHAA cases by analogy on the accommodation question because the HUD/DOJ Joint Statement says the reasonable-accommodation principles it discusses generally apply to requests for reasonable accommodations to rules, policies, practices, and services under Section 504.

First, in United States v. California Mobile Home Park Management Co., 29 F.3d 1413 (9th Cir. 1994), the Ninth Circuit held that the FHAA imposes an affirmative duty to make reasonable accommodations and rejected the argument that generally applicable fees are categorically immune from scrutiny. The court explained that the reasonable-accommodation inquiry is highly fact-specific and turns on whether a waiver or adjustment may be necessary to afford an equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling.

Second, in Giebeler v. M & B Associates, 343 F.3d 1143 (9th Cir. 2003), the Ninth Circuit held that apartment owners could not inflexibly apply a no-cosigner policy where an adjustment was necessary to afford a disabled tenant equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling. Giebeler also explains that accommodations may require preferential treatment in some circumstances and are not excluded simply because the requested adjustment has some cost or financial dimension.

Third, in McGary v. City of Portland, 386 F.3d 1259 (9th Cir. 2004), the Ninth Circuit rejected the view that interference with the use and enjoyment of a dwelling is limited to complete denial of occupancy. McGary specifically relied on California Mobile Home and Giebeler in holding that neutral rules and financial burdens can interfere with the use and enjoyment of housing and can therefore require accommodation.

Fourth, Hardaway v. District of Columbia Housing Authority, No. 14-7144 (D.C. Cir. Dec. 16, 2016), is important because it treats live-in-aide approval and two-bedroom voucher entitlement as legally connected. The D.C. Circuit explained that when the housing authority revoked live-in-aide approval, it simultaneously extinguished the plaintiff's legal entitlement to a two-bedroom voucher because only approved live-in aides count toward unit size absent some other lawful exception.

Fifth, Wren v. City of Cherryvale, No. 22-1180-JWB (D. Kan. Aug. 30, 2022), is not binding authority here, but it is factually useful. In granting preliminary relief, the court described a disabled voucher participant whose two-bedroom arrangement allowed nighttime aides to use a second bedroom and whose transfer to a one-bedroom unit would have forced her back into a living-room sleeping arrangement. The court concluded she had shown a substantial likelihood of success on her failure-to-accommodate claim. Wren therefore provides persuasive support for the practical point that a nominal offer of housing may still be legally inadequate if it defeats the actual functioning of disability-related care.

Sixth, Huynh v. Sanchez, No. 14-cv-02367-LHK (N.D. Cal. Sept. 2, 2014), is useful regional authority. At the pleading stage, the court described a voucher participant who had medical documentation supporting his need for a separate bedroom, and allowed his FHA, ADA, and Rehabilitation Act claims to proceed after a bedroom-size reduction. Later proceedings in the same case are also significant. In 2017, the court described the settlement as requiring the Santa Clara County housing authority to stop counting a living room as a bedroom when evaluating reasonable-accommodation requests for an additional bedroom that would change subsidy size. Huynh is therefore helpful not only because it recognized a viable federal claim, but because the resulting relief directly addressed the same living-room-as-bedroom problem presented here.

These authorities do not directly decide the precise Section 504 bedroom-count issue presented here. The memorandum relies on them more narrowly: they support the practical proposition that disability-accommodation analysis in housing is functional, can require exceptions to neutral rules, and is not defeated merely because the defendant offers a nominal arrangement that fails in practice.

### VII. Adverse authority and the best distinction

The main adverse case presently identified is Binns v. City of Marietta, No. 16-14924 (11th Cir. Aug. 3, 2017), where the Eleventh Circuit held that the regulations did not automatically require a separate bedroom for a live-in aide in a two-person household. If opposing counsel relies on Binns, the best response is that Binns rejected an automatic rule, not the case-specific accommodation analysis required by 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(b)(8), PIH 2014-25, and the current HUD Guidebook. Binns also did not involve the same developed record of practical living-room dysfunction described in Wren, the same Ninth Circuit-region treatment reflected in Huynh, or the same physician-supported argument that a one-bedroom arrangement would not be usable in practice.

## Argument

### I. A housing authority does not satisfy Section 504 by approving a live-in aide on paper while denying the bedroom configuration needed for the aide arrangement to work in reality.

The central question is not whether the housing authority has nominally acknowledged that a live-in aide may be present. The question is whether the housing arrangement offered by the authority is accessible to and usable by the disabled tenant in practice. If a disabled tenant needs a live-in aide, and the authority insists on a one-bedroom unit that forces the aide into the living room, the authority has a serious Section 504 problem because the offered arrangement does not function as a real live-in-aide accommodation.

The live-in-aide regulation is framed in functional terms. It requires approval of a live-in aide when needed as a reasonable accommodation to make the program accessible to and usable by the disabled family member. 24 C.F.R. § 982.316(a). And the subsidy-standard regulation says an approved live-in aide must be counted in determining family unit size. 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(b)(6). Read together, those regulations strongly support the position that a PHA cannot treat a live-in aide as approved for access purposes while refusing to account for that aide in the bedroom-size determination. An arrangement that places the aide in the living room and eliminates the minimum separation needed for caregiving and household use is therefore vulnerable to challenge as inaccessible or unusable in practical effect.

HUD's own program guidance points the same way. PIH 2014-25 says that a family may request a reasonable-accommodation exception to subsidy standards and specifically uses an additional bedroom for disability-related overnight care as an example of the kind of request a PHA must evaluate case by case. HUD's June 2025 Housing Choice Voucher Guidebook repeats that instruction. Together, those sources materially strengthen the argument that the dispute here is not about a preference for more space, but about whether the PHA must adjust bedroom size so the accommodation actually works.

### II. The requested accommodation is modest, definite, and medically supported: two bedrooms.

This request should be presented narrowly and clearly. The household is not seeking unlimited space or a discretionary upgrade. The request is for a two-bedroom unit. That request is supported by the doctor's recommendation and directly corresponds to the practical need for a disabled tenant to have a live-in aide who can actually reside in the unit as an aide.

The cleanest formulation is simple:

1. The tenant needs a live-in aide.
2. The doctor supported a two-bedroom accommodation.
3. One bedroom forces the aide into the living room.
4. Therefore, one bedroom is not an effective accommodation, and two bedrooms is the minimum reasonable accommodation.

### III. Privacy is part of functional housing use, dignity, and equal enjoyment.

The authority's one-bedroom position is vulnerable not only because it disrupts sleep, but because it destroys the ordinary privacy and dignity necessary for adults to live in a home. If the aide must sleep in the living room, then common space becomes sleeping space, work space, circulation space, and private adult space at the same time. That collapse of functions is incompatible with normal residential use.

This point is especially important here because:

1. The mother should have her own bedroom for rest, privacy, and disability-related use.
2. The live-in aide should not be forced to conduct all sleeping and private life in the living room.
3. The mother's bedroom should not become the substitute room for every privacy or separation problem created by the denial of a second bedroom.

For Section 504 purposes, privacy in this setting is not merely aesthetic or preferential. It is part of whether the dwelling remains usable and whether the accommodation affords equal use and enjoyment in practice.

There is also a narrower Oregon privacy point that counsel may wish to evaluate. HACC's stated position that one household member can use the bedroom and the second member can use the living room for sleep does not merely reduce comfort. It forces intimate adult life and bodily privacy out of a bedroom and into a common space visible to another household member. That matters under ORS 163.467 because Oregon's private-indecency statute applies to exposure in a place where another person has a reasonable expectation of privacy, including a residence, when the remaining statutory elements are met. The point is not that the present record independently proves every element of a criminal offense. The point is that a state housing authority should not force a living arrangement that creates avoidable risk of exposure, accusation, or conflict under a criminal privacy statute by eliminating ordinary bedroom privacy.

The same facts also fit within the broader Oregon constitutional concern with government-imposed scrutiny of private life. Campbell holds that a privacy interest under Article I, section 9 is an interest in freedom from particular forms of scrutiny and asks whether the government's practice, if engaged in wholly at the discretion of the government, will significantly impair the people's freedom from scrutiny. State v. Campbell, 306 Or 157, 170-71 (1988). Lien/Wilverding then makes explicit that the privacy protected by Article I, section 9 is not defined by private property or contractual rights, although those rights may inform the analysis. State v. Lien/Wilverding, 364 Or 750, 760 (2019). Simons recently reaffirmed that same framework in a modern setting involving long-term digital monitoring and contractual terms of service. State v. Simons, 375 Or 70, 80-81 (2026). Those cases do not themselves decide a housing-accommodation dispute, but they reinforce the argument that a government housing authority's insistence on living-room sleeping for the aide has a genuine privacy dimension under Oregon law, not merely a convenience dimension.

### IV. The dog and visitor-management facts demonstrate why one bedroom fails in practice.

The dog facts should be used as practical evidence, not as a stand-alone claim to additional space for a pet. If the dog barks or becomes agitated when someone knocks, visits, or performs work in the unit, the household needs a workable way to create temporary separation. In a one-bedroom unit, that burden falls on the mother's bedroom or on the already overburdened common space. That is a concrete example of why the one-bedroom arrangement does not function.

The problem is not that the household prefers more convenience. The problem is that a one-bedroom layout leaves no workable room assignment once the household must simultaneously manage:

1. the mother's private bedroom
2. the aide's sleeping needs
3. visitors or workers entering the unit
4. temporary dog separation
5. common-space use

Those facts support the position that the second bedroom is part of the minimum separation needed to make the accommodation usable.

### V. The work-equipment and mobility facts reinforce that the common room cannot serve every function at once.

The aide's work equipment does not create a free-standing entitlement to home-office space. But it does help demonstrate why the living room cannot realistically serve as sleeping space, work space, common area, visitor area, and circulation path at the same time. Likewise, if the mother uses a scooter or other mobility device, overflow sleeping and congestion in the living room further undermine safe access and usability.

These facts reinforce the same core point: the authority's one-bedroom position is ineffective because it compresses incompatible functions into shared common space and thereby makes the live-in-aide arrangement unworkable in practice.

### VI. The strongest theory is ineffective accommodation or constructive denial.

The best legal framing is that the housing authority has effectively denied the requested accommodation by offering a configuration that does not work in practice. Put plainly, the authority cannot satisfy Section 504 by recognizing that a live-in aide is needed while denying the minimum separate bedroom arrangement required for the aide to live in the unit as an aide.

Depending on the procedural setting, counsel may describe this as:

1. denial of reasonable accommodation under Section 504
2. ineffective accommodation
3. constructive denial
4. denial of equal use and enjoyment

### VII. The saved research and preserved record both support the same practical theory.

The local record and the legal research point in the same direction. The preserved March 2026 emails show that HACC itself treated the bedroom-size issue as a reasonable-accommodation question. The physician verification form appears to request a two-bedroom apartment as part of the accommodation record. The saved litigation-research files further preserve the allegation that HACC denied the two-bedroom request and suggested Benjamin Barber could sleep in the living room instead.

Against that factual backdrop, the strongest researched legal position is not merely that the household wanted more space. It is that HACC cannot satisfy its Section 504 obligations by recognizing the possibility of a live-in aide while offering only a one-bedroom arrangement that places the aide in the living room and thereby defeats privacy, caregiving, sleep, safe circulation, and practical household operation.

## Conclusion

The strongest presentation for counsel is that the tenant requested a two-bedroom unit as a reasonable accommodation under Section 504, that the request was supported by the tenant's doctor because the tenant needs a live-in aide, and that the housing authority's one-bedroom position is not an effective accommodation in practice. A one-bedroom unit forces the aide into the living room, destroys ordinary adult privacy, interferes with caregiving and work, and creates unnecessary conflict over use of the mother's bedroom and shared space. On these facts, counsel has a substantial basis to argue that the authority cannot satisfy Section 504 by approving a live-in aide in principle while denying the minimum bedroom configuration needed to make that aide arrangement accessible, usable, and effective.
