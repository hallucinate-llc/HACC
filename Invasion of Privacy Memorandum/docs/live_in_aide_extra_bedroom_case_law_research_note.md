# Live-In Aide / Extra Bedroom Case Law Research Note

## Core Takeaways

The strongest currently retrieved authorities for the proposition that a housing authority may need to approve an additional bedroom as a disability accommodation are HUD Notice PIH 2014-25 and HUD's 2025 Housing Choice Voucher Guidebook. Both state that a family may request a reasonable-accommodation exception to subsidy standards, and that the family's circumstances may warrant an additional bedroom to permit disability-related overnight care and allow equal use and enjoyment of the unit.

The best factual court analogy currently retrieved is Wren v. City of Cherryvale, No. 22-1180-JWB (D. Kan. Aug. 30, 2022), where the court granted preliminary relief to a disabled voucher holder who needed nighttime aides and would otherwise be forced to use the living room as sleeping space for those aides.

The best entitlement-style case is Hardaway v. District of Columbia Housing Authority, No. 14-7144 (D.C. Cir. 2016), which treated rescission of live-in-aide approval as extinguishing the plaintiff's legal entitlement to a two-bedroom voucher.

The most important adverse authority currently retrieved is Binns v. City of Marietta, No. 16-14924 (11th Cir. Aug. 3, 2017), which held that federal regulations do not automatically require a separate bedroom for a live-in aide in a two-person household. Binns should be addressed if the memorandum becomes more formal or litigation-facing.

The additional research since this note began reinforces a narrower but stronger position: the best cases are not the ones announcing an automatic bedroom rule. The best materials are the ones showing that housing authorities must analyze whether the requested configuration is actually effective on the specific facts, and that HUD itself treats an extra bedroom for disability-related overnight care as a recognized form of accommodation.

## Best Authorities

### 1. HUD Notice PIH 2014-25

Source:
<https://www.hud.gov/sites/documents/14-25pihn.pdf>

Best passage:

- HUD states that a family may always request a reasonable accommodation to program rules, policies, practices, or services, including the live-in-aide policy.
- HUD further states that a family's composition or circumstances may warrant an additional bedroom to permit disability-related overnight care and allow the family equal use and enjoyment of the unit.
- HUD states that the PHA must consider such requests case by case and provide the exception where necessary as a reasonable accommodation.

Retrieved lines:

- turn6view3 lines 95-107
- turn6view4 lines 95-105

Why it matters:

This is the cleanest authority for the exact argument that the issue is not simply counting household size mechanically. HUD expressly recognizes that the accommodation analysis can justify an additional bedroom for disability-related overnight care and equal use and enjoyment.

### 2. HUD Housing Choice Voucher Guidebook (June 2025)

Source:
<https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/PIH/documents/HCV-Guidebook_Housing-Search-and-Leasing-Chapter_June-2025.pdf>

Best passages:

- The PHA must approve a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation.
- A PHA may not approve a larger unit for an unidentified live-in aide, and intermittent or rotating caregivers generally do not qualify as live-in aides.
- But the Guidebook separately states that a family may always request a reasonable-accommodation exception to the live-in-aide policy, and that a family's circumstances may warrant an additional bedroom to permit disability-related overnight care and equal use and enjoyment of the unit.
- The PHA must consider such requests case by case and provide an exception where necessary as a reasonable accommodation.

Retrieved lines:

- turn14view0 lines 674-688
- turn14view0 lines 702-713

Why it matters:

This confirms that HUD still presents PIH 2014-25 as current operational guidance in 2025. It also helps distinguish true live-in aides from occasional or rotating caregivers.

### 3. Wren v. City of Cherryvale, No. 22-1180-JWB (D. Kan. Aug. 30, 2022)

Source:
<https://ecf.ksd.uscourts.gov/cgi-bin/show_public_doc?2022cv1180-15=>

Best passages:

- Plaintiff had previously slept in the living room of a one-bedroom apartment.
- The court found that if nighttime aides were required to sleep in the living room, that would impair the functionality of the living room for plaintiff's enjoyment.
- The court found the accommodation reasonable because otherwise plaintiff would be required to sacrifice living-room space for aides' sleeping area and might lose one of her nighttime aides.

Retrieved lines:

- turn6view6 lines 48-51
- turn6view6 lines 145-149
- turn6view6 lines 184-188
- turn6view7 lines 120-123

Why it matters:

This is the closest factual analog currently retrieved to the argument that a one-bedroom/living-room arrangement is not an effective accommodation in practice.

### 4. Hardaway v. District of Columbia Housing Authority, No. 14-7144 (D.C. Cir. 2016)

Source:
<https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/cadc/14-7144/14-7144-2016-12-16.html>

Best passages:

- By revoking live-in-aide approval, the authority extinguished the plaintiff's legal entitlement to a two-bedroom voucher.
- A two-bedroom voucher retained after denial of live-in-aide approval was described as only an act of administrative grace.

Retrieved lines:

- turn6view8 lines 78-93
- turn3view2 lines 90-93

Why it matters:

This is strong authority for the proposition that live-in-aide approval and the two-bedroom entitlement are legally linked.

### 5. Huynh v. Sanchez, No. 14-cv-02367-LHK (N.D. Cal. Sept. 2, 2014)

Source:
<https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-cand-5_14-cv-02367/pdf/USCOURTS-cand-5_14-cv-02367-0.pdf>

Best passages:

- The hearing officer determined that Huynh was a person with a disability and needed a separate bedroom to accommodate his disability.
- The court allowed the case to proceed at the pleading stage after a voucher reduction from three bedrooms to two bedrooms despite documented disability-related need.

Retrieved lines:

- turn10view0 lines 245-249
- turn10view0 lines 232-251

Why it matters:

Huynh is not a final merits win on the bedroom issue, but it is useful Ninth Circuit-region authority showing that documented disability-related need for an additional bedroom can support FHA, RA, and ADA claims.

### 6. Burton v. Darby, No. 2:23-cv-03034 (E.D. Cal. Aug. 9, 2024)

Source:
<https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/california/caedce/2%3A2023cv03034/439396/21/>

Best passages:

- The court reasoned that denial of access to housing that meets a disabled plaintiff's needs can be construed as denial of Section 8 benefits.
- The court cited Huynh for the proposition that denial of expanded vouchers for additional bedrooms as disability accommodations can violate the ADA and Rehabilitation Act.

Retrieved lines:

- turn8view0 lines 108-116

Why it matters:

This is only a magistrate judge findings-and-recommendations document, so it is weaker than Wren or Hardaway. But it helps on standing and injury framing.

### 7. Matter of Washington v. New York City Department of Housing Preservation & Development, 2016 N.Y. Slip Op. 51139(U) (Sup. Ct. N.Y. Cnty. 2016)

Source:
<https://law.justia.com/cases/new-york/other-courts/2016/2016-ny-slip-op-51139-u.html>

Best passages:

- The petitioner argued that reducing the household from three bedrooms to two would require him and his wife to sleep in the living room.
- The request was tied to the wife's disability and to the physical impossibility or pain associated with using the living room as sleeping space.
- The decision recognizes the framing of a bedroom-size dispute as a disability-accommodation question, even though the petitioner ultimately lost.

Retrieved lines:

- turn3view1 lines 89-100

Why it matters:

Washington is not a win for the tenant, so it is not core affirmative authority. But it is still useful because it shows courts understand that a forced living-room sleeping arrangement can be part of a disability-accommodation argument about voucher or unit size.

## Mixed Or Adverse Authorities

### 8. Thorson v. Hawaii Public Housing Authority, No. 1:23-cv-00412 (D. Haw. 2025)

Sources:

- <https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-hid-1_23-cv-00412/pdf/USCOURTS-hid-1_23-cv-00412-4.pdf>
- <https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/hawaii/hidce/1%3A2023cv00412/167088/129/>

Useful parts:

- The court expressly states that HUD authorizes housing vouchers with an additional bedroom to allow a live-in aide to reside in the unit and provide required support services.
- The court notes that the agency had approved a two-bedroom voucher to allow for a live-in aide.
- The court also notes that an unreasonable delay can in some circumstances amount to constructive denial.

Retrieved lines:

- turn13view0 lines 61-64
- turn13view0 lines 164-169
- turn1view10 lines 706-713

Caution:

Thorson is not a clean plaintiff-side case for our facts. The court ultimately held there was no denial on the record before it because the plaintiff had been approved for the two-bedroom/live-in-aide accommodation and later asked for only intermittent or non-live-in support.

### 9. Binns v. City of Marietta, No. 16-14924 (11th Cir. 2017)

Source:
<https://media.ca11.uscourts.gov/opinions/unpub/files/201614924.pdf>

Best adverse passages:

- The court held that the applicable regulations do not require the PHA to provide a separate bedroom for a live-in aide.
- The court emphasized that the regulations require at least one bedroom or living/sleeping room for each two persons and concluded that a one-bedroom subsidy for a two-person household was not an unreasonable accommodation denial.

Retrieved lines:

- turn4view0 lines 87-119

Why it matters:

This is the clearest adverse appellate authority presently retrieved. It should be confronted directly in any litigation-quality memo.

Possible distinctions:

- Binns is Eleventh Circuit, not Ninth Circuit.
- Binns reasoned primarily from general occupancy regulations, not the later-emphasized HUD guidance about disability-related overnight care and subsidy-standard exceptions.
- Binns did not involve the same practical living-room functionality findings present in Wren.
- Binns should be framed as rejecting an automatic rule, not foreclosing all case-specific reasonable-accommodation exceptions under 24 C.F.R. § 982.402(b)(8) and PIH 2014-25.

## Additional Guidance And Administrative Materials

### 10. HUD Housing Choice Voucher Guidebook (2020 and 2025 versions)

Sources:

- <https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/PIH/documents/HCV_Guidebook_Housing_Search_and_Leasing_November%202020.pdf>
- <https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/PIH/documents/HCV-Guidebook_Housing-Search-and-Leasing-Chapter_June-2025.pdf>

Best passages:

- HUD states that the PHA may approve only one additional bedroom for a live-in aide.
- HUD also separately states that a family may request a reasonable-accommodation exception to subsidy standards where disability-related overnight care and equal use and enjoyment require an additional bedroom.

Retrieved lines:

- turn6search12 summary snippet
- turn6search8 summary snippet
- turn14view0 lines 674-688
- turn14view0 lines 702-713

Why it matters:

These Guidebooks are not judicial authority, but they are important because they show HUD continues to treat both propositions as true at the same time: there is no free-floating right to a larger unit for an unidentified aide, but there is a recognized path for an additional bedroom when disability-related overnight care makes it necessary.

### 11. HUD OIG Audit Materials On Extra Bedrooms

Sources:

- <https://archives.hud.gov/offices/oig/reports/internal/ig0760001.pdf>
- <https://archives.hud.gov/offices/oig/reports/files/ig671008.pdf>

Best passages:

- HUD permits an additional bedroom when the family shows it needs the room for a live-in aide or another disability-related reason.
- HUD criticized agencies that approved additional bedrooms for only occasional caregivers or on vague medical support.
- HUD also emphasized that there must be a defined and reasonable nexus between the disability and the larger voucher.

Retrieved lines:

- turn5view0 lines 272-307
- turn5view1 lines 85-107

Why it matters:

These audit materials are not plaintiff-side merits authority, but they are still useful for strategy. They suggest that the strongest presentation is one with a clearly identified live-in aide or clearly described overnight care function, concrete medical support, and a precise explanation of why the added bedroom is necessary rather than merely beneficial.

### 12. Housing Authority Of Baltimore City Reasonable Accommodation Policy

Source:
<https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/crt/legacy/2010/12/14/bhasettle2_attach.pdf>

Best passages:

- The policy states that requests for reasonable accommodations are considered case by case.
- It also states that a live-in aide would not be required to share a bedroom with another member of the household.

Retrieved lines:

- turn1view1 lines 1503-1529

Why it matters:

This is not binding law. But it is helpful as a practical program example showing that at least some housing-authority policies expressly treat a live-in aide as someone who should not be forced to share a bedroom. That supports the broader argument that the requested second bedroom is not outside ordinary housing-administration practice.

## Working Litigation Position After Research

The best researched formulation at this point is:

1. Federal law does not create an automatic entitlement to a separate bedroom for every live-in aide in every two-person household.
2. But HUD expressly authorizes case-by-case reasonable-accommodation exceptions to subsidy standards, including an additional bedroom for disability-related overnight care and equal use and enjoyment.
3. The strongest plaintiff-side argument is therefore not that every approved aide automatically gets a bedroom, but that on these specific facts a one-bedroom/living-room arrangement is not an effective accommodation in practice.
4. Wren and Hardaway are the best currently retrieved cases for the practical and entitlement sides of that argument.
5. Huynh strengthens the argument in the Ninth Circuit region that documented disability-related need for a separate bedroom can support FHA, ADA, and Rehabilitation Act claims.
6. Binns is the principal case that must be distinguished.
7. The HUD audit materials suggest that the record should stay disciplined: identified aide or clearly defined overnight care, concrete medical nexus, and a precise explanation of why one bedroom fails in practice.
