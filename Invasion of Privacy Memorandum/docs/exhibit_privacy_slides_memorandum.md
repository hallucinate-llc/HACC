## Short Intro Paragraph For The Memorandum

Exhibit A is a demonstrative slide deck showing why privacy is a central part of the requested accommodation. The exhibit illustrates that a one-bedroom layout would force the live-in aide into the living room, collapse sleeping space and common space into the same area, interfere with ordinary adult privacy and dignity, and create practical conflicts involving visitors, the household dog, and the mother's private bedroom. The exhibit is offered to show why a one-bedroom unit is not an effective accommodation in practice and why a two-bedroom unit is the minimum reasonable accommodation.

## Suggested Memorandum Reference

See Exhibit A, demonstrative slides showing that a one-bedroom layout eliminates ordinary privacy, forces household conflict into shared space, and makes the requested live-in aide arrangement ineffective in practice.

## Page-By-Page Explanation

### Page 1

Page 1 visually compares one-bedroom, two-bedroom, and three-bedroom layouts. For memorandum purposes, the key point is that the one-bedroom configuration produces conflict because everyone is forced together, while the two-bedroom configuration provides the minimum separation necessary for the mother and live-in aide to have distinct sleeping space. The page supports the argument that privacy is not a luxury here. It is part of what makes the accommodation functional.

### Page 2

Page 2 shows that when someone knocks at the door and the dog begins barking, the household needs a way to move the dog into a separate room. This supports the privacy argument because it shows that the mother's room should not become the overflow space for every household conflict created by a one-bedroom layout.

### Page 3

Page 3 shows the problem with forcing the dog into the disabled mother's room. The slide demonstrates that doing so can create agitation and possible safety risk around the mother's mobility scooter. This is relevant to privacy because the mother's bedroom should remain her private and safe space, not a substitute containment area made necessary by the lack of a second bedroom.

### Page 4

Page 4 shows the visitor problem in a one-bedroom setup. If the dog cannot go into the mother's room and the aide is sleeping or living in the common area, the household may have no workable way to separate the dog from a visitor. This supports the argument that the lack of a second bedroom destroys ordinary household privacy and functional separation.

### Page 5

Page 5 addresses the loss of privacy when the aide must sleep or work in the living room. For memorandum purposes, the strongest reading of this slide is that a one-bedroom layout destroys ordinary adult privacy, dignity, and autonomy by exposing intimate personal life to the shared household environment. The memorandum should rely on that privacy and dignity point, rather than on any broader or more aggressive characterization.

## Clean Privacy Framing To Use With The Exhibit

The point of Exhibit A is not that the household seeks extra space for convenience. The point is that a one-bedroom layout removes the basic separation needed for adult privacy, safe caregiving, visitor management, work, and the mother's private use of her own room. Privacy matters here because the accommodation must work in reality, and a one-bedroom unit forces intimate life, dog management, and household disruption into spaces that should remain private and functional.

## Short Closing Paragraph

Exhibit A therefore supports the conclusion that privacy is not incidental to the accommodation request. Privacy is one of the concrete functional interests that a two-bedroom unit protects and a one-bedroom unit destroys.
