# Section 504 Review Notes

This note reviews how to present the current facts under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act in a stronger and cleaner way.

## Core framing

The strongest Section 504 argument is not that the household would merely prefer more space. It is that the dwelling must be usable in practice by a disabled tenant and the live-in aide arrangement must function in reality, not just on paper. Here, the requested accommodation is a two-bedroom unit, which is supported by the doctor and is more reasonable than the housing authority's one-bedroom position. The request should therefore be framed around:

- safe and effective caregiving
- private sleeping space for separate adults
- disability-related mobility and circulation needs
- the ability to manage the household without forcing one person into the other's private room
- equal use and enjoyment of the dwelling

## Review of the current points

### A. Dog barks at knocks, visitors, and workers, so the dog may need to be placed in an enclosed room

This point is helpful, but it is stronger if tied to why a one-bedroom unit does not function rather than treated as a free-standing right to extra space for a pet.

Suggested framing:

"Because the household includes a dog that may bark or become alert when visitors arrive or work is performed in the unit, a one-bedroom layout does not provide enough separation to manage the dog without disrupting caregiving, sleep, work, or private family activities. A two-bedroom layout is a more workable accommodation."

Important caution:

If the dog is a service animal or assistance animal, say that clearly. That makes the point stronger. If the dog is only a pet, this point is still useful as part of practical layout reality, but it is weaker as a Section 504 point standing alone.

### B. When the dog is agitated, the dog should not have to be put into the room with your mother

This is a stronger version of Point A because it ties the space issue to your mother's disability-related privacy and quiet.

Suggested framing:

"When the dog is agitated or alerting, the dog should not have to be confined in my mother's room. My mother needs her own bedroom for rest, privacy, and disability-related use. In a one-bedroom unit, that leaves no separate private sleeping space for the live-in aide and no workable way to manage ordinary household activity. A two-bedroom unit avoids forcing those conflicts into my mother's room."

### C. Privacy with your girlfriend and not wanting your mother to witness sexual activity

This should be framed carefully and more cleanly. The likely intended sentence is that you do not want your mother seeing you have sex with your girlfriend.

This is not just a preference argument. It should be framed as adult privacy, dignity, and autonomy within the home.

Suggested framing:

"Separate adult sleeping rooms are necessary to preserve basic privacy, dignity, and autonomy in the home. If I am forced to sleep in a common area because the unit has only one bedroom, my mother is exposed to intimate aspects of my personal life, including my relationship with my girlfriend, and I am denied ordinary adult privacy within the dwelling. A two-bedroom unit is the reasonable accommodation that prevents that intrusion."

This is much stronger than phrasing it as simply wanting more room for sex.

### D. Standing desk, desk chair, printers, and filing cabinets

This is useful if the point is that the live-in aide also works remotely and cannot realistically sleep in the same space that must function as a full work area.

Suggested framing:

"I work remotely and need a functional workspace that includes a standing desk, chair, printers, and file storage. A one-bedroom layout that requires me to sleep in the living room collapses sleeping space, common space, and work space into the same area, making the arrangement ineffective. A two-bedroom unit is a more reasonable and workable accommodation."

This point is strongest when used to show why living-room sleeping is not a real accommodation. It is weaker if presented as a general entitlement to office space divorced from the live-in aide arrangement.

### E. Mobility scooter for your mother

This is a strong Section 504 point if the scooter is medically necessary or actually used.

Suggested framing:

"My mother's mobility needs require sufficient interior space for a mobility scooter, including storage, charging, and clear paths of travel. A one-bedroom layout that forces overflow sleeping into shared areas or blocks circulation interferes with her safe and equal use of the dwelling. A two-bedroom unit better accommodates those disability-related needs."

### F. Dog bed

This is usually not a stand-alone legal point. It is better treated as part of the overall space reality.

Suggested framing:

"The household also needs space for the dog's bed and safe placement during ordinary daily use of the home."

### G. Bed for your mother

This is essential. It should be stated directly.

Suggested framing:

"My mother requires a private bedroom with her own bed."

### H. Bed for you

This is also essential if you are the live-in aide or necessary household caregiver.

Suggested framing:

"As the live-in aide, I also require my own actual sleeping space with a bed, rather than a temporary arrangement in the living room."

## Best combined argument

The cleanest overall formulation is:

"Under Section 504, the accommodation must afford my mother an equal opportunity to use and enjoy the dwelling in a way that works in practice. My doctor has supported a two-bedroom accommodation. That two-bedroom layout is necessary so my mother can have her own bedroom and I, as the live-in aide, can have my own bedroom, rather than being forced into the living room. A one-bedroom unit is not an effective accommodation because it collapses sleeping, caregiving, privacy, work, and household management into the same shared space."

## Minimum number of bedrooms

On the facts you listed and the request you actually made, the most defensible position is:

- Two bedrooms are the reasonable accommodation you requested.
- Two bedrooms are supported by your doctor.
- One bedroom is inadequate because it forces the live-in aide into the living room and destroys the functional separation needed for sleep, privacy, work, caregiving, and ordinary household management.

So the clearest bottom-line position is:

"A two-bedroom unit is the minimum reasonable accommodation. A one-bedroom unit is not an effective accommodation."

## Recommended wording to avoid

Avoid saying:

- "I want more space."
- "I need a room for sex."
- "The dog needs its own bedroom."

Those versions sound discretionary.

Prefer saying:

- "The accommodation must function in practice."
- "Separate enclosed space is necessary to avoid forcing activity into my mother's room or the shared living area."
- "The current layout denies privacy, caregiving effectiveness, sleep, workability, and disability-related circulation."

## Bottom line

Your strongest Section 504 theory is that a one-bedroom or living-room-sleeping arrangement is not an effective accommodation because it forces sleeping, caregiving, work, privacy, and dog-management conflicts into the same shared space. The cleanest position is to keep asking for the two-bedroom unit your doctor supported and to use the dog, privacy, work, and mobility facts to show why the housing authority's one-bedroom proposal fails in practice.

## Complaint-style paragraph

"Under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, the housing authority must provide a reasonable accommodation that affords my mother an equal opportunity to use and enjoy the dwelling. My mother's doctor supported a two-bedroom unit because she needs a live-in aide and the arrangement must function in practice. The housing authority's one-bedroom position is not an effective accommodation because it forces the live-in aide to sleep in the living room, destroys ordinary adult privacy, interferes with caregiving and remote work, complicates management of the household dog during visitors or workers, and undermines safe circulation for my mother's mobility needs. Approving a live-in aide while insisting on a one-bedroom layout denies the accommodation in practical effect."

## Short demand paragraph

"I am requesting a two-bedroom unit as a reasonable accommodation under Section 504. That request is supported by my mother's doctor. A one-bedroom unit is not an effective accommodation because it would force the live-in aide into the living room and would not provide the functional separation needed for sleep, caregiving, privacy, work, and household management. Please approve the two-bedroom accommodation."

## Hearing-style paragraph

"This case is not about a preference for extra space. It is about whether the accommodation works in reality. My mother's doctor supported a two-bedroom unit. If the housing authority limits us to one bedroom, then the live-in aide is pushed into the living room, and the arrangement stops functioning as a real accommodation. That one-bedroom setup interferes with caregiving, privacy, work, dog management, and my mother's disability-related use of the home. A two-bedroom unit is the minimum reasonable accommodation."
