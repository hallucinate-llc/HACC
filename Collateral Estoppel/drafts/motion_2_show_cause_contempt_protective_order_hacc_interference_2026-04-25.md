### III. Legal Standard

ORS 124.012 provides that contempt proceedings for violation of an EPPDAPA restraining order must be conducted by the issuing court or the circuit court for a county where a violation occurs.

ORS 33.015(2)(b) defines contempt to include willful disobedience of, resistance to, or obstruction of a court order. ORS 33.055 provides the procedure for remedial contempt.

ORS 33.015 also treats refusal to produce a record, document, or other object contrary to an order of the court as contempt.

ORS 33.105 authorizes remedial sanctions including compensatory money sanctions, coercive per-day sanctions for continuing contempt, orders designed to ensure compliance with a prior court order, attorney fees incurred because of the contempt, and other effective remedial sanctions.

ORS 40.135 / OEC 311 provides that evidence willfully suppressed would be adverse to the party suppressing it. The requested adverse inference is especially appropriate where the missing records are within Solomon's control, directly concern the ordered production, and go to the core factual question whether he used collateral housing channels to evade the protective order.

Oregon cases state the essential contempt elements as a valid court order, knowledge of the order, and voluntary noncompliance. For purposes of ORS 33.015, willfulness means intentional action with knowledge that the act or omission was forbidden.

### IV. Argument

The protective order was not optional. If Solomon believed service, scope, or enforcement issues existed, the lawful path was a hearing, modification, clarification, or appeal. The preserved Google Voice messages show that Solomon instead treated compliance as conditional on his own service theory.

The housing interference allegations are serious because the order protected Jane from Solomon's control and interference, and because HACC's lease and household decisions directly affected Jane and Benjamin's housing security. If Solomon used HACC, claimed authority documents, purported power-of-attorney powers, or other third-party channels to affect Jane's housing or remove Benjamin from the household, that conduct functionally evaded the Court's protective framework.

The lease-removal record is presently stronger as a show-cause and compelled-accounting record than as a final contempt finding. The Court does not need to decide at the show-cause stage that Solomon definitively caused the removal. The Court can require him to identify his HACC communications, produce all claimed authority documents, and explain why his conduct did not violate or obstruct the order.

The elimination-based inference is substantial. Benjamin did not ask to be removed. Benjamin's declaration states that, to his knowledge, Jane did not ask for his removal. Jane can confirm that point by supplemental declaration. Julio was later himself removed from tenancy and, according to Benjamin's declaration, could not read or write in any language. Meanwhile, HACC linked the removal to outside document flows from Benjamin's brother and stepfather, and Solomon later made detailed housing allegations in a collateral probate petition. Those facts support an order requiring Solomon to answer under oath.

At minimum, the Court should order Solomon to appear, preserve and produce all HACC and claimed-authority records, identify every third-party housing communication, and obey a clear noninterference directive barring further collateral housing contact concerning Jane or Benjamin except through authorized court process.

8. Coercive daily sanction: If the Court finds continuing contempt, Solomon should be assessed a daily coercive sanction under ORS 33.105 until he produces the required records and sworn accounting.

11. Record-completion relief: Solomon should be ordered to sign any releases or authorizations needed to obtain HACC, phone, email, cloud, or messaging records from third parties, or alternatively to identify the custodians so Petitioner can subpoena them.
12. Further contempt review: If Solomon still refuses after the Court's deadline, the Court should set a continued compliance hearing and consider stronger coercive sanctions authorized by ORS 33.105.
