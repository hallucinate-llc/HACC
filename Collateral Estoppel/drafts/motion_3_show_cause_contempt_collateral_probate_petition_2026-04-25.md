### III. Legal Standard

ORS 33.015(2)(b) defines contempt to include willful disobedience of, resistance to, or obstruction of the court's authority, process, orders, or judgments.

ORS 33.055 allows an aggrieved party to initiate remedial contempt and requires supporting documentation or affidavits sufficient to give notice of the specific acts alleged. ORS 33.105 authorizes remedial sanctions designed to ensure compliance and remedy contempt.

ORS 124.012 provides that contempt proceedings for violation of an EPPDAPA restraining order must be conducted by the issuing court or by the circuit court for a county where a violation occurs.

Oregon cases state that contempt requires a valid court order, knowledge of the order, and voluntary noncompliance. Remedial contempt requires a specific finding of willfulness.

ORS 162.065 provides the criminal perjury framework for a false sworn statement or false unsworn declaration concerning a material issue, made with knowledge of falsity. ORS 162.085 separately addresses unsworn falsification. Those statutes are not the direct sanctions vehicle requested in this motion, but they show why the petition's penalty-of-perjury declaration and material factual allegations matter.

ORCP 17 sanctions for the probate filing itself belong in probate case `26PR00641`. This motion uses the petition differently: as evidence that Solomon sought to bypass or undermine the `25PO11530` restraining order through a collateral filing.

### IV. Argument

The restraining order should not be defeated through a collateral probate filing. If Solomon believed the restraining order was invalid, overbroad, or factually wrong, the lawful course was to seek relief from the issuing court. Instead, he filed or pursued a probate petition that sought emergency control over Jane while omitting the restraining-order context and the record-production dispute.

The petition did not merely omit minor background facts. It omitted facts central to the relief requested: Jane's restraining-order posture against Solomon, the HACC/Quantum housing timeline, the outside-document-flow evidence, Benjamin's actual household history, and Solomon's own alleged role in the housing and authority-document disputes.

The petition also affirmatively alleged facts that are now candidates for sanctions review: that Benjamin was a recent household arrival, that Benjamin caused Jane's voucher failure, that Benjamin isolated Jane from APS and police, that no less restrictive alternative existed, and that Solomon was a suitable emergency guardian despite the restraining order. Those allegations were material because they supported waiver of notice, emergency guardianship, plenary authority, fees from Jane's funds, and a writ of assistance.

The Court should require Solomon to identify the factual source for each disputed allegation. If Solomon cannot identify competent sources, or if the evidence shows he knew the allegations were false or materially incomplete, the Court should find that the collateral probate petition was part of a willful effort to evade this Court's protective order.

This motion does not ask the Court in `25PO11530` to impose ORCP 17 sanctions for a probate pleading. Any ORCP 17 sanctions request belongs in `26PR00641`. This motion asks the restraining-order court to determine whether the collateral probate filing was part of a willful effort to resist, obstruct, or evade the order entered in this case.

### V. Probate Petition Allegation Matrix

Petitioner relies on the separately prepared petition allegation matrix, which identifies each petition allegation, its petition source, why it was material, the present contradiction or omission, and the proof still needed.

The highest-priority allegations for hearing are:

1. `Estate under $10,000` while also alleging a substantial inheritance / special-needs trust.
2. `No trustee / no attorney-in-fact / no less restrictive alternative` while Solomon withheld trust, inheritance, HACC, or claimed-authority records.
3. Benjamin `was incarcerated until recently` and `has begun living with` Jane.
4. Benjamin failed to provide housing documentation and caused Jane's voucher failure.
5. Benjamin interfered with APS and police while the petition omitted Jane's same-period restraining-order posture against Solomon.
6. Jane was isolated and in immediate danger because of Benjamin.
7. Solomon was willing and able to serve as guardian despite the existing restraining order and his refusal to produce records.
8. Waiver of notice, plenary authority, fees from Jane's funds, and a writ of assistance were necessary.

Petitioner further alleges that the housing-related probate allegations omitted federal relocation protections. If the HACC displacement path was a public-housing demolition or disposition relocation, 24 CFR 970.21 required 90-day notice before displacement and comparable housing. That regulation also provides that tenant-based assistance such as a Housing Choice Voucher is not treated as comparable housing until the family is actually relocated into such housing. To the extent a separate federally assisted relocation regime applies, 49 CFR 24.203 separately provides that a lawful occupant may not be required to move without at least 90 days advance written notice and cannot be required to move unless a comparable replacement dwelling has been made available.
