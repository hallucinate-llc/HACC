2. The order restrained Solomon Samuel Barber from abuse, intimidation, contact/interference, certain property-control conduct, and control or transfer of Jane Kay Cortez's money or property.
3. The order also required Solomon Samuel Barber to follow guardian or conservator instructions and required return or production of records by December 12, 2025.
4. The records required by the order included legal documents allegedly transferring interests in real property or other estate property, records relating to the estate, and records mentioning Jane Kay Cortez.
5. Solomon Samuel Barber did not produce the required records by December 12, 2025.

7. Solomon Samuel Barber had actual notice of the restraining-order framework. Preserved Google Voice messages show that he knew about the order and later took the position that he would comply only after service occurred on his terms.

15. On April 20, 2026, Benjamin Barber again asked whether Solomon and counsel would comply with the court order and provide the requested information or wait for a subpoena.

### III. Legal Standard

Under ORS 33.015(2)(b), contempt includes willful disobedience of, resistance to, or obstruction of a court's orders or judgments. Under ORS 33.015(2)(d), contempt separately includes refusal to produce a record, document, or object contrary to a court order.

ORS 33.055 allows an aggrieved party to initiate remedial contempt by filing supporting documentation or affidavits sufficient to give notice of the specific acts alleged. Inability to comply is an affirmative defense. For remedial sanctions other than confinement, contempt must be proved by clear and convincing evidence.

ORS 33.105 authorizes remedial sanctions, including compliance orders, compensatory relief, daily coercive fines within statutory limits, attorney fees caused by contempt, and any other effective remedy.

ORS 40.135 / OEC 311 supports a presumption that evidence willfully suppressed would be adverse to the party suppressing it. ORS 40.120 / OEC 308 provides that, in civil proceedings, a presumption imposes on the party against whom it is directed the burden of proving that the nonexistence of the presumed fact is more probable than its existence.

### IV. Argument

The order was clear enough to require production. Solomon Samuel Barber had notice. The production deadline passed months ago. The later emails with counsel show that the nonproduction was identified again in April 2026 and that Solomon was asked to comply. No production or sworn accounting has been provided.

This is remedial contempt because the requested relief is designed to compel compliance, account for missing records, repair prejudice caused by nonproduction, and prevent Solomon from benefiting from papers he was ordered to disclose but withheld.

The requested adverse presumption is also remedial. Petitioner alleges that Solomon abused Jane Cortez, interfered with Jane's property and estate interests, and used or claimed authority over Jane's affairs without producing the records that would reveal what he did, what Jane signed, what authority he claimed, and what communications he had with third parties. If Solomon refuses to produce those records after a court order, he should not be allowed to benefit from the evidentiary gap he created. That gap should be treated as evidence suppression or spoliation unless Solomon proves an innocent explanation.

The Court should therefore presume, unless Solomon proves otherwise, that the withheld records would be unfavorable to Solomon on the disputed issues of abuse, property control, estate or trust interference, claimed power-of-attorney authority, HACC communications, and Jane Cortez's knowledge or consent.

If Solomon contends that records do not exist, are no longer in his possession, or cannot be produced, he should be required to say so under oath, identify what happened to them, and identify every person or entity with whom he communicated about those records.

3. An adverse inference that the records would show Solomon claimed, used, or attempted to use authority over Jane Cortez's estate, trust, inheritance, housing, or property affairs without lawful basis or without Jane's informed consent;

5. An order barring Solomon from relying on any document, communication, claimed authority, or factual assertion responsive to the restraining order that he failed to produce; and
6. Any additional remedial sanction necessary to prevent Solomon from using nonproduction as a litigation advantage.
