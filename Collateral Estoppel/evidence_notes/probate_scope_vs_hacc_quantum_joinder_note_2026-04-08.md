# Probate Scope vs. HACC / Quantum Joinder Note - 2026-04-08

This note states the current best procedural posture for Housing Authority of Clackamas County (`HACC`) and Quantum Residential in the probate matter `In the Matter of Jane Kay Cortez`, Case No. `26PR00641`.

## Bottom line

The stronger probate position is not a full joinder or third-party-liability motion against HACC or Quantum.

The stronger position is:

1. ask the probate court to limit the case to guardianship and protective-proceeding issues;
2. require Section 18 relocation-duty, eviction-merits, and housing-liability issues to be litigated in the proper eviction or federal forum; and
3. invoke limited `ORCP 29` joinder only as a fallback if the probate court insists on reaching housing merits anyway.

## Why ORCP 22C is weak here

`ORCP 22C` third-party practice is derivative-liability focused. It fits best when the absent party may be liable over to the defending party for all or part of the plaintiff's claim.

On the present record:

1. Solomon's probate petition seeks protective / guardianship-related relief;
2. HACC and Quantum are alleged primary actors in separate housing disputes;
3. HACC and Quantum are not natural derivative-liability parties for Solomon's probate claim.

So `ORCP 22C` is not the best fit for this probate posture.

## Why ORCP 29 is only a fallback

`ORCP 29A` can support joinder where:

1. complete relief cannot be accorded among existing parties without the absent person; or
2. proceeding without the absent person creates a serious risk of inconsistent obligations or impaired interests.

That does not strongly support full joinder here unless the probate court first decides it must adjudicate housing-merits questions.

If the probate court stays within its proper lane:

1. it can decide guardianship-related questions without trying Section 18 compliance;
2. HACC and Quantum remain nonparty actors whose conduct may be relevant background but not central merits defendants.

## Probate authority that supports scope control

The current best source path is:

1. `ORS 125.050`: ORCP applies in protective proceedings unless Chapter 125 provides otherwise.
2. `ORS 125.025`: the court has continuing authority to manage protective proceedings and act by petition or motion.
3. `ORS 125.065`, `125.075`, and `125.080`: notice, objections, and hearing rules govern probate motions.

This supports a probate motion that asks the court to:

1. define the scope of the proceeding;
2. avoid relitigation of non-probate housing merits;
3. reserve those merits to the proper eviction or federal tracks.
