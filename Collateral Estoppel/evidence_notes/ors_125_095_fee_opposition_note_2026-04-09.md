## Official statutory backdrop

The current Oregon statutes support two important points:

1. `ORS 125.095` permits the court to approve payment of reasonable fees from funds of a person subject to a protective proceeding.
2. `ORS 125.098` governs whether attorney fees should be awarded, and the factor given the greatest weight is the benefit to the person subject to the protective proceeding by the party's actions in the proceeding.

Official sources:

- Oregon Legislature, Chapter 125: https://www.oregonlegislature.gov/bills_laws/ors/ors125.html
- `ORS 125.095`: funds of a person subject to a protective proceeding may be used to pay reasonable fees related to the proceeding.
- `ORS 125.098(2)(a)`: the benefit to the person subject to the protective proceeding is the factor that must receive the greatest weight.
- `ORS 125.098(2)(b)-(e)`: the court also considers the objective reasonableness of the party's position, the party's self-interest, whether relief was granted, and the party's conduct in the transactions or occurrences that gave rise to the protective proceeding.

## Best current opposition posture

The strongest current argument is not that `ORS 125.095` can never apply. The stronger argument is that this Court should deny any fee request on the present record because the statutory factors weigh against shifting Solomon Barber's fees to Jane Kay Cortez.

### 1. Benefit to Jane Kay Cortez is not presently shown

The present record does not cleanly show that Solomon Barber's requested relief has benefited Jane Kay Cortez. To the contrary, the staged record presently includes:

- coercive writ-based transport or forced-removal process;
- a disputed waiver-of-notice emergency narrative;
- requests for plenary authority and possible facility placement;
- materially disputed housing-causation allegations; and
- Jane Kay Cortez's own declaration describing police pickup, lack of approval, and unresolved housing delay.

On this record, the Court should not conclude that Solomon Barber's actions in the proceeding have benefited Jane Kay Cortez in the way `ORS 125.098` requires the Court to prioritize.

### 2. The petition is materially disputed and partially overbroad

The petition seeks:

- plenary authority;
- possible placement in a facility or other residential placement;
- a writ of assistance authorizing law-enforcement-aided transport;
- and fees from Jane Kay Cortez's own funds.

Where the record is materially disputed and the requested control is unusually broad, the objective reasonableness of shifting those attorney fees to Jane Kay Cortez is weak.

### 3. Self-interest concerns are substantial

The present record supports substantial concern that the petition is not merely a neutral effort to protect Jane Kay Cortez, but also a vehicle for family-control, residence-control, housing-control, and possible financial-control claims. That makes the `ORS 125.098` self-interest factor weigh against a fee award on the present record.

### 4. The underlying occurrences remain disputed

The Court may also consider the conduct of the party in the transactions or occurrences that gave rise to the proceeding. The present record includes:

- a restraining-order history involving Solomon Barber;
- preserved March 10, 2026 communications reflecting a noncooperation / service-evasion posture;
- a disputed writ-based forced-transport sequence;
- and a petition that overstates Benjamin Barber's role while understating HACC / Quantum delay and outside-document-flow concerns.

Those disputes weigh against immediate fee shifting from Jane Kay Cortez's funds.

## Best filing-safe conclusion

The clean conclusion for current use is:

1. Any request to shift Solomon Barber's attorney fees or costs to Jane Kay Cortez's funds should be denied on the present record.
2. At minimum, fee issues should be deferred until after:
   - the Court determines whether any protective relief is actually warranted;
   - the Court resolves the disputed emergency / notice / writ issues;
   - and the Court can assess the `ORS 125.098` factors on a fuller and cleaner record.

## Separate-forum fee point

If there is any serious fee-shifting question arising out of the Section 18 / HACC / Quantum branch, the cleaner location for that issue is not this probate matter and not Jane Kay Cortez's funds.

The safer current formulation is:

1. probate should not shift Solomon Barber's fees to Jane Kay Cortez under `ORS 125.095` on this disputed record; and
2. if HACC or Quantum Residential are later found liable in a separate federal civil-rights or related housing-rights action, any fee request should be pursued there under the fee statutes and remedies applicable to that separate action.
3. the present record instead supports the position that Benjamin Barber completed the relocation-side tasks assigned to him and was left waiting for the responsible housing actors to materialize an actual contract or lease for signature, whether through the Clackamas County side involving Housing Authority of Clackamas County and Quantum Residential / Parkside Heights / Blossom, or through the Multnomah County side involving Home Forward and Brighthaven / Vera / Waterleaf options.

Important limitation:

- `42 U.S.C. § 1988(b)` is a civil-rights fee statute. It applies in an action or proceeding to enforce certain listed civil-rights provisions, including `42 U.S.C. § 1983`, but it is not itself a direct fee provision for `42 U.S.C. § 1437p` standing alone.

So the safest litigation position is:

- do not argue that `§ 1988(b)` directly authorizes Section 18 fees in this probate case;
- do argue that if HACC / Quantum fee exposure exists, it belongs in the separate federal civil-rights / housing track, not as a probate-side charge against Jane Kay Cortez's funds.
