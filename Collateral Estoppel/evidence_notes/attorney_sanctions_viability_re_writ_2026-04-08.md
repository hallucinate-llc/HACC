## Why the present procedural posture is questionable

1. The writ expressly relies on an earlier order:
- `pursuant to the terms of an order entered in the above captioned matter, the Court has authorized the issuance of a writ of assistance`
- but the underlying signed order is not included with the writ packet itself.

2. The checked UTCR readiness box is not the same as proving the underlying appointment/order complied with the separate statutory notice rules for temporary fiduciary practice.

3. The record presently available to Movant still does not cleanly show:
- the signed underlying temporary-guardian order,
- the exact immediate-danger finding,
- the docket trail,
- or the notice path required by the protective-proceeding statutes.

## Knowledge problem: actual knowledge versus what counsel should have known

The current record is stronger on `should have known` than on `actually knew`.

### What is not yet cleanly proved

- The writ certificate is dated March 27, 2026.
- The preserved counsel-notice chain about the prior restraining-order conflict begins on April 3, 2026, with follow-up on April 4 and April 8.

So the present record does not yet cleanly prove that Bluestone had actual notice of the restraining-order conflict before he submitted the writ on March 27.

### What is fairly arguable now

Even without that prior-knowledge proof, a reasonable attorney should have known that submitting a writ authorizing forced entry and transport required:

- a valid signed predicate order,
- a sound notice basis,
- and accurate service/readiness certification.

If those prerequisites were absent, incomplete, or materially obscured, the filing becomes sanction-risk conduct even without proof that counsel already knew every contrary fact in the broader family dispute.

## Best current legal framing

The strongest present theories are:

- `ORCP 17`: counsel signed and presented an extraordinary proposed writ without an adequately shown factual/procedural basis if the underlying order and notice predicates were missing or defective.
- Oregon RPC `3.1`: filing extreme coercive process without a sufficient basis may implicate meritorious-claims/contentions standards.
- Oregon RPC `3.3` and ORS `9.460(2)`: if later records show omission of a material contrary order history or misleading presentation to the court, the candor problem becomes stronger.
