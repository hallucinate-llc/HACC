## OPENING STATEMENT (5 minutes—read exactly as written)

```
Your Honor, defendants respectfully move to join Quantum Residential Property Management 
as a necessary and indispensable party under Oregon Rules of Civil Procedure 29A and 22B.

The facts are straightforward: When HACC approved the demolition of Hillside Park under 
Section 18 in September 2024, it triggered statutory relocation obligations that required 
HACC to process the household's intake application and provide Section 18 preconditions—
counseling, comparability analysis, moving expenses, and consultation.

The household did their part: They submitted their intake application to Quantum Residential, 
the property manager at Hillside Manor, the replacement housing. That is the fact established 
by HACC Housing Resource Manager Ashley Ferron's own email of January 26, 2026.

But Quantum never transmitted that application to HACC. And now HACC sits here asking this 
court to hold it liable for intake processing, while Quantum—the party that actually 
obstruct the intake—remains free from liability.

This Court cannot grant complete relief under those circumstances. If HACC is held responsible 
for intake failure, will it be liable to the household for damages? Simultaneously, Quantum—who 
actually has the application and failed to transmit it—will escape responsibility. That is 
precisely the "inconsistent obligations" standard that makes joinder mandatory under ORCP 29A(3).

Moreover, Quantum is not just a peripheral PM. According to HACC's own Administrative Plan, 
Quantum is the property manager of Hillside Manor Limited Partnership, the RAD successor to 
Hillside Park. Under HUD Notice PIH 2019-23, RAD successors and their PM's inherit all 
tenant-protection and relocation obligations. Quantum is bound by the same $42 USC 1437p 
Section 18 duties as HACC.

Your Honor, the threshold question is whether HACC can obtain complete relief without 
holding Quantum liable. The answer is clearly no. The intake failure was caused by Quantum's 
act—receipt and non-transmission. The relocation obstruction was caused by Quantum's act—
withholding access to its office and replacement housing.

Joinder is necessary. Joinder is proper. We respectfully request the Court grant the motion.
```

## ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS FROM BENCH (with answers)

### Q1: "How do we know Quantum actually received the intake application?"

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, HACC's own Housing Resource Manager Ashley Ferron confirmed receipt in writing 
on January 26, 2026 (Exhibit L). Ms. Ferron states: 'The intake packet was submitted to 
Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office.' She then directs the 
household to 'Ask Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office to send the 
previously submitted intake packet directly to me.'

The phrase 'previously submitted' proves Ms. Ferron knows Quantum had the packet. And the 
fact that she had to ask the household to request it shows Quantum was not forwarding it of 
its own accord.

This is from HACC's own agent. If there is a credibility contest, it's not between the family 
and Quantum—it's between HACC's own employee saying Quantum has the packet, and Quantum 
claiming it doesn't."

---

### Q2: "Isn't Quantum just a property manager? Why should property managers be 'third-party defendants' in every HUD case?"

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, Quantum is not just any property manager. According to HACC's own Administrative 
Plan (Exhibit B, line 37308), Quantum manages a PHA-owned property that was created through a 
Rent Assistance Demonstration (RAD) conversion. The RAD conversion is governed by HUD Notice 
PIH 2019-23, which explicitly imposes successor-owner and successor-PM obligations on all 
parties involved in RAD properties.

Quantum accepted the role of PM at Hillside Manor LP—a PHA property with RAD obligations. 
Quantum did not accept a role in a purely private transaction. HACC made clear in its 
Administrative Plan filing to HUD that Quantum is managing PHA functions.

Only in this case, the 'functions' include being the intake point for Section 18 relocation. 
That is the household's evidence: They were directed to submit intake to Quantum's office.

So the question is not 'why should all PMs be defendants?' but rather 'why should Quantum—
a PM in a RAD PHA property, serving as the intake point, and controlling access to relocation 
housing—escape responsibility when it blocks intake?'

The answer is: it shouldn't. That's why joinder is mandatory."

---

### Q3: "Can't HACC just sue Quantum later in a separate case to recover damages? That seems easier than joining them now."

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, that argument misunderstands the joinder standard. ORCP 29A does not ask 'is 
there an easier way to get relief?' It asks: 'Can complete relief be accorded among those 
already parties, or would any party face inconsistent obligations?'

The answer is no and yes. The reason is that Quantum controls the essential fact—whether the 
intake packet was received and is still in Quantum's possession. If this case proceeds without 
Quantum:

(1) The household cannot get complete relief because Quantum controls whether relocation housing 
    is provided, and Quantum is not bound by any judgment against HACC;

(2) HACC faces inconsistent obligations: it is told by this court it must provide Section 18 
    relocation, but it cannot do so because Quantum—the PM of the replacement housing—is free 
    to obstruct the process;

(3) A later lawsuit against Quantum years down the road does not solve the immediate problem: 
    by then, the Section 18 relocation window has closed, other relocation opportunities may 
    be gone, and the family is permanently displaced.

Joinder now is not duplicative. It is the only way this court can render complete relief."

---

### Q4: "The ORCP 29A compulsory joinder standard requires 'complete relief cannot be accorded among those already parties.' HACC is the housing authority. Can't HACC just order Quantum to comply as its agent?"

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, that assumes facts not in evidence. HACC has not claimed that Quantum is its 
agent. According to HACC's Administrative Plan, Quantum is the named property manager of 
Hillside Manor LP, which is a Limited Partnership entity. HACC owns the property, but Quantum 
manages it under either a third-party management agreement or a RAD LP structure.

The evidence shows HACC treats Quantum as an independent operator: HACC had to ask the 
household to ask Quantum to do something. That is not how principals deal with agents. That is 
how separate entities deal with each other.

Moreover, even if Quantum were HACC's agent, HACC has not shown it can control Quantum's 
conduct now. The fact that Quantum falsely denied receiving the application (per the household 
declaration) shows Quantum is not freely cooperating.

This court cannot render complete relief against Quantum's obstruction without making Quantum 
a party and holding it bound by the judgment."

---

### Q5: "This case is an eviction. Shouldn't we finish the eviction and handle the Section 18 and relocation issues in a separate case?"

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, that approach violates ORCP 29A. The household's defenses to eviction include:

(1) Section 18 non-compliance (lack of counseling, comparability analysis, moving expenses, 
    consultation);
(2) Disability accommodation failure (third-floor unit offered to someone with mobility 
    limitation; no accessible alternative provided);
(3) Estoppel / prevent doctrine (HACC and Quantum jointly obstructed relocation; household 
    cannot be evicted without relocation).

All three defenses depend on facts involving Quantum. If Quantum is not a party, this court 
cannot order Quantum to provide relief. The judgment against HACC might be worthless if Quantum 
refuses to coordinate.

Example: If this court orders HACC to pay relocation moving expenses, but Quantum refuses to 
allow the family into comparable housing, the family is still evicted. Only joinder gives this 
court power to order Quantum to cooperate.

Furthermore, the RAD successor obligations (PIH 2019-23) bind Quantum in this very transaction. 
Joinder is not creating a new liability; it is holding Quantum accountable for obligations it 
already inherited when it became PM of the RAD property."

---

### Q6: "The household also has a disability accommodation claim mentioned in the motion. Isn't that a separate issue from joinder?"

**Answer:**
"Your Honor, disability accommodation is one of the six findings that supports joinder. The 
household has a documented mobility limitation. HACC approved a unit at Hillside Manor—the 
third floor. That unit is not accessible. The household requested an accessible unit. No 
accessible alternative was offered.

Under 24 CFR Part 8 (disability non-discrimination) and the Fair Housing Act, both HACC and 
Quantum—as PM of the property—have duties to offer accessible housing or provide reasonable 
accommodations (accessible relocation).

This court cannot grant complete relief for disability accommodation without also holding 
Quantum liable. Quantum controls the property. Quantum is the PM who evaluates accessibility 
and unit assignments. Joinder is essential to remedy this claim."

### Exhibit #4: ORCP 29A Compulsory Joinder Standard (printout for court reference)

```
OREGON RULES OF CIVIL PROCEDURE 29A —— JOINDER OF PERSONS

"A person must be joined as a party if:
  (1) In the person's absence, complete relief cannot be accorded among those 
      already parties, OR
  (2) The person claims an interest in the subject matter of the action and is 
      so situated that the disposition of the action in the person's absence 
      may: 
      (i)  impair or impede the person's ability to protect that interest, or
      (ii) leave any party subject to substantial risk of incurring double, 
           multiple, or otherwise inconsistent obligations relating to or arising 
           out of the same transaction."

                                    ───────────────────

HOUSEHOLD'S POSITION IN THIS CASE:

Standard (1) — Complete Relief Impossible Without Quantum:
✓ Quantum has the intake packet
✓ Quantum failed to transmit
✓ Without Quantum's transmissal, HACC cannot process application
✓ Without processing, no relocation offer can be made
✓ Without relocation, household faces eviction with no relief
✓ Quantum must be party for complete relief

Standard (2) — Inconsistent Obligations Risk:
✓ If HACC is held liable, it will owe relocation damages
✓ If Quantum is not joined, Quantum owes nothing despite causing the failure
✓ Court's judgment against HACC does not bind Quantum
✓ Quantum remains free to refuse to cooperate
✓ Both standards satisfied: Joinder is MANDATORY, not discretionary
```

**Talking point:** "Your Honor, ORCP 29A has an 'or' in it. We satisfy both prongs. Complete 
relief is impossible without Quantum because Quantum controls the packet and the housing. And 
there is substantial risk of inconsistent obligations because HACC could be held liable while 
Quantum—the actual cause of obstruction—goes free."

## CRITICAL REMINDERS

1. **Do not ad-lib.** Use the opening statement and answers above. If you have a different 
   phrasing you prefer, practice it beforehand.

2. **Pause before answering.** Let the judge's question finish; take a breath; then answer. 
   This shows confidence.

3. **Do not bad-mouth HACC or Quantum.** Stick to facts. Let the facts speak. "Your Honor, 
   Ferron's email shows the packet was submitted to Quantum. The lack of transmission is 
   objective fact."

4. **Do not ask the judge questions.** You make statements; the judge asks you questions.

5. **If the judge is skeptical, don't push back.** Instead, say: "Your Honor, I understand 
   your concern. Let me address it this way: [answer]. The point is that ORCP 29A has an 'or' 
   in it. We satisfy both standards."

6. **If you lose, do not show disappointment.** Say: "Thank you, Your Honor. We respectfully 
   disagree and may appeal, but we will continue with our affirmative defenses."
