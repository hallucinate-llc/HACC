# Defendants' Motion For Joinder Of Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P.

IN THE CLACKAMAS COUNTY JUSTICE COURT
STATE OF OREGON

HOUSING AUTHORITY OF CLACKAMAS COUNTY,
Plaintiff,

v.

BENJAMIN JAY BARBER and JANE KAY CORTEZ,
Defendants.

Case No. 26FE0586

DEFENDANTS' MOTION FOR JOINDER OF QUANTUM RESIDENTIAL PROPERTY MANAGEMENT, L.P.

Defendants Benjamin Jay Barber and Jane Kay Cortez move to join Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P. ("Quantum") under ORCP 29A and, in the alternative, seek leave for third-party practice under ORCP 22C. This motion is intended to be filed with defendants' response to Plaintiff's eviction motion and related request for narrow interim relief because the same housing-processing chain is already at issue in that packet.

## Basis For The Motion

Quantum should be joined because the present eviction and relocation dispute already turns on a housing-processing chain that HACC's own records place partly on the Quantum side.

Specifically:

1. HACC directed the household into a replacement-housing path tied to the Hillside Park redevelopment, including the Blossom / Hillside Manor intake route reflected in HACC's January 8, 2026 notice.
2. HACC later acknowledged in writing that the intake packet had been submitted to Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC, and HACC told the household to ask Quantum staff to send the previously submitted packet directly to HACC.
3. Defendants contend that this intake failure helped prevent timely relocation, timely housing review, and timely completion of the replacement-housing path.
4. Defendants also seek interim relief that would preserve the intake, forwarding, review, and status-tracking chain for the same housing path.

The supporting theory is source-linked rather than conclusory. Section 18 imposed relocation duties on HACC; HACC's own notices and March 26, 2026 options email imposed additional process duties on the replacement-housing path HACC selected; and HACC's later acknowledgment placed the intake-routing failure on the Quantum side of that same path. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122); Ex. AA (binder pp. 164-167).

## Why Joinder Is Necessary

Under ORCP 29A, joinder is appropriate where complete relief cannot be accorded among the existing parties in the absent party's absence, or where the existing parties face a substantial risk of inconsistent obligations.

That standard is met here because:

1. Complete relief is difficult without Quantum where HACC's own evidence says the disputed intake packet was left with Quantum staff and did not reach HACC.
2. The Court cannot fully resolve responsibility for the alleged intake failure, preservation of the missing packet trail, and any forward-looking processing relief while Quantum remains outside the case.
3. There is a real risk of inconsistent positions or obligations if HACC continues to attribute the intake failure to Quantum while Quantum is not before the Court to admit, deny, explain, or correct the same record.

## Alternative Basis

If the Court concludes that compulsory joinder is not the best procedural vehicle, third-party practice remains proper in substance under ORCP 22C because HACC's own account places Quantum in the same intake-and-processing chain that underlies defendants' relocation, housing-access, and interim-relief defenses.

More specifically, the same record supports closely related third-party or directly related claims arising from the same facts:

1. HACC's own position implies that Quantum failed to carry out part of the replacement-housing processing work expected on the project-management side, which could support a breach-of-contract, indemnity, or related nonperformance claim by HACC against Quantum if HACC contends Quantum's conduct contributed to the stalled relocation path.
2. ORCP 22C is the Oregon rule governing third-party practice against a nonparty who is or may be liable for all or part of the claim being litigated, and it is therefore the better procedural fit if the Court prefers impleader over direct compulsory joinder.
3. Once Quantum is properly before the Court as a third-party defendant, ORCP 22C(1) also allows other parties to assert claims against the third-party defendant arising out of the same transaction or occurrence. That matters here because Benjamin Jay Barber and Jane Kay Cortez likewise contend that Quantum failed to process, forward, or bring to a clear result the Blossom / Hillside Manor application path after accepting the packet through the route HACC directed them to use. On the present record, that can support direct claims by defendants against Quantum for breach of undertaken processing obligations, breach of implied contract, promissory estoppel, and related housing-access claims arising from the same nucleus of facts.

## Relief Requested

Defendants ask the Court to:

1. join Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P. as a party to this action; or
2. alternatively permit third-party practice or other comparable procedure sufficient to bring Quantum before the Court on the intake-processing, contract-breach, and relocation-chain issues already raised in this case; and
3. grant such further relief as is just and proper.
