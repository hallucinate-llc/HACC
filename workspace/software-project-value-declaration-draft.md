# Declaration Re Software Project Value and Lost Opportunity

I, Benjamin Jay Barber, declare as follows:

1. I am a plaintiff in this matter and have personal knowledge of the facts stated here.

2. During the time period relevant to this case, I was actively developing open-source AI legal software intended to assist with legal intake, evidence organization, legal research, and complaint generation. I was also working to convert case law and law into formal logic so that legal rules and reasoning could be compiled like a programming language through an intermediate representation using frame logic, deontic cognitive event calculus, and deontic temporal first-order logic.

3. That software project was not a vague idea or purely hypothetical future concept. It was an active development effort reflected in local repository work, project materials, and ongoing development activity during the same period as the housing dispute.

4. I was developing this software in connection with work involving the Linux Foundation and Stanford Law School. I am on the technical steering committee of the Linux Foundation LF Edge / Infinite Edge AI effort, and I am also a contributor to the Stanford Law School Codex program.

5. My plan for the project included a dual-license and service model. The software and the underlying legal-logic work were intended to be open sourced and licensed under the AGPL by default, while any non-AGPL license would require payment. I also intended to provide related services tied to the software, including attorney matchmaking and contingency litigation financing. The intended users and customers for the software and related services included government agencies and attorneys. My expected revenue model for at least part of those services was a percentage of fees, which could include attorney fees, placement-related fees, financing returns, or some combination of those categories.

6. This was a large and ongoing research and development project rather than a project with only one discrete milestone. Even so, I expected to continue advancing the project during the relevant time period through ongoing development, formalization of legal logic, preparation for open-source release, and movement toward later licensing and service opportunities.

7. During the same period, I was forced to spend substantial time on relocation instability, voucher issues, accommodation disputes, documentation demands, notice disputes, and related housing matters described elsewhere in the complaint.

8. Those disruptions reduced the time and attention I could devote to software development, slowed milestone completion, and interfered with my ability to move the project toward deployment, licensing, service delivery, or institutional collaboration.

9. As a result, I contend that the housing-related disruption caused economic harm in the form of one or more of the following:
   delayed development time, delayed service revenue, delayed dual-license revenue, lost collaboration opportunity, lost pilot opportunity, reduced commercialization speed, or reduced project value.

10. The clearest examples of the disrupted project value are:
   delayed development progress, delayed formalization of legal rules into the project's intermediate representation, delayed open-source publication work, delayed progress toward later non-AGPL licensing opportunities, and delayed progress toward service offerings such as attorney matchmaking and contingency litigation financing.

11. I am continuing to gather supporting materials including git history, project records, milestone notes, pricing ideas, service plans, correspondence, and any other materials that reflect the timing and value of the project.

12. I make this declaration in support of the complaint and any request for damages, injunctive relief, or related relief.

I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the United States that the foregoing is true and correct.
