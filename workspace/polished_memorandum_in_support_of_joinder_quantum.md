# Memorandum In Support Of Defendants' Motion For Joinder Of Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P.

IN THE CLACKAMAS COUNTY JUSTICE COURT
STATE OF OREGON

HOUSING AUTHORITY OF CLACKAMAS COUNTY,
Plaintiff,

v.

BENJAMIN JAY BARBER and JANE KAY CORTEZ,
Defendants.

Case No. 26FE0586

Defendants submit this memorandum in support of their motion to join Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P. This memorandum is designed to pair with defendants' response to Plaintiff's eviction motion and related cross-motion for narrow interim relief because the same intake, forwarding, review, and relocation chain is already before the Court in that filing set.

## I. Core Point

The present eviction dispute is not limited to a simple failure to vacate. The source-linked question is whether HACC was still obligated to complete the relocation and replacement-housing paths it had triggered and whether Quantum was part of the process chain through which those duties were supposed to be carried out. HACC itself has placed Quantum inside that chain. Once HACC did that, Quantum ceased to be a stranger to the dispute. It became a materially involved actor whose presence is needed for complete relief and to avoid inconsistent positions about what happened to the household's intake packet and replacement-housing path.

## II. Record Basis For Joinder

The current record supports the following narrow points:

1. HACC triggered redevelopment-related relocation obligations through the Hillside Park process.
2. HACC's January 8, 2026 notice directed the household into the Blossom / Tenant Protection Voucher path, which defendants understood to include the Hillside Manor office intake route.
3. HACC later wrote that the intake packet had been submitted to Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC.
4. HACC told the household to ask Quantum staff to send the previously submitted packet directly to HACC.
5. Benjamin Barber contemporaneously complained that Blossom had not processed the application, had refused to house a service animal, and had engaged in race discrimination.
6. Defendants' broader eviction response now seeks to preserve exactly that intake, forwarding, review, and status-tracking chain while the Court determines whether eviction is premature.

The notice record is also sequentially coherent rather than overstated. The current packet contains direct February 2, 2026 notice to `blossom@quantumres.com` through Exhibit T (binder pp. 63-93), later preserved February 26, March 2, and March 9 complaint notice to Kati Tilton, Ashley Ferron, and `charity@magikcorp.com` through Exhibit R (binder pp. 58-62), and then HACC's own later written acknowledgment that the packet had been left with Quantum staff and had not reached HACC through Exhibit V (binder pp. 94-122). That sequence matters because it shows both project-side notice and HACC-side acknowledgment inside the same application path.

That record is already enough to show that Quantum is not merely adjacent to the controversy. Quantum is alleged to be one of the reasons the replacement-housing path stalled.

## III. ORCP 29A Supports Joinder

ORCP 29A allows joinder where complete relief cannot be accorded among existing parties in the person's absence, or where the action may leave existing parties subject to a substantial risk of inconsistent obligations.

That standard is met here for at least two reasons.

First, complete relief is difficult without Quantum. HACC's own written account is that the disputed intake packet was left with Quantum staff but did not reach HACC. If the Court is asked to decide whether the household followed the directed housing path, whether the intake chain failed, whether records must be preserved, or whether additional processing relief is warranted, Quantum's absence leaves a material gap in the adjudication.

Second, there is a substantial risk of inconsistent positions or obligations if Quantum is not joined. HACC has effectively said: the packet was submitted to Quantum staff, and Quantum did not get it to HACC. If HACC remains the only party, the Court could be asked to allocate blame, preserve records, or shape relief around a processing failure that Quantum has not had to admit, deny, or explain. ORCP 29A exists precisely to avoid that kind of fragmented adjudication.

## IV. The Strongest Theory Is Processing-Chain Joinder, Not Maximum-Theory Joinder

The safest joinder theory is the narrow one.

Defendants do not need to prove at this stage that Quantum inherited every possible Section 18, RAD, VAWA, disability, or race-discrimination duty in full before joinder can be justified. The stronger and cleaner point is that:

1. HACC itself routed the household into a housing path that used Quantum's office and staff.
2. HACC itself later said the packet had been given to Quantum staff and had not been transmitted onward.
3. Defendants now seek relief that would preserve or restore that same processing chain.

That alone is enough to make Quantum materially connected to the Court's ability to accord complete relief.

Quantum's property-management role at Hillside Manor still matters, but primarily because it reinforces that Quantum was participating in the same replacement-housing environment and intake route, not because the Court must decide every underlying federal merits issue before joinder.

## V. Quantum Also Fits The Alternative ORCP 22C Theory

If the Court views this issue through third-party practice rather than compulsory joinder, Quantum is still a proper party in substance under ORCP 22C. HACC's own account places Quantum in the packet-routing and intake-processing chain that defendants say contributed to the stalled relocation path and the premature eviction posture. That is enough to support bringing Quantum into the same action so the Court can resolve the connected processing dispute in one place.

That is also the correct Oregon rule pairing. ORCP 29A addresses persons needed for just adjudication. ORCP 22B, by contrast, addresses cross-claims between defendants already in the case. ORCP 22C is the rule that addresses third-party practice against a nonparty who is or may be liable for all or part of the claim being litigated. Because Quantum is presently outside the case, ORCP 22C is the better alternative procedural vehicle if the Court prefers impleader over direct joinder. ORS 52.010, ORS 52.030, and ORS 52.310 support using circuit-court pleading rules in this justice-court civil action.

The alternative ORCP 22C theory is stronger if stated concretely. The same factual record supports at least three closely related categories of claims that all turn on what happened to the application packet after it reached the Hillside Manor / Quantum side:

1. HACC-against-Quantum contract-side claims. If HACC contends Quantum was responsible for receiving, handling, scanning, logging, reviewing, or forwarding the replacement-housing packet and failed to do so, HACC's own position points toward a breach-of-contract, failure-to-perform, indemnity, or closely related claim by HACC against Quantum arising from the management or intake relationship between them.
2. Barber-and-Cortez-against-Quantum direct claims once Quantum is brought in. ORCP 22C(1) expressly permits any party to assert claims against a third-party defendant arising out of the same transaction or occurrence. That matters here because Benjamin Jay Barber and Jane Kay Cortez contend that they used the very route HACC directed them to use, delivered the packet, and were then deprived of timely processing, forwarding, or a clear written result. On that record, their direct claims against Quantum are not collateral. They arise from the same failed application path and can fairly be described as breach of undertaken processing obligations, breach of implied contract, promissory estoppel, and related housing-access interference or discrimination claims.
3. Shared causation and allocation questions. Whether styled as joinder, impleader, or directly related claims, the same core questions recur: who received the packet, what duties attached after receipt, what should have been done with it, and whether Quantum's nonprocessing helped cause the stalled relocation and premature-eviction posture now before the Court.

That is why alternative ORCP 22C treatment makes procedural sense. The Court should not have to decide the eviction and interim-relief issues in one proceeding while leaving the closely related project-side breach and nonprocessing issues for a separate later action built on the same facts.

## VI. The Same Source-Linked Duty Structure Makes Quantum's Presence More Important

The eviction-response packet now identifies the main duties at issue through specific sources rather than generalized assertion. That matters here too.

The case now turns in part on:

1. Section 18 relocation completion duties under 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d) and 24 C.F.R. § 970.21.
2. VAWA transfer and related confidentiality duties under 24 C.F.R. §§ 5.2007 and 5.2009.
3. Hearing duties under 42 U.S.C. § 1437d(k) and 24 C.F.R. § 982.555.
4. Fair Housing Act accommodation duties under 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B) and 24 C.F.R. § 100.204.
5. FHA anti-interference duties under 42 U.S.C. § 3617 and 24 C.F.R. § 100.400.
6. Section 1981 contracting-equality duties under 42 U.S.C. § 1981.

The joinder point is not that Quantum necessarily bears all of those duties in the same way as HACC. The joinder point is that the Court will be asked to evaluate whether HACC was obligated to complete those source-linked processes before possession could be granted, and HACC's own records place Quantum inside the factual chain through which those duties were supposed to be carried out. That makes Quantum's presence materially useful and, on this record, likely necessary.

More specifically, HACC's own documents show:

1. Hillside Manor is a RAD PBV project in HACC's Administrative Plan;
2. Quantum is identified there as the property management company;
3. HACC publicly stated that applications for Blossom and Community Apartments would be processed through HACC's PBV waitlist and that Hillside relocation households were priority groups; and
4. HACC later wrote that the packet had been submitted to Quantum staff at the Hillside Manor office but had not reached HACC.

That combination is why Quantum belongs in the case. The Court is not being asked to drag in a stranger to the relocation process. The Court is being asked to join the project-side participant HACC itself placed inside the federally rooted and locally administered replacement-housing chain. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122); Ex. Y (binder pp. 156-159); Ex. Z (binder pp. 160-163).

## VII. Conclusion

The Court should grant the motion to join Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P. The narrower and better-grounded basis is that HACC's own records place Quantum in the exact intake-and-relocation chain the Court is already being asked to evaluate and preserve. At minimum, the Court should permit joinder or equivalent third-party practice so the intake-processing dispute is resolved in one action rather than through partial litigation against only HACC.
