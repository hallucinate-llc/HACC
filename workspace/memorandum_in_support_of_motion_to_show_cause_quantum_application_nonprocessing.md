# Memorandum In Support Of Defendants' Motion For Order To Show Cause Re Quantum Residential's Nonprocessing Of The Barber-Cortez Applications

IN THE CLACKAMAS COUNTY JUSTICE COURT
STATE OF OREGON

HOUSING AUTHORITY OF CLACKAMAS COUNTY,
Plaintiff,

v.

BENJAMIN JAY BARBER and JANE KAY CORTEZ,
Defendants.

Case No. 26FE0586

Defendants submit this memorandum in support of their motion for an order to show cause directed to Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P. This request is designed to pair with defendants' joinder motion and eviction-response packet because the same application-processing failure already sits at the center of the dispute.

## I. The Request Is Narrow

Defendants are not asking the Court, through this motion alone, to decide every merits issue against Quantum. The narrower request is that once Quantum is before the Court, Quantum should be required to explain what happened to the Blossom / Hillside Manor application path for Benjamin Barber and Jane Kay Cortez.

That is a practical case-management request. It is aimed at clarifying a discrete processing failure already reflected in HACC's own written records.

## II. The Record Already Supports A Real Nonprocessing Question

The current record supports the following:

1. HACC's January 8, 2026 notice directed the household into the Blossom / Tenant Protection Voucher path.
2. Benjamin Barber states that he personally delivered a paper Blossom application in December 2025.
3. HACC later acknowledged in writing that the intake packet had been submitted to Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC.
4. Benjamin Barber then sent contemporaneous written complaints stating that Blossom had refused to process the application, had refused to house a service animal, and had engaged in discrimination.

That record is enough to justify a direct explanation from Quantum once Quantum is in the case.

The present packet is also careful about the recipient trail. Exhibit T (binder pp. 63-93) is the direct February 2, 2026 email to `blossom@quantumres.com`. Exhibit R (binder pp. 58-62) separately preserves the later February 26, March 2, and March 9 complaint chain to Kati Tilton, Ashley Ferron, and `charity@magikcorp.com`. Exhibit V (binder pp. 94-122) then preserves HACC's own packet-routing acknowledgment. Taken together, those exhibits support a clean chronology of direct project-side notice, later complaint notice, and HACC-side acknowledgment without overstating who received which later email.

This is not merely an unexplained apartment application in the abstract. The application was part of a documented replacement-housing process. HACC's own materials show that:

1. the Hillside redevelopment had already triggered relocation obligations rooted in Section 18 and HUD's relocation rule;
2. HACC's public Blossom materials stated that applications for Blossom and Community Apartments would be processed through HACC's PBV waitlist and that Hillside relocation households were priority groups;
3. HACC's Administrative Plan identifies Hillside Manor as a RAD PBV project and Quantum as the property management company; and
4. HACC's January 8, 2026 notice directed the household to choose between the Blossom path and the Tenant Protection Voucher path.

HACC's March 26, 2026 options email then confirmed that relocation was still being administered through three unresolved housing paths, all with an April 30, 2026 completion deadline and a threatened eviction consequence if the move was not completed. That matters here because it shows the application-processing issue was still part of a live relocation process, not a stale side dispute. The record therefore supports a narrower and more practical conclusion: HACC was still responsible for administering the offered replacement-housing paths, defendants were entitled to rely on those paths while they were being processed, and the Court may require a project-side participant already identified in HACC's own records to explain a packet-routing failure inside that same chain. See Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122); Ex. Y (binder pp. 156-159); Ex. Z (binder pp. 160-163); Ex. AA (binder pp. 164-167).

So the application-processing problem the Court is being asked to clarify is not a side issue. It sits inside the same federal-relocation and HACC-administered replacement-housing chain that underlies the eviction response.

## III. The Show-Cause Request Helps The Court Resolve The Existing Eviction And Interim-Relief Questions

The Court is already being asked to decide whether eviction is premature and whether the housing-processing chain should be preserved. That inquiry is materially aided by knowing:

1. whether Quantum actually received the applications;
2. what Quantum did with them;
3. whether Quantum forwarded anything to HACC;
4. whether Quantum ever gave any written result or deficiency notice; and
5. whether service-animal, voucher, race, or other screening concerns played a role.

Without that information, the Court is left with a record in which HACC attributes a packet-routing failure to Quantum while Quantum has not yet been required to admit, deny, or explain the same events.

## IV. The Court Should Require A Targeted Written Response, Not A Broad Merits Brief

The most useful form of relief is a short written show-cause response under appropriate certification. The response should identify:

1. receipt date or nonreceipt position;
2. identity of staff who handled the materials, if known;
3. whether the materials were scanned, logged, copied, forwarded, rejected, or lost;
4. whether any deficiency notice or final application result was issued; and
5. what records exist now.

That is narrow enough to fit the present posture while still giving the Court the information it needs to evaluate preservation, joinder, and interim relief.

It is also the least burdensome way to clarify whether the documented intake and forwarding failure occurred inside the very process HACC used to satisfy its relocation and replacement-housing obligations. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. T (binder pp. 63-93); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122).

## V. Conclusion

The Court should grant defendants' motion and direct Quantum Residential Property Management, L.P., upon joinder or other authorized appearance, to show cause in writing why the Benjamin Barber / Jane Kay Cortez application path was not processed to a clear result and what happened to the related application materials.
