# Declaration Of Benjamin Jay Barber In Support Of Defendants' Response To Eviction Motion And Cross-Motion For Interim Relief

IN THE CLACKAMAS COUNTY JUSTICE COURT
STATE OF OREGON

HOUSING AUTHORITY OF CLACKAMAS COUNTY,
Plaintiff,

v.

BENJAMIN JAY BARBER and JANE KAY CORTEZ,
Defendants.

Case No. 26FE0586

I, Benjamin Jay Barber, declare as follows:

1. I am a defendant in this action. I make this declaration from personal knowledge, and if called as a witness I could and would testify competently to the matters stated here.
2. I reside in the household directly affected by the housing, relocation, voucher, accommodation, and transfer problems described in this case.
3. My household has been dealing with displacement pressure, relocation delays, voucher delays, accommodation problems, service-animal issues, and the breakdown of the Blossom and Waterleaf housing paths.
4. HACC issued notices in late 2025 and early 2026 that put our housing stability at risk, including the December 23, 2025 no-cause notices, the December 31, 2025 displacement notice, and the February 4, 2026 for-cause notice. Exhibits C (binder pp. 8-13), D (binder pp. 14-18), E (binder pp. 19-22), and H (binder pp. 27-36).
5. During the same general period, our household was trying to move through the HACC-directed relocation and voucher path rather than remain where we were.
6. On January 8, 2026, HACC sent a Blossom-related notice stating that the Tenant Protection Voucher process had not yet started and identifying Ashley Ferron as the contact to begin that process. Exhibit G (binder pp. 23-26).
7. From my experience and review of the related communications, I understood HACC to be directing our household into the Hillside Park redevelopment replacement-housing application path, including the Blossom / Hillside Manor office intake route.
8. In December 2025, I personally delivered a paper Blossom application in person to a woman working on the Blossom-side intake or application process. I do not know her name.
9. On the same day that I turned in that Blossom paperwork, I was told in substance that my dog could not live there. At that time, my dog was my service animal.
10. I later learned that the paper Blossom application had not been turned in to HACC, despite my in-person submission.
11. On February 2, 2026, I emailed Ashley Ferron and `blossom@quantumres.com` stating that I had met a Black couple with one child at Balfour Park who had just been granted Section 8 and were interviewing for Blossom while our own application had not been sent on to HACC. Exhibit T (binder pp. 63-93).
12. In that same February 2, 2026 email, I expressly accused Quantum of race-based `lemon dropping`. Exhibit T (binder pp. 63-93).
13. After that February 2, 2026 email, I continued making written complaints to Kati Tilton, Ashley Ferron, and `charity@magikcorp.com` that Blossom had refused to process the application, had refused to house a service animal, and had engaged in discrimination, while HACC and Quantum continued to delay the steps needed to leave HACC-controlled housing. Exhibit R (binder pp. 58-62).
14. HACC later acknowledged in writing that the intake packet had been submitted to Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC. Exhibit V (binder pp. 94-122).
15. In my experience, that failure to process or forward the packet materially delayed our housing path.
16. Our household was also seeking an out-of-county move to Waterleaf in Multnomah County.
17. The Waterleaf application record shows a created date of December 23, 2025 and shows the application on the waiting list by January 28, 2026. Exhibit W (binder pp. 123-126).
18. HACC's own correspondence shows that by January 12, 2026 HACC was told Waterleaf wanted the voucher amount and that the application had already been signed and turned in, and that by January 26, 2026 HACC acknowledged that the household had chosen to port to Multnomah County. Exhibit X (binder pp. 127-155).
19. By March 17 and March 18, 2026, I wrote that I had been trying to move to Waterleaf for nearly three months. Exhibit M (binder pp. 37-44).
20. By March 25 and March 26, 2026, Waterleaf still had our household only on the waiting list pending issuance of the voucher. Exhibit M (binder pp. 37-44).
21. In my experience, HACC did not process that Waterleaf transfer path with reasonable promptness.
22. As I understand the timeline, that alternate port-out path stretched across four calendar months, beginning with the December 23, 2025 Waterleaf application and still remaining incomplete by late March 2026.
23. During the March 23, 2026 inspection at Hillside Park, my mother and I heard Charley Skee make statements in substance explaining why we had not yet been moved.
24. In substance, we understood Charley Skee to say that I had been taken off the lease because my brother and my stepfather had been emailing documents to HACC, and that the situation continued until staff were able to sort through the documents after I sent a large batch of materials.
25. I did not request my own removal from the lease, and to my knowledge Jane Kay Cortez did not request my removal from the lease.
26. To my personal knowledge, Julio Regal Florez-Cortez cannot read or write in any language.
27. Because Julio could not read or write in any language, I do not believe he was independently generating written housing demands, packet corrections, or asserted-authority paperwork during the same period that HACC was acting on outside documents affecting household composition.
28. I offer paragraphs 23 through 27 as statements of what my mother and I personally heard during that inspection and of my own personal knowledge, not as a claim that I possess a separate written memorialization by Charley Skee using those exact words.
29. Our household also sought a two-bedroom, first-floor accommodation because Jane Cortez could not climb stairs, needed disability-related support, and needed a housing arrangement that fit her caregiver, privacy, mobility, ADL/IADL, and service-animal needs.
30. HACC first sent materials reflecting a two-bedroom voucher, then stated on March 19, 2026 that the two-bedroom issuance had been created and sent in error and that a one-bedroom voucher would instead issue. Exhibit M (binder pp. 37-44).
31. I responded that I had a scheduled appointment to obtain a reasonable accommodation for a two-bedroom unit and that the one-bedroom arrangement was not workable. Exhibit M (binder pp. 37-44).
32. By March 24, 2026, provider verification had been completed supporting Jane Cortez's disability status, the service-animal need, and the requested accommodation. Exhibit O (binder pp. 45-52).
33. Even after that, the accommodation and housing path remained delayed and uncertain, and HACC denied the two-bedroom increase while stating that a one-bedroom voucher could be used for a two-bedroom ground-floor unit within the affordability range. Exhibit P (binder pp. 53-57).
34. In my experience, the combined effect of the Hillside Park replacement-housing application nonprocessing, Waterleaf delay, voucher instability, accommodation problems, and the delay explanation Charley Skee gave during the March 23, 2026 inspection left our household without a stable and lawful path out of HACC-controlled housing.
35. Without immediate court intervention, I believe our household faces continuing risk of displacement, continued collapse of the transfer and relocation path, and continued loss of housing opportunities that cannot be fully repaired later with money damages.
36. If the Hillside Park replacement-housing application path, Waterleaf path, or voucher path is allowed to collapse while this case is pending, I believe the practical housing opportunity itself will be lost even if we later win on the merits.
37. The requested preliminary injunction would not give us an unfair windfall. It would preserve the status quo, require defendants to stop interfering with the housing path, and require them to process the relocation, transfer, voucher, and application issues lawfully while the Court decides the merits.

I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of Oregon that the foregoing is true and correct.
