## Emergency Motion

Plaintiffs Benjamin Barber, Jane Cortez, and Julio Cortez move for a temporary restraining order and preliminary injunction under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 65.

Plaintiffs request narrowly targeted emergency relief to preserve the status quo, prevent immediate housing loss, and require an accessible hearing process while this action proceeds. Plaintiffs seek an order that:

1. bars Defendants from evicting, removing, locking out, or otherwise displacing Plaintiffs from their current housing based on the challenged notices and lease-administration sequence;
2. bars Defendants from terminating, withholding, canceling, or impairing Plaintiffs' Tenant Protection Voucher, Housing Choice Voucher, transfer opportunity, or relocation benefits based on the challenged conduct described in the complaint;
3. requires HACC to provide Julio Cortez a prompt, meaningful, and accessible hearing process that does not depend on a written request and that permits oral, assisted, or otherwise accessible invocation of hearing rights;
4. requires HACC to preserve the status of Plaintiffs' relocation, transfer, and accommodation requests pending further order of the Court;
5. bars Defendants from retaliating against Plaintiffs for filing this action, requesting accommodations, requesting a hearing, or complaining about housing discrimination; and
6. sets an expedited schedule for a preliminary-injunction hearing.

## Grounds

This motion is based on the accompanying complaint, the companion authority and evidence map, the exhibits identified in the complaint, and the following grounds:

1. Plaintiffs are likely to succeed, or at minimum raise serious questions going to the merits, on their federal claims because the complaint alleges:
   a. HACC and Quantum denied or delayed disability-related housing relief and a two-bedroom accommodation despite documented disability-related needs;
   b. HACC conditioned Julio Cortez's access to a hearing on a written request even though he is incapable of writing in any language and had specifically requested a hearing orally;
   c. HACC provided no meaningful hearing before threatened housing loss;
   d. HACC and Quantum delayed or failed to process relocation and Blossom-related housing opportunities; and
   e. Defendants escalated notices, documentation barriers, and related conduct after protected complaints and threatened legal action.
2. Plaintiffs are likely to suffer immediate and irreparable harm absent emergency relief because the challenged conduct places them at risk of eviction, housing loss, denial of relocation opportunity, loss of voucher benefits, further delay in obtaining accessible housing, and continuing deprivation of hearing rights.
3. Monetary damages alone are inadequate because loss of housing, imminent displacement, denial of accessible process, and interference with disability accommodations cannot be fully remedied after the fact.
4. The balance of equities sharply favors Plaintiffs because the requested relief preserves the status quo and requires only lawful administration of hearing, voucher, relocation, and accommodation processes.
5. The public interest favors compliance with the Fair Housing Act, Section 504, Title II, due-process requirements, and federal housing-program hearing obligations.

## Memorandum Of Law

### I. Legal Standard

A temporary restraining order or preliminary injunction is appropriate where Plaintiffs show likely success on the merits, likely irreparable harm absent relief, a balance of equities in their favor, and that an injunction is in the public interest. Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 555 U.S. 7, 20 (2008).

In the Ninth Circuit, a preliminary injunction may also issue where serious questions going to the merits are raised and the balance of hardships tips sharply in the movant's favor, so long as irreparable harm and public interest are also shown. Alliance for the Wild Rockies v. Cottrell, 632 F.3d 1127, 1134-35 (9th Cir. 2011).

### II. Plaintiffs Are Likely To Succeed Or Raise Serious Questions On The Merits

The complaint alleges substantial federal violations.

First, Plaintiffs allege disability-discrimination and reasonable-accommodation violations. HACC allegedly reversed a two-bedroom voucher as having been issued in error, then conditioned reissuance on an accommodation process that Plaintiffs allege was delayed or denied even though the household's disability-related needs had been explained. Plaintiffs further allege that Quantum failed to process the Blossom application and refused to accommodate the household's service animal.

Those allegations are supported by the January 8, 2026 Blossom notice reflecting that the Tenant Protection Voucher process had not yet started, the preserved Gmail sent-mail complaint records dated February 26, March 2, and March 9, 2026, the broader additional-information thread corroborating those complaints, and the preserved March 17 through March 20 orientation and voucher emails. See Exhibit G, Exhibit R, Exhibit J, Exhibit M.

Second, Plaintiffs allege that HACC denied Julio Cortez meaningful access to the hearing process. According to the complaint, HACC told Julio Cortez he could request a hearing in writing even though HACC knew or should have known he was incapable of writing in any language, and HACC still provided no hearing after an oral request. On those facts, Plaintiffs plausibly state violations of due process, 42 U.S.C. section 1437d(k), 24 C.F.R. section 982.555, Title II, and Section 504.

The notice and hearing backdrop for that claim is reflected in the displacement and notice sequence preserved in Exhibits B, C, D, E, H, K, and N, together with the intake facts summarized in the accompanying declaration and authority map.

Exhibit N is especially significant because it is a direct 30-Day Notice of Termination addressed to Julio Cortez himself, dated February 13, 2026 and effective March 19, 2026. That document materially strengthens both the immediacy of the threatened injury to Julio Cortez and the need for an accessible hearing process before HACC can lawfully proceed.

Third, Plaintiffs allege retaliation and interference. The complaint asserts that HACC linked adverse action to protected complaints and threatened litigation, and that Defendants continued documentation barriers, notice conduct, application delay, and accommodation delay after protected complaints were made.

That retaliation and interference theory is supported in part by the February 4, 2026 for-cause notice, the February 13, 2026 termination notice addressed directly to Julio Cortez, and the preserved Gmail sent-mail records dated February 26, March 2, and March 9, 2026 in which Benjamin Barber complained to Kati Tilton, Ashley Ferron, and `charity@magikcorp.com` that Blossom had refused to process the application, refused to house a service animal, and that HACC and Quantum were continuing to delay relocation and voucher progress. See Exhibit H, Exhibit N, Exhibit R, and Exhibit J. Plaintiffs' more specific account that HACC personnel tied the notice sequence to threatened litigation, and that related complaints reached county counsel, is presently supported through declaration testimony, intake materials, and recipient metadata rather than a preserved email quoted in the motion record.

At the emergency-relief stage, those allegations are more than sufficient to show at least serious questions going to the merits.

### III. Plaintiffs Face Immediate And Irreparable Harm

The threatened injuries are quintessentially irreparable. Plaintiffs allege imminent housing loss, continuing risk of eviction or displacement, loss or impairment of voucher and relocation rights, denial of access to accessible housing, and deprivation of an accessible hearing process. If Defendants proceed before this Court can adjudicate the claims, Plaintiffs risk losing housing stability and federally protected process in ways that cannot be fully repaired by damages later.

The risk of immediate harm is grounded in the notice sequence itself, including the December 23, 2025 no-cause notices, the December 31, 2025 displacement notice, the February 4, 2026 for-cause notice, and the February 13, 2026 30-Day Notice of Termination addressed to Julio Cortez. See Exhibit C, Exhibit D, Exhibit E, Exhibit H, and Exhibit N.

Because Exhibit N is addressed personally to Julio Cortez and gives a fixed termination date, the record supports immediate injunctive relief even apart from the broader household notice sequence.

Julio Cortez's hearing-access problem is itself an ongoing irreparable injury. A hearing process that exists only on paper, but is unavailable to a person who cannot write and who has already asked orally for a hearing, is not a meaningful process at all.

The same emergency record also shows ongoing uncertainty in voucher and relocation administration because HACC represented on January 8, 2026 that the Tenant Protection Voucher process had not yet started and later reversed a two-bedroom voucher as having been issued in error. See Exhibit G and Exhibit M.

### IV. The Balance Of Equities And Public Interest Favor Relief

The requested relief is narrow. Plaintiffs do not ask the Court to finally decide every housing-placement issue now. They ask the Court to preserve the status quo, stop eviction or retaliatory displacement, preserve voucher and relocation rights, and require HACC to provide an accessible hearing mechanism.

That request is consistent with the existing exhibit record because the preserved documents show both the threat of housing loss and the absence of a completed lawful transition process. See Exhibit E, Exhibit G, Exhibit H, Exhibit M, and Exhibit N.

Any administrative burden on Defendants from complying with federal law is slight compared to the severe hardship Plaintiffs face from losing housing, relocation opportunities, and hearing rights. The public interest likewise favors lawful administration of federally funded housing programs and the prevention of disability discrimination and retaliation.

### V. Security

Plaintiffs request that the Court waive bond under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 65(c), or set nominal security, because Plaintiffs are indigent housing-program participants seeking to prevent unlawful loss of housing and to enforce important public-interest rights.
