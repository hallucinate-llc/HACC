# Race And Retaliation Theory Note

This note summarizes the strongest current pleading posture for the First Amendment retaliation and 42 U.S.C. section 1981 theories in the packet.

## First Amendment Retaliation Theory

The current record supports a standalone First Amendment retaliation theory against HACC under 42 U.S.C. section 1983.

The strongest current support includes:

1. Preserved February 26, March 2, and March 9, 2026 Gmail sent-mail records showing written complaints about race discrimination, service-animal refusal, Blossom non-processing, voucher delay, and unlawful housing administration.
2. Preserved statements that Benjamin Barber had previously indicated to HACC and Quantum an intent to sue under the Civil Rights Act.
3. Escalated county-side communications reaching county personnel and county counsel context.
4. The chronology showing that notices, documentation escalation, voucher delay, and housing obstruction continued after those complaints.

The safer causation theory is that the preserved complaint sequence, privacy-accommodation dispute, and threatened lawsuits support an inference of retaliatory motive. The current packet does not preserve a clean written admission that threatened lawsuits alone caused a specific 30-day notice.

## 42 U.S.C. Section 1981 Theory

The current record also supports a race-discrimination-in-contracting theory against Quantum.

The strongest current support includes:

1. Plaintiffs' December 2025 paper Blossom application and the allegation that Quantum accepted it in person.
2. The later allegation that Quantum never transmitted or processed the application.
3. Preserved February 26, March 2, and March 9, 2026 complaints stating that Blossom had refused to process the application for about two months and had engaged in race discrimination.
4. The allegation that other displaced households were moved into Blossom while plaintiffs' application did not move forward.

The safer section 1981 theory is that Quantum impaired plaintiffs' opportunity to make and enforce a housing-related contract on unequal racial terms through the Blossom intake and placement process.

## Quantum Former-Employee Review Material

The packet includes former-employee review material referencing reported retaliation after fair-housing complaints, use of "woke" terminology, and DEI-focused culture.

That material is best used as:

1. Context for why plaintiffs believed race discrimination was occurring.
2. Notice and motive background that plaintiffs themselves communicated during the dispute.
3. Corroborating context for the section 1981 and retaliation theories.

That material should not be framed as a direct corporate admission by Quantum.

## SHS, RLRA, H4S, And County Policy Materials

The strongest current county-policy use is motive, context, and program-structure evidence.

Current official SHS and RLRA materials preserved in the workspace state that the programs:

1. Are rooted in commitments to lead with racial equity.
2. Especially meet the needs of Black, Indigenous and people of color who are described as disproportionately impacted by housing instability and homelessness.
3. Use equity criteria to inform program design and evaluate racially equitable outcomes.
4. Require some landlord and service-partner participants to follow anti-racism and non-discrimination guidelines.

The safer theory is not that Oregon law or county law lawfully required race discrimination against plaintiffs.

The safer theory is that defendants acted within a county-linked policy environment that expressly centered racial-equity objectives while the county's own published civil-rights materials simultaneously stated that the same programs must comply with nondiscrimination law. Plaintiffs can therefore use those materials as context for motive, notice, program structure, and contracting environment, while still alleging that defendants intentionally denied plaintiffs equal housing or contracting access because of race.

## Filing Caution

Avoid the following overstatements unless additional direct evidence is added:

1. Do not state that Oregon law or Clackamas County law required race discrimination.
2. Do not describe the SHS materials as imposing a racial quota unless a specific quota text tied to race is preserved and cited.
3. Do not treat the former-employee review material as a direct Quantum admission.
4. Do not claim the county policy materials alone prove intentional discrimination; use them together with the preserved application non-processing, race-complaint emails, and contracting chronology.
