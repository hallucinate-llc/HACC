# Declaration Of Benjamin Barber In Support Of Emergency Motion

IN THE UNITED STATES DISTRICT COURT
FOR THE DISTRICT OF OREGON

Benjamin Barber, Jane Cortez, and Julio Cortez,
Plaintiffs,

v.

Housing Authority of Clackamas County; Quantum Residential; and Does 1-10,
Defendants.

I, Benjamin Barber, declare as follows:

1. I am a plaintiff in this action. I make this declaration from personal knowledge. If called as a witness, I could and would testify competently to the matters stated here.
2. I live with members of my household affected by the events described in the complaint, including Jane Cortez and Julio Cortez.
3. Our household has been dealing with displacement, voucher delays, accommodation problems, and hearing-access problems connected to HACC and the Blossom relocation process.
4. HACC issued notices in late 2025 and early 2026 that put our housing stability at risk. The notice sequence includes the December 23, 2025 no-cause notices, the December 31, 2025 displacement notice, the February 4, 2026 for-cause notice, and a February 13, 2026 30-Day Notice of Termination addressed directly to Julio Cortez and effective March 19, 2026. Exhibit C, Exhibit D, Exhibit E, Exhibit H, Exhibit N.
5. Instead of lawfully handling the requested bifurcation process involving Julio Cortez, HACC effectively removed or excluded him from the tenancy and treated him as evicted. The February 13, 2026 notice addressed directly to Julio Cortez is consistent with that treatment. Exhibit N.
6. Julio Cortez cannot write in any language.
7. Julio Cortez requested a hearing orally.
8. HACC told Julio Cortez that he could request a hearing in writing.
9. HACC did not provide Julio Cortez a hearing after his oral request.
10. HACC also did not provide an oral, assisted, or otherwise accessible way for Julio Cortez to invoke his hearing rights.
11. Because HACC required a written request that Julio Cortez could not make, the hearing process was not meaningfully available to him.
12. I understand from HACC's own materials and communications that hearing rights and participant-dispute procedures should exist for housing-program decisions affecting a resident's rights, obligations, welfare, or status.
13. Our household also sought relocation and voucher relief through the Tenant Protection Voucher and Housing Choice Voucher processes.
14. HACC's January 8, 2026 Blossom notice stated that the Tenant Protection Voucher process had not yet started and instructed the household to contact Ashley Ferron. Exhibit G.
15. By February 26, 2026, I was already emailing Kati Tilton, Ashley Ferron, and `charity@magikcorp.com` about the unprocessed Blossom application, service-animal issues, discrimination, and ongoing documentation barriers. Exhibit R.
16. The preserved Gmail sent-mail records show Ashley Ferron remained included on those written complaints through March 10, 2026, and by March 17 through March 20, 2026 Kati Tilton was sending the orientation and voucher emails. Exhibit R, Exhibit J, and Exhibit M.
17. From my personal knowledge, our household had already been seeking relocation and voucher help before those March 2026 orientation emails.
18. During the same general period, our household sought transfer or placement at Blossom Apartments.
19. A paper application for Blossom was submitted in December 2025.
20. In preserved Gmail sent-mail records dated February 26, March 2, and March 9, 2026, I wrote that Blossom had refused to process applications for about two months, refused to house a service animal, and engaged in race discrimination, while HACC and Quantum were still delaying the steps needed to leave HACC-controlled housing. Exhibit R.
21. HACC and Quantum did not provide a prompt resolution after those complaints.
22. HACC also imposed repeated and escalating documentation demands.
23. The February 9, 2026 additional-information demand and the related email thread required extensive tax, bank, asset, and business documentation. Exhibit I and Exhibit J.
24. Those demands continued even after documents had already been provided.
25. In my experience, those requests delayed relocation and voucher progress rather than resolving it.
26. The household also sought a two-bedroom accommodation.
27. Our household needs included mobility, cognitive, caregiving, privacy, remote-work, scooter, and service-animal concerns that made a one-bedroom arrangement inadequate.
28. HACC first sent a two-bedroom subsidy worksheet and voucher issuance, then stated on March 19, 2026 that the two-bedroom voucher had been created and sent in error and that a one-bedroom voucher would be issued instead. Exhibit M.
29. I responded that I had a scheduled appointment to obtain a reasonable accommodation for a two-bedroom unit and that the one-bedroom arrangement was not workable.
30. On March 20, 2026, HACC stated that the voucher could be reissued as a two-bedroom if the accommodation was approved. Exhibit M.
31. Even then, the accommodation and housing process remained delayed and uncertain.
32. I also complained about discrimination and threatened legal action.
33. According to what was said to me and reflected in the intake information, HACC personnel linked threatened lawsuits or complaints to the issuance of a 30-day notice.
34. That sequence made me believe the household was being retaliated against for complaining and asking for help.
35. Without immediate court intervention, I believe our household faces continuing risk of eviction, displacement, loss of relocation opportunity, loss or impairment of voucher benefits, and ongoing denial of an accessible hearing for Julio Cortez.
36. Loss of housing or relocation opportunity would cause immediate and severe harm to the household.
37. If Julio Cortez is denied a meaningful hearing because he cannot comply with a writing requirement, that denial cannot be fully repaired after the fact.
38. The relief requested in the motion would preserve the status quo and require Defendants to provide lawful and accessible process while the Court considers the merits.
