## Nature Of Action

1. This is a civil action arising from housing discrimination, refusal to accommodate disability-related housing needs, retaliation for protected complaints, interference with fair-housing rights, denial of federally required notice and hearing protections, and related Oregon housing-law violations.
2. Plaintiffs seek emergency and permanent relief to prevent eviction and housing loss, require lawful voucher and accommodation processing, and recover damages caused by Defendants' notice practices, application failures, documentation barriers, and retaliatory conduct.
3. The dispute centers on HACC's public-housing, displacement, Tenant Protection Voucher, and Housing Choice Voucher administration; the handling of Plaintiffs' relocation from HACC-controlled housing; and the failure to process or honor Plaintiffs' transfer and accommodation requests connected to Blossom Apartments, a HACC-linked relocation destination managed by Quantum Residential.

## Jurisdiction And Venue

4. This Court has federal-question jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. section 1331 because Counts I through III arise under the Fair Housing Act, 42 U.S.C. sections 3601 through 3619, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. section 794, Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act, 42 U.S.C. section 12132, 42 U.S.C. section 1983, 42 U.S.C. section 1437d(k), and 24 C.F.R. section 982.555.
5. This Court also has jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. section 1343(a)(3) and (4) because Plaintiffs seek redress for deprivations of federal civil-rights protections under color of state law.
6. This Court has supplemental jurisdiction over Counts IV and V under 28 U.S.C. section 1367 because those claims arise from the same nucleus of operative facts.
7. Venue is proper in this District under 28 U.S.C. section 1391(b) because the housing unit, the notices, the voucher administration, the application events, and the principal injuries all occurred in Clackamas County, Oregon, within the District of Oregon.
8. Plaintiffs also seek declaratory and further relief under 28 U.S.C. sections 2201 and 2202.

## Authorities Incorporated Into This Pleading

47. The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in the terms, conditions, privileges, services, or facilities connected to a dwelling because of disability and requires reasonable accommodations in rules, policies, practices, or services when necessary to afford equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling. 42 U.S.C. section 3604(f)(2) and (3)(B).
48. The Fair Housing Act also prohibits coercion, intimidation, threats, and interference with persons who exercise or aid fair-housing rights. 42 U.S.C. section 3617; 24 C.F.R. section 100.400.
49. Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act prohibits disability discrimination by programs or activities receiving federal financial assistance. 29 U.S.C. section 794.
50. Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act prohibits disability discrimination by public entities in services, programs, and activities. 42 U.S.C. section 12132.
51. Federal housing-program law requires notice and an opportunity for an informal hearing in covered participant disputes. 42 U.S.C. section 1437d(k); 24 C.F.R. section 982.555.
52. Oregon law independently prohibits disability discrimination and source-of-income discrimination in housing transactions and authorizes civil remedies. ORS 659A.145; ORS 659A.421; ORS 659A.425; ORS 659A.885.
53. Oregon law and HACC policy also govern the notice and termination process implicated by the challenged displacement and lease actions. ORS 90.427; HACC ACOP 13-IV.D.
54. Case law supporting these theories includes Giebeler v. M & B Associates, 343 F.3d 1143 (9th Cir. 2003), Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970), Basham v. Freda, 805 F. Supp. 930 (M.D. Fla. 1992), and Hayward v. Brown, No. 8:15-cv-03381 (D. Md. 2016), among other authorities identified in the accompanying authority map.

## Count I
## Failure To Provide Reasonable Accommodation And Disability Discrimination
## Against HACC And Quantum Under The Fair Housing Act, And Against HACC Only Under Section 504 And Title II

55. Plaintiffs reallege paragraphs 1 through 54.
56. Plaintiffs are persons with disabilities or are associated with persons with disabilities within the meaning of the Fair Housing Act, Section 504, and Title II.
57. HACC administered the relevant housing, voucher, relocation, hearing, and accommodation processes as a public entity and as a recipient of federal housing funds.
58. Quantum participated in the housing placement or application process for Blossom, a HACC-linked relocation option represented as available to displaced households through HACC-administered processing.
59. Plaintiffs requested a two-bedroom accommodation and related housing relief because Jane Cortez required mobility and cognitive accommodations, Benjamin Barber served as a live-in caregiver and remote worker, the household needed room for a scooter and service animal, and the one-bedroom arrangement was not effective or dignified.
60. Plaintiffs further allege that Julio Cortez required an accessible means of requesting a hearing because he could not write in any language and therefore could not use a hearing-request procedure conditioned on written submission.
61. HACC acknowledged in writing that the voucher could be reissued as a two-bedroom if the accommodation were approved, but had already reversed a two-bedroom issuance and replaced it with a one-bedroom issuance it claimed had been sent in error. Exhibit M.
62. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to provide a prompt, interactive, and lawful accommodation process; denied or delayed an effective accommodation; and failed to process or honor the housing opportunity in a manner that afforded equal use and enjoyment of the dwelling and housing program.
63. Plaintiffs further allege that Quantum refused to process the Blossom application and refused to accommodate the household's service animal, thereby participating in disability-based housing discrimination under the Fair Housing Act.
64. HACC's conduct violated 42 U.S.C. section 3604(f)(2) and (3)(B), 29 U.S.C. section 794, and 42 U.S.C. section 12132, including by failing to offer Julio Cortez a meaningful and accessible means to invoke hearing rights connected to continued housing and relocation services.
65. Quantum's conduct violated 42 U.S.C. section 3604(f)(2) and (3)(B).
66. Plaintiffs are entitled to declaratory relief, injunctive relief, compensatory damages, and all other available remedies on this count.

## Count II
## Fair Housing Retaliation And Interference
## Against HACC And Quantum Under 42 U.S.C. Section 3617 And 24 C.F.R. Section 100.400

67. Plaintiffs reallege paragraphs 1 through 66.
68. Plaintiffs engaged in protected activity by complaining about race discrimination, disability discrimination, service-animal issues, unlawful housing administration, retaliation, and violations of fair-housing rights to HACC and Quantum. Plaintiffs further allege from the completed intake session that related complaints also reached county counsel.
69. Defendants knew of those complaints.
70. After those complaints, Defendants escalated adverse conduct by issuing or maintaining notices to vacate, delaying voucher issuance, reversing a two-bedroom voucher, demanding excessive documentation, refusing to process the Blossom application, and blocking or burdening relocation out of HACC-controlled housing.
71. Plaintiffs further allege, based on the completed intake session, that HACC personnel explicitly stated that Benjamin Barber's threatened lawsuits were a reason for giving a 30-day notice to vacate, and that county counsel was later informed that plaintiffs contended the notice was unlawful.
72. Plaintiffs allege that the notice sequence, documentation barriers, application failures, accommodation delays, and inaccessible hearing-request requirement imposed on Julio Cortez were intended to coerce, intimidate, punish, or interfere with protected fair-housing activity.
73. The challenged conduct violated 42 U.S.C. section 3617 and 24 C.F.R. section 100.400.
74. Plaintiffs are entitled to declaratory relief, injunctive relief, compensatory damages, punitive damages where authorized, and all other available remedies on this count.

## Count III
## Procedural Due Process And Federal Hearing Violations
## Against HACC Under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983, 42 U.S.C. Section 1437d(k), And 24 C.F.R. Section 982.555

75. Plaintiffs reallege paragraphs 1 through 74.
76. HACC acted under color of state law when administering public-housing, displacement, TPV, PBV, HCV, hearing, and relocation processes.
77. Plaintiffs had constitutionally and statutorily protected interests in continued housing benefits, lawful displacement procedures, and the notice and hearing protections attached to HACC-administered housing assistance.
78. HACC issued or maintained adverse notices and housing-administration decisions affecting Plaintiffs' rights, obligations, welfare, or status without providing the clear, timely, and meaningful hearing process required by federal law and HACC's own administrative materials.
79. HACC's program materials themselves reference informal-hearing procedures under 24 C.F.R. section 982.555 for participant disputes and acknowledge hearing rights tied to owner actions affecting resident rights, obligations, welfare, or status.
80. Plaintiffs allege that HACC conditioned Julio Cortez's access to a hearing on a written request even though he was incapable of writing in any language and had specifically requested a hearing orally.
81. Plaintiffs allege that no meaningful hearing or appeal was scheduled before the threatened loss of housing and that HACC failed to provide sufficient notice, document access, hearing access, accommodation in the hearing-request process, or a timely decision process.
82. This conduct violated the Fourteenth Amendment, actionable under 42 U.S.C. section 1983, together with 42 U.S.C. section 1437d(k) and 24 C.F.R. section 982.555.
83. Plaintiffs are entitled to declaratory relief, temporary and permanent injunctive relief, compensatory damages, attorney fees under 42 U.S.C. section 1988 where applicable, and all other available remedies on this count.
