## II. This Eviction Is Intertwined With An Uncompleted Section 18 Relocation Process.

The record assembled in this matter supports that HACC triggered formal redevelopment-related relocation duties no later than September 19, 2024, when the Phase II notice issued. Those duties are not merely asserted here. Section 18 itself required HACC to provide displacement notice, offer comparable housing, pay actual and reasonable relocation expenses, provide necessary counseling, and refrain from completing displacement before residents were actually relocated. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d). HUD's implementing rule likewise requires comparable replacement housing, relocation assistance, and related notice-and-counseling steps in the redevelopment process. See 24 C.F.R. § 970.21. HACC's own materials treated relocation counseling, replacement housing, and relocation assistance as part of the operative process. HACC's later notices show that displacement pressure continued into late 2025 and early 2026. Yet the replacement-housing path remained incomplete through the same period.

Once those duties were triggered, HACC was not merely expected to perform them in a general sense. HACC had to perform the required relocation steps before treating displacement as complete, and the present record therefore supports withholding possession while counseling, comparable replacement housing, relocation-expense support, portability / voucher administration, and related relocation performance remained materially unfinished. In encoded form, the present record supports `O(HACC, perform the triggered relocation steps)` and `F(HACC, treat displacement as complete before actual relocation)`. See [housing_duties_fact_law_encoding_2026-04-09.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Collateral%20Estoppel/evidence_notes/housing_duties_fact_law_encoding_2026-04-09.md).

That formulation is not presented here as a bare conclusion. It is grounded in the statute, regulation, Administrative Plan excerpts, Blossom lease-up materials, and HACC's own January-March 2026 processing communications. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; Ex. B (binder pp. 3-7); Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122); Ex. Y (binder pp. 156-159); Ex. Z (binder pp. 160-163).

The strongest current record points are:

1. HACC's Phase II redevelopment and relocation notice.
2. The December 31, 2025 displacement notice.
3. The January 8, 2026 Blossom / Tenant Protection Voucher notice directing the household to Ashley Ferron to begin that process.
4. HACC's related communications directing that the packet be returned through the Blossom-side or Hillside Manor office intake route if the household was pursuing that replacement-housing path.
5. HACC's later written acknowledgment that the Blossom intake packet had been submitted to Quantum Residential staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC.
6. The March 2026 voucher and accommodation communications showing that even then, the household was still dealing with voucher issuance, bedroom-size reversal, accommodation review, and unresolved relocation status.

Where the relocation process remained incomplete, possession should not be granted as though the household simply refused to move. On the current record, the stronger and safer formulation is that eviction was premature and inconsistent with unresolved Section 18 and undertaken relocation-process duties while the relocation conditions precedent remained materially unsatisfied.

## II.A. Section 18 And Undertaken Relocation Duties Made Eviction Premature.

The anti-eviction point can also be stated more directly. This is not just a case where federal relocation law existed in the background. It is a case where direct Section 18 relocation duties and HACC's own undertaken replacement-housing process converged.

On the direct federal side, Section 18 and HUD's relocation rule required HACC to provide relocation and replacement-housing assistance as part of the Hillside redevelopment displacement process, including comparable housing, reasonable relocation expenses, necessary counseling, and no completion of displacement until actual relocation occurred. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p and 24 C.F.R. § 970.21. Those are not discretionary aspirations. They are the governing legal framework for relocation tied to demolition and disposition.

On the local process side, HACC's own documents show that HACC adopted and represented a concrete replacement-housing structure. HACC's July 1, 2025 Administrative Plan identifies Hillside Manor as a RAD PBV project, identifies Quantum Residential Property Management as the property management company, states that HACC will manage separate waiting lists for individual RAD PBV projects including Hillside Manor, and states that the owner must provide an opportunity for an informal hearing before eviction. HACC's public Blossom lease-up page states that applications for Blossom and Community Apartments will be processed through HACC's PBV waitlist and that Hillside relocation households are priority groups. HACC's January 8, 2026 notice then told this household it had to choose between moving forward with Blossom and Community Apartments or using a Tenant Protection Voucher.

Once HACC did those things, HACC could not fairly treat the household as though it had simply failed to secure alternate housing on its own without first accounting for the unresolved HACC-directed process. HACC had undertaken a specific replacement-housing process and directed the household into it. HACC then later admitted that the intake packet submitted through the Hillside Manor / Quantum route had not reached HACC. That means the relevant constraint here is supported from both directions:

- federal law required completion of the relocation process before displacement was treated as complete; and
- HACC's own documented process required HACC to administer the Blossom-or-TPV path it directed the household to use rather than convert its own process failure into a claimed eviction default.

That same logic became even clearer on March 26, 2026, when HACC put three relocation options in writing and stated that all of them required a move by April 30, 2026, while also stating that eviction documentation would be filed if the move was not completed. That email matters because it is not merely background narrative. It is a direct record that HACC itself was still treating relocation as an active, unresolved process with three live paths and had still not carried defendants to a signable lease or completed move. Once HACC did that, the narrower legal consequence was that HACC remained responsible for administering those written options coherently, defendants had continued to follow those paths while they were being processed, and the unresolved noncompletion of those same paths should not have been treated as ordinary eviction default on the present record. Exhibit AA (binder pp. 164-167).

That formulation is supported by the statute, regulation, Administrative Plan excerpts, Blossom lease-up materials, HACC's January 8, 2026 notice, HACC's March 26, 2026 options email, and HACC's later packet-routing acknowledgment. See 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; Ex. B (binder pp. 3-7); Ex. G (binder pp. 23-26); Ex. V (binder pp. 94-122); Ex. Y (binder pp. 156-159); Ex. Z (binder pp. 160-163); Ex. AA (binder pp. 164-167). On the present record, that is why eviction was at minimum premature and inconsistent with HACC's unresolved relocation obligations.

That same record also supports the household-side participation premise. The point is now summarized in [household_submission_and_participation_chronology_2026-04-09.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Collateral%20Estoppel/evidence_notes/household_submission_and_participation_chronology_2026-04-09.md), and the corresponding primary-source ladder is summarized in [housing_primary_source_record_ladder_2026-04-09.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Collateral%20Estoppel/evidence_notes/housing_primary_source_record_ladder_2026-04-09.md). The present argument therefore does not depend on a theory that defendants sat idle while HACC worked. It depends on the opposite theory: defendants kept participating while HACC-directed paths remained unresolved.

## III. The Blossom And Waterleaf Paths Show That The Housing Transition Was Still In Progress.

The record further shows that defendants were actively pursuing replacement-housing paths while Plaintiff was still moving toward eviction.

On the Hillside Park replacement-housing / Blossom side:

1. Defendants were directed into the Hillside Park redevelopment replacement-housing path, including the Blossom / Hillside Manor office intake route.
2. Defendants submitted a paper application packet in December 2025 through that route.
3. Benjamin Barber later complained in writing that Blossom had refused to process the application and had refused to house a service animal.
4. On February 2, 2026, Benjamin Barber emailed both Ashley Ferron and `blossom@quantumres.com` stating that he had met a Black couple with one child at Balfour Park who had just been granted Section 8 and were interviewing for Blossom while defendants' own application had not been sent onward to HACC.
5. HACC later acknowledged in writing that the packet had been submitted to Quantum staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been transmitted to HACC.

On the Waterleaf side:

1. The Waterleaf application record shows a created date of December 23, 2025.
2. HACC's own correspondence shows that by January 12, 2026 Waterleaf wanted the voucher amount and that the application had already been signed and turned in.

On this record, that path stretched across four calendar months, beginning with the December 23, 2025 Waterleaf application, continuing through HACC's January 12 and January 26 portability communications, and still remaining incomplete in late March 2026 when Waterleaf was waiting on voucher issuance. By early April 2026, the same alternate route still had not produced a completed move. The record then became even more concrete: after the March 19 voucher marker, Benjamin submitted a Parkside Heights application on March 29, 2026, copied Kati Tilton and HACC Relocation, and Parkside Heights replied on March 30 that the two-bedroom units were still under construction with estimated occupancy at the end of April. Read together with HACC's March 26 options email requiring a move by April 30, that Parkside exchange shows that the household was still being pushed through the same unresolved April 30 move-out framework rather than having reached a completed relocation. ## IV. The Accommodation Path Was Still Unresolved During The Same Time Period.

The same record shows that disability-related housing needs remained unresolved while Plaintiff was pressing toward eviction. The duty being invoked here is likewise source-linked: the Fair Housing Act and its regulations require a housing provider to make reasonable accommodations in rules, policies, practices, or services when necessary to afford a person with a disability equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling. 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B); 24 C.F.R. § 100.204.

The preserved accommodation record supports that:

1. Jane Cortez had disability-related mobility and support needs.
2. A two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement was requested as necessary for equal housing access.
3. Provider verification was completed by March 24, 2026.
4. HACC nevertheless denied the two-bedroom increase on March 26, 2026 while stating that a one-bedroom voucher could be used for a two-bedroom ground-floor unit within the affordability range.

That is not a record of completed relocation. It is a record of an unresolved and partially denied accommodation path during the same period possession was being pursued.

## V. Quantum Is Material To The Court's Interim Relief Analysis Even If Formal Joinder Is Handled Separately.

Even if the Court reserves any joinder ruling, Quantum remains central to the merits and to interim relief because HACC itself wrote that the packet for this Hillside Park replacement-housing path had been submitted to Quantum staff at the Hillside Manor leasing office but had not been provided to HACC. Defendants' request for interim relief is therefore not asking the Court to supervise a stranger to the case. It is asking the Court to preserve a housing-processing chain that HACC's own records already place partly on the Quantum side. The narrower encoded theory is not that Section 18 directly imposed the full HACC duty chain on Quantum; it is that if Quantum received or controlled the intake path, Quantum had project-side duties not to lose, block, or discriminatorily mishandle it. To the extent more granular intake, forwarding, or hold-status records exist for that chain, those records are within HACC's or Quantum's control and support preservation, disclosure, and proper party-structure treatment rather than a presumption that the chain was functioning correctly. ## VI. The Record Also Supports That Time Was Wasted While HACC Was Acting In Plain Tension With Governing Law.

The delay here was not merely unfortunate timing. The current record supports the stronger inference that substantial time was wasted while HACC was acting in ways materially out of alignment with the law governing this household's situation.

First, the record supports a VAWA-related safety-transfer problem. HACC knew the household was invoking domestic-violence-related safety concerns, restraining-order issues, and out-of-county transfer efforts, yet the Waterleaf / Multnomah path was allowed to drift for months while eviction pressure continued. Even if VAWA does not impose a short fixed numeric deadline for a completed port-out, it does impose concrete duties that are directly linked to source text: the PHA must maintain and use an emergency transfer plan, 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009, must protect confidentiality of survivor-status and transfer information, 24 C.F.R. § 5.2007(c), and when notified of a protection order must comply with that order in access and control decisions, 34 U.S.C. § 12491(b)(3)(C)(i).

Second, the record supports restrained-party and confidentiality concerns involving Solomon and related household-status communications. Defendants' position is not merely that Solomon existed in the background. It is that HACC was on notice of court-order and household-safety issues, yet still handled lease, relocation, and household-status matters in a way that appears to have consumed time, created confusion, and potentially allowed outside pressure to distort the process instead of resolving it lawfully.

Third, the record supports ongoing confusion and inconsistency about Julio's household status and process rights. Rather than cleanly handling bifurcation, hearing access, and household-composition consequences, HACC appears to have spent months acting inconsistently about who was in the household, what process applied, and what the resulting bedroom size, voucher, and relocation consequences should be. The duties invoked here are not free-floating: VAWA's bifurcation rule appears at 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009(a)-(b), and hearing/process rights for adverse housing action appear at 42 U.S.C. § 1437d(k) and 24 C.F.R. § 982.555.

Fourth, the record supports prolonged mishandling of disability and live-in-aide issues. The household was not simply seeking a larger unit as a preference. The record reflects disability-related mobility limits, caregiver needs, privacy needs, and service-animal needs, together with provider verification and live-in-aide-type facts that should have narrowed and clarified the housing and income-review questions. Instead, HACC appears to have spent substantial time reversing bedroom sizing, delaying accommodation review, and imposing broad document demands even while the household was asserting a caregiver or live-in-aide role that should have changed how parts of the income-review process were handled. The source-linked duties there include the FHA accommodation duty, 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B); 24 C.F.R. § 100.204, and HUD's live-in-aide treatment rules under 24 C.F.R. §§ 5.403 and 5.609 plus PIH Notice 2013-04, which distinguish a live-in aide from a regular income-counted family member.

Fifth, the record also includes discrimination complaints and comparator concerns that further undermine the idea that this was a wholly routine and neutral housing process. The strongest present points are that Benjamin Barber complained in writing that Blossom was engaging in race discrimination and refusing to process the application, and that on February 2, 2026 he specifically reported meeting a Black family that had just received Section 8 and was interviewing for Blossom while defendants' own application had not been sent onward to HACC. Even if those discrimination issues are not fully adjudicated in the eviction case itself, they reinforce the narrower conclusion that the Court should not treat the present delay-and-displacement sequence as a routine neutral process entitled to possession as a matter of course.

In practical terms, the Court can view this as a period in which the household was losing time, housing stability, and viable relocation opportunities while HACC was still not performing the basic lawful things it needed to do under identified legal and record sources: complete Section 18 relocation before displacement, 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d), 24 C.F.R. § 970.21; process the transfer path coherently under VAWA and portability rules, 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009 and 24 C.F.R. § 982.355; carry out the replacement-housing application-routing path HACC itself directed in its January 8, 2026 notice and later described in its written acknowledgment that the packet had been left with Quantum at Hillside Manor but had not reached HACC; handle household-composition and safety issues lawfully under VAWA bifurcation and court-order provisions, 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009 and 34 U.S.C. § 12491(b)(3)(C)(i); and recognize and process disability and live-in-aide issues correctly under the FHA and HUD live-in-aide rules, 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B), 24 C.F.R. § 100.204, 24 C.F.R. §§ 5.403 and 5.609. The discrimination-related points remain part of the broader context, but the narrower present conclusion is that possession should not be granted on this unresolved record.

## VII. The Court Has Ample Ground To Deny Possession Or Enter Narrow Interim Relief.

The Court need not decide every federal claim to rule correctly on the present eviction motion. The narrow point is enough:

1. HACC triggered relocation duties.
2. HACC directed defendants into a replacement-housing and voucher path.
3. That path remained incomplete.
4. A substantial amount of the delay was traceable to HACC-side administration.
5. HACC nevertheless pursued eviction.

On that record, the Court should deny possession as premature. More precisely, the current Section 18 and contract record supports withholding possession while the triggered relocation duties and the undertaken relocation-process commitments remained materially incomplete. At minimum, the Court should stay or abate possession and enter narrow interim orders preserving the application, transfer, relocation, and accommodation path, consistent with the law-first and encoded duty map preserved in [law_first_hacc_quantum_duty_memo_2026-04-09.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Collateral%20Estoppel/evidence_notes/law_first_hacc_quantum_duty_memo_2026-04-09.md) and [housing_duties_fact_law_encoding_2026-04-09.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Collateral%20Estoppel/evidence_notes/housing_duties_fact_law_encoding_2026-04-09.md).

## VIII. If The Court Considers Equitable Relief, It Should Preserve The Subject Matter Rather Than Award Final Relief.

Defendants do not ask the Court at this stage to compel the immediate award of a specific apartment. The narrower and safer request is to preserve the subject matter:

1. no possession while relocation remains incomplete;
2. no treatment of relocation as complete unless lawful comparable housing is actually provided;
3. no destruction or frustration of the Blossom intake and related replacement-housing paperwork;
4. a written status statement within 7 days; and
5. continued processing of the transfer, voucher, and accommodation path.

That relief is consistent with preserving the possibility of final equitable relief and preventing the case from becoming moot through housing loss before adjudication.

## IX. Source-Linked Duty Matrix

The motion relies on the following duties because each one is tied to an identified source, not merely asserted:

1. Section 18 relocation completion duty.
   Source: 42 U.S.C. § 1437p(d); 24 C.F.R. § 970.21.
   Why it matters here: HACC triggered redevelopment relocation, so displacement and any possession request should not be treated as routine while comparable replacement-housing, reasonable relocation expenses, necessary counseling, and actual relocation itself remained incomplete.

2. HACC hearing and adverse-action notice duty.
   Source: 42 U.S.C. § 1437d(k); 24 C.F.R. § 982.555.
   Why it matters here: when HACC changed household status, voucher size, or assistance-related conditions, it had to provide reasoned notice and a hearing path rather than operate through unexplained reversals.

3. VAWA emergency-transfer administration duty.
   Source: 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009; HACC Administrative Plan (effective 7/1/2025) emergency-transfer passages; HUD HCV Fair Housing Guidebook, Apr. 2025, at 12-13.
   Why it matters here: once HACC was processing a safety-related out-of-county move, it had to administer that transfer path lawfully and coherently rather than leave it unresolved while eviction pressure continued.

4. VAWA confidentiality and court-order compliance duties.
   Source: 24 C.F.R. § 5.2007(c); 34 U.S.C. § 12491(b)(3)(C)(i).
   Why it matters here: once HACC was on notice of Solomon-related protection-order issues, it had to preserve confidentiality and comply with court-order constraints in access and household-control decisions.

5. VAWA bifurcation and household-protection duty.
   Source: 24 C.F.R. § 5.2009(a)-(b).
   Why it matters here: HACC was not free to handle Julio- or perpetrator-related household changes in a way that stripped remaining lawful occupants of protected rights without using the lawful bifurcation framework.

6. Fair Housing Act accommodation duty.
   Source: 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B); 24 C.F.R. § 100.204.
   Why it matters here: the requested first-floor / bedroom-size / disability-related housing modifications had to be addressed as accommodations necessary for equal housing opportunity, not treated as optional preferences.

7. HUD live-in-aide treatment rules.
   Source: 24 C.F.R. §§ 5.403 and 5.609; PIH Notice 2013-04.
   Why it matters here: once caregiver or live-in-aide facts were being asserted, HACC had to apply the correct status and income-treatment rules rather than default to broad ordinary-household income review.

8. FHA anti-interference and anti-retaliation duty.
   Source: 42 U.S.C. § 3617; 24 C.F.R. § 100.400.
   Why it matters here: neither HACC nor Quantum could lawfully obstruct, interfere with, or retaliate against the household for pursuing housing rights, accommodations, or fair-housing complaints in the same housing path, although the present motion does not depend on full adjudication of every discrimination sub-theory.

9. Section 1981 contracting equality duty.
   Source: 42 U.S.C. § 1981.
   Why it matters here: Quantum could not impair the household's Blossom contracting opportunity on unequal racial terms, but on the present record this remains a reinforcing rather than indispensable branch of the eviction argument.

10. Undertaken intake-routing and application-processing obligation.
   Source: HACC's January 8, 2026 Blossom / TPV notice directing the household into the replacement-housing path, and HACC's later written acknowledgment that the packet had been submitted to Quantum staff at the Hillside Manor office but had not been provided to HACC.
   Why it matters here: this is not framed as a free-floating statute-only duty; it is a concrete processing obligation HACC itself undertook and then failed to carry through in the same relocation chain.

## X. Conclusion

The Court should deny Plaintiff's eviction request. In the alternative, the Court should stay or abate possession and enter the narrow interim protections requested in defendants' cross-motion.
