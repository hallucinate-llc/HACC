## 1. Core Theme

The short version is:

- HACC triggered relocation duties, did not complete them, and then moved toward eviction anyway.

## 2. Very Short Opening

Your Honor, this case should not be treated like an ordinary holdover where the household simply failed to move. HACC's own records show that the Phase II redevelopment notice triggered relocation duties, and the current record also shows that when HACC pursued eviction, relocation was still incomplete. The household still lacked comparable housing, relocation expenses, and counseling. HACC should not obtain possession while those relocation preconditions remained unmet.

## 4. Best Bench Narrative

The Court can treat this as a simple sequence:

1. HACC triggered formal redevelopment and relocation duties.
2. Those duties included a workable relocation path before displacement was completed.
3. The relocation path remained incomplete because the household still lacked a workable unit, timely voucher completion, and related relocation support.
4. HACC nevertheless pursued eviction while those same relocation issues were still unresolved.
5. HACC therefore seeks to benefit from an incomplete relocation process that its own records show was not finished.

## 5. Best Legal Framing

If the Court asks for the legal point, the answer is:

- once relocation duties were triggered, HACC could not lawfully move to displacement before the required relocation path was actually completed;
- where comparable housing, counseling, relocation expenses, and accommodation issues were still unresolved, the eviction was premature;
- and that is especially true where the same record also shows packet obstruction, accommodation delay, and inaccessible replacement problems.

## 6. Best One-Sentence Ask

The Court should deny possession because HACC pursued eviction before completing the relocation process it had already triggered.

## 7. If The Court Pushes Back

If the Court asks `but wasn't HACC still trying to work through relocation`, the answer is:

- yes, and that is exactly the point;
- if relocation was still being worked through, then it was not complete, and HACC should not have been simultaneously using the incomplete process as the basis to remove the household.

## 8. Short Closing

The central fairness problem is that HACC triggered relocation obligations, did not complete them, and then tried to shift the consequences of that incomplete process onto the household. The eviction should not move forward on that record.
