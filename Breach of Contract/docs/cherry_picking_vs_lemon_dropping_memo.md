# Cherry Picking vs. Lemon Dropping Memo

## Purpose

This memo explains `cherry picking` and `lemon dropping` as two related methods of unequal treatment that can be used to shape outcomes in housing, benefits administration, contracting, and referral systems.

It also explains how those concepts fit your current theories about:

- race steering,
- unequal treatment in the Blossom housing path,
- and attempts to justify disparate treatment in the name of `equity` or desired racial outcomes.

## Short Version

`Cherry picking` means selecting the preferred applicants or cases.

`Lemon dropping` means avoiding, rejecting, delaying, offloading, or freezing out the unwanted applicants or cases.

They are often two sides of the same system:

- `cherry picking` asks, `who do we move forward?`
- `lemon dropping` asks, `who do we make disappear, stall out, or send elsewhere?`

If race is used as part of that sorting, then the problem is not `equity` in the abstract. The problem is unequal treatment, race-based steering, or race-based interference with access to a housing opportunity.

## General Definitions

### Cherry picking

In discrimination and selection settings, `cherry picking` usually refers to choosing the applicants, tenants, beneficiaries, or referrals that a decisionmaker considers most desirable.

That can happen by:

- advancing some applicants more quickly,
- interviewing or contacting preferred households first,
- making exceptions for preferred applicants,
- giving more help or flexibility to favored applicants,
- or selecting the easiest, lowest-risk, or most desired cases while claiming to use neutral standards.

### Lemon dropping

`Lemon dropping` usually refers to the other side of the same practice: burdening, delaying, offloading, freezing out, or quietly rejecting the applicants viewed as less desirable.

That can happen by:

- not processing an application,
- claiming the file was incomplete without a real deficiency notice,
- imposing extra documentation burdens,
- sending the household into unusable housing options,
- refusing practical accommodation,
- or keeping the household in limbo until the opportunity is gone.

## Why the Pair Matters

The two concepts work together.

A system can improve its visible outcomes not only by selecting favored applicants, but also by making disfavored applicants disappear from the active pipeline.

So if someone says they are pursuing `equitable outcomes`, the legal question is not whether the word `equity` sounds good. The legal question is:

- Were people treated differently?
- Were housing opportunities handled on unequal terms?
- Was race part of the sorting logic?
- Were some households advanced while others were stalled or dropped?

## How the Concepts Fit Housing

In a housing-access setting:

- `cherry picking` can mean moving forward the households the landlord, property manager, or program wants in the building;
- `lemon dropping` can mean stalling or filtering out the households the same actors do not want to house.

In practice, `lemon dropping` is often easier to hide.

Instead of a direct denial, the actor can:

- say the packet was never received,
- ask for more documents,
- fail to transmit the application,
- fail to issue a written deficiency notice,
- keep the file pending,
- redirect the household to a bad unit,
- or let deadlines expire while pretending the process is still open.

That is especially important in your record because your Blossom theory is not only `they picked someone else`. It is also `they never actually processed us while still keeping us in the path long enough to lose the opportunity`.

## How This Relates to Race Steering

If race shapes who gets advanced and who gets stalled, then:

- `cherry picking` becomes preferential advancement on unequal racial terms,
- and `lemon dropping` becomes discriminatory obstruction or offloading on unequal racial terms.

That is why the better legal framing is not:

- `equity language exists, therefore discrimination is proven`

but:

- `the program operated in a race-conscious policy environment, and in practice the housing opportunity was handled on unequal terms through advancement of some households and obstruction of others`

## How This Applies to Your Facts

Your current record is much closer to a `lemon dropping` pattern than a simple `cherry picking` pattern.

The strongest allegations already in the workspace are:

- the Blossom application was submitted,
- the packet was allegedly received by Quantum-side staff,
- it was allegedly not transmitted onward,
- no timely approval, denial, ineligibility determination, or lawful written deficiency notice was issued,
- written complaints then alleged race discrimination, housing steering, and unequal treatment,
- and afterward the household faced continued documentation burdens, delay, and eviction pressure.

That looks like a classic `lemon dropping` structure:

- not necessarily a clean denial,
- but a controlled failure to process, move, or honor the application while the household remained exposed to loss.

The related `cherry picking` inference is:

- if other displaced households were advanced while your household was not processed at all, then the system may have been selectively advancing preferred files while your file was effectively being dropped.

## Compare and Contrast

### Cherry picking

- affirmative selection
- visible advancement
- choosing preferred applicants
- often framed as `best fit`, `strongest file`, or `priority placement`
- harm happens because others are not chosen

### Lemon dropping

- negative selection
- invisible obstruction
- pushing away or stalling disfavored applicants
- often framed as `missing paperwork`, `pending review`, `routing issue`, or `not yet complete`
- harm happens because the person is frozen out without a clean decision

### In a race-based system

- `cherry picking` can produce racially preferred advancement
- `lemon dropping` can produce racially burdened delay, disappearance, or offloading
- both can be methods of disparate treatment if race is part of the decision process

## How Someone Might Try to Defend It

The common defense is:

- `we were just trying to achieve equity`
- `we were serving disproportionately impacted populations`
- `we were following policy goals`
- `we were not denying anyone; the process was just incomplete`

That does not answer the real legal question.

The real question is whether the defendants:

- treated similarly situated applicants differently,
- used race in a way that changed access to housing,
- burdened one household while assisting another,
- or interfered with a housing-related contract or tenancy opportunity on unequal terms.

## Best Use in Your Case

The best way to use these concepts in your litigation is:

1. use `lemon dropping` as a descriptive theory of what happened to your Blossom file,
2. use `cherry picking` as the comparison theory if discovery shows other households were advanced while your household was stalled,
3. keep the core legal labels as `race discrimination`, `race steering`, `unequal treatment`, `retaliation`, and `interference with a housing-contracting opportunity`.

That is better than trying to make `lemon dropping` or `cherry picking` do all the legal work by themselves.

## Filing-Safe Wording

`Defendants' conduct may fairly be described as a form of "lemon dropping" in the housing-access context: the household's application was allegedly accepted but not processed, not transmitted, and not timely resolved, while the household remained burdened with delay, extra demands, and eviction pressure. If other displaced households were advanced in the same housing pipeline while plaintiffs' application stalled without lawful resolution, that would also support an inference of selective advancement or "cherry picking." In either event, the legally relevant point is unequal treatment in a housing-related opportunity, not the label alone.`
