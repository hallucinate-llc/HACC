# Defendant-by-Defendant Federal Exposure and SHS / RLRA Equity Memo

## Bottom Line

The current record supports a meaningful distinction:

- there is clear official `race-equity` language in Metro, RLRA, SHS, and H4S materials;
- that policy environment is relevant and real;
- but it is **not automatically the same thing** as a provably unlawful race-based housing decision in your individual case.

The strongest current federal-civil-rights targets remain:

- `Quantum` for a direct `42 U.S.C. § 1981` contracting theory tied to the Blossom application path;
- `HACC` for `42 U.S.C. § 1983` retaliation and other state-actor civil-rights theories;
- `HACC` and `Quantum` for Fair Housing Act retaliation / interference and potentially race-steering theories.

The SHS / RLRA / H4S / Metro policy materials make the discrimination theory more serious because they show a race-conscious policy environment and an express emphasis on racial-equity outcomes. But the current file still needs comparator and implementation evidence before those materials strongly prove an unlawful housing decision.

## What the Official Policy Materials Show

### 1. RLRA / Clackamas policy language

The RLRA materials in the workspace state that the program is rooted in a commitment to lead with racial equity by especially meeting the needs of Black, Indigenous and people of color, using equity criteria to inform program design, and evaluating disaggregated data to ensure racially equitable outcomes.

The same policy text also says counties will be accountable for ensuring referral pathways are equitable, inclusive, and effective at connecting eligible participants with housing options and supportive services.

That matters because RLRA is not just a general values statement. It specifically addresses:

- referral pathways,
- access,
- housing options,
- and supportive services.

## What This Does and Does Not Prove

### What it does prove

The current official materials prove:

- a real race-conscious policy environment existed;
- Metro and Clackamas openly used racial-equity and BIPOC-centered language in SHS / RLRA / H4S;
- Clackamas and HACC also tied that language directly to HACC forms, letters, internal practices, outreach, and outcome monitoring through the AFH;
- OHCS maintained statewide housing-equity and racial-disparity frameworks upstream of local implementation;
- referral pathways and supportive-services structures were explicitly discussed in racial-equity terms;
- HACC-administered or HACC-guided housing programs were operating inside that environment.

### What it does not yet prove

The materials do **not yet** prove by themselves:

- that a formal unlawful racial quota governed your specific Blossom file;
- that defendants expressly denied or delayed your application because you were not in a preferred racial group;
- that a court would automatically treat all racial-equity language in these materials as illegal on its face;
- or that every race-conscious policy statement was itself unlawful discrimination.

The missing step is implementation evidence:

- who got referred,
- who got advanced,
- what criteria were used,
- who got interviewed or placed,
- and whether race actually affected those decisions in the housing pipeline at issue.

## Stronger Causation Theory

The strongest filing-safe causation theory is not that every county or state `DEI` or `racial equity` policy was automatically unlawful on its face. The stronger theory is that the harms felt by plaintiffs were an extension of an official race-conscious housing-policy environment that had already been translated into planning, outreach, forms, referrals, monitoring, and intake practices inside the same county-linked pipeline that handled the Blossom opportunity.

That theory is materially stronger now because the record supports five linked steps.

### 1. Official county and HACC planning documents adopted race-conscious housing administration language

The 2022-2027 Clackamas County / HACC Assessment of Fair Housing states that internal policies and practices were to be reviewed with a `racial equity lens`, that HACC forms and letters were to be `racially equitable`, and that the County and HACC would `monitor housing stability outcomes for Black, Indigenous and People of Color and all protected classes`.

The same AFH states that these goals would become part of planning and performance reporting documents for the Housing Authority and the Community Development Division for the 2022 through 2026 program years.

That matters because it ties race-conscious language to:

- HACC forms,
- HACC letters,
- HACC internal practices,
- and performance monitoring,

which are exactly the kinds of instruments through which plaintiffs say they were burdened.

### 2. HACC also documented BIPOC-focused outreach in the waitlist and publicly supported housing system

The same AFH says HACC last opened its waitlists in June 2020 with a focus on `BIPOC outreach`, including outreach through BIPOC media outlets and partner agencies serving BIPOC populations.

That does not by itself prove unlawful discrimination against plaintiffs, but it does make the policy environment more concrete. It shows that race-conscious administration was not confined to abstract statements about values; it was reflected in actual outreach and intake strategy inside HACC-administered housing systems.

### 3. SHS / RLRA materials extended that same race-conscious framework into referrals and supportive services

The RLRA materials say the program is rooted in a commitment to lead with racial equity, especially meet the needs of Black, Indigenous and people of color, use equity criteria in program design, and evaluate disaggregated data to ensure racially equitable outcomes.

The SHS materials go further and state that housing and services should be provided to Black, Indigenous and people of color at higher rates than their representation among those experiencing homelessness.

That matters because these materials are not just about internal county employment culture. They speak directly to:

- referral pathways,
- housing access,
- service access,
- prioritization,
- and outcomes.

### 4. Plaintiffs' specific housing path sat inside that same county-linked pipeline

The current file supports that:

- Blossom was processed through a HACC PBV-linked path;
- the intake packet was submitted into a Quantum-linked pathway but not transmitted onward to HACC;
- Ashley Ferron and HACC were actively involved in the intake and voucher-processing chain;
- and the same chain was handling relocation, voucher issuance, household composition, and replacement-housing access.

That means the challenged conduct did not occur outside the county housing apparatus. It occurred inside a pipeline where county and HACC race-conscious planning and supportive-services language already existed.

### 5. Plaintiffs contemporaneously described the harm as race-linked while the pipeline was still active

The February 2, 2026 email to Ashley Ferron and `blossom@quantumres.com` is especially important because it narrows the causation gap. In that email, Benjamin Barber wrote that he met a Black couple with one child at Balfour Park who had just been granted Section 8 and were interviewing for Blossom while plaintiffs' application had not been sent to HACC, and he accused Quantum of race-based `lemon dropping`.

That matters because it shows:

- a contemporaneous comparator observation,
- direct notice to both county and Blossom-side actors,
- and a contemporaneous statement that plaintiffs themselves understood the unequal treatment to be race-linked while it was happening.

## The Best Theory of Extension

The best current theory is therefore:

- state and regional fair-housing and housing-equity frameworks supplied the broader planning structure;
- county SHS, RLRA, AFH, and HACC planning documents translated that structure into race-conscious county housing administration;
- HACC and Quantum operated together inside the same county-linked intake, referral, voucher, and placement pipeline;
- and plaintiffs experienced nonprocessing, nontransmission, delay, and comparator-skewed treatment inside that pipeline.

So the strongest statement is not that plaintiffs were injured by a free-floating `DEI policy` in the abstract. The stronger statement is that plaintiffs were injured by concrete housing-administration acts that occurred inside a county-linked program environment that openly used race-conscious criteria, race-conscious monitoring, and BIPOC-focused outcome language.

## Are These Policies “Illegal”?

The safest and most accurate answer is:

- `not automatically on the face of the current record`
- `but potentially unlawful if used to sort, prefer, burden, or deny housing opportunities on racial terms`

That distinction matters.

The current federal enforcement environment is more hostile to race-based decisionmaking than it was a few years ago. DOJ's July 30, 2025 guidance says federal antidiscrimination laws still apply to programs involving discriminatory practices even if they are labeled `DEI`.

But it is still not enough, standing alone, to say:

- every racial-equity policy is illegal,
- every BIPOC-centered statement is unlawful,
- or your case is won just because those documents exist.

The stronger filing-safe position is:

- these materials show a race-conscious operating environment;
- your case alleges a housing-contracting opportunity was obstructed on unequal racial terms inside that environment;
- and discovery should test whether the policy language translated into actual differential treatment.
