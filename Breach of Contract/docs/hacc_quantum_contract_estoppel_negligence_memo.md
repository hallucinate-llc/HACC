# HACC / Quantum Contract, Implied Contract, Estoppel, and Negligence Memo

## Bottom Line

On the current record, the strongest private-law theories are:

- `HACC`: breach of the relocation commitment and breach of an implied-in-fact relocation-process agreement.
- `Quantum`: breach of the intake-processing commitment and breach of an implied-in-fact application-processing agreement.

The strongest quasi-contract / reliance theory is:

- `HACC`: promissory estoppel based on concrete relocation representations and referral directions that induced the household to pursue the HACC-directed path.

The negligence theories are narrower:

- `HACC`: possible negligent performance of undertaken operational duties, but only if the claim can fit the Oregon Tort Claims Act, including notice and discretionary-immunity limits.
- `Quantum`: possible negligent intake handling or negligent misrepresentation, but only if discovery strengthens the special-relationship / reliance side enough to get past Oregon economic-loss limits.

## HACC

### 1. Breach of contract / relocation commitment

This remains the strongest HACC theory. The present record supports that HACC:

- triggered and directed a relocation process,
- linked the household to specific replacement-housing pathways,
- left relocation incomplete,
- and still pursued eviction.

That is stronger than treating HACC's conduct as only a statutory problem. It looks like a failed relocation transaction.

### 2. Breach of implied contract

This is the next-strongest HACC theory. Even if HACC later argues there was no single fully produced written contract with all household-specific terms, Oregon law allows an implied-in-fact agreement to be inferred from conduct. Here, the conduct includes:

- the Phase II relocation notice,
- the redevelopment-page representations,
- the January 2026 instruction to contact Ashley Ferron to start the Blossom process,
- and HACC's later acknowledgment that the household had already submitted the intake packet through Quantum's office.

That supports an argument that HACC undertook more than abstract policy obligations. It undertook a concrete relocation process for this household.

### 3. Promissory estoppel

This is the safest fallback if HACC argues that the relocation promises were too incomplete to count as a fully formed contract. The current theory is:

- HACC represented that relocation pathways were real and available,
- HACC directed the household into those pathways,
- the household relied by submitting packets and continuing relocation efforts,
- and HACC then pressed toward eviction while the pathways remained unfinished.

That is stronger than saying a final lease already existed. It is a reliance theory.

### 4. Negligence / official duty

This theory should be pleaded carefully. The stronger version is not:

- "HACC negligently failed the public."

The stronger version is:

- HACC undertook a specific household relocation process and then may have carelessly mishandled routine implementation steps within that undertaken process.

The current risk points are:

- OTCA notice under ORS 30.275,
- discretionary-function immunity under ORS 30.265,
- and the need to distinguish operational mistakes from protected policy judgment.

So negligence against HACC is real but narrower than the contract and implied-contract theories.

## Quantum

### 1. Breach of contract / intake-processing commitment

This remains the strongest Quantum theory. The record supports that:

- the packet was submitted to Quantum staff,
- Quantum did not pass it on to HACC,
- HACC later told Quantum to send the previously submitted packet,
- and the intake path never moved cleanly to completion.

That is already a substantial direct theory.

### 2. Breach of implied contract

This may be the cleanest Oregon framing against Quantum if the written management agreements are still not produced. The implied-in-fact argument is:

- HACC routed the household into Quantum's intake office,
- Quantum functioned as the owner/agent review point,
- the household submitted the materials there,
- and Quantum's conduct created an implied agreement to receive, communicate about, and timely process that application.

This is likely stronger than waiting for a fully produced written contract before pressing the claim.

### 3. Promissory estoppel

Quantum's estoppel theory is weaker than HACC's on the present record. Right now, HACC made the clearer relocation representations. Quantum's best exposure is still contract / implied contract / processing duty, not estoppel.

### 4. Negligence

Quantum's negligence theory is possible but should also be pleaded carefully. The limiting issue is Oregon's economic-loss doctrine. The strongest version is:

- Quantum undertook an applicant-information or intake-processing role,
- applicants and HACC were expected to rely on accurate receipt and status handling,
- and Quantum negligently failed to forward or accurately report the application's status.

That becomes materially stronger if discovery shows:

- front-desk intake procedures,
- applicant instructions,
- status communications,
- or any formal duty to provide accurate processing information.

## Best Current Ranking

### Strongest current theories

1. `HACC` breach of relocation commitment
2. `Quantum` breach of intake-processing commitment
3. `HACC` implied-in-fact relocation-process agreement
4. `Quantum` implied-in-fact application-processing agreement

### Strong but narrower theories

1. `HACC` promissory estoppel
2. `HACC` negligent operational handling of undertaken relocation duties
3. `Quantum` negligent intake handling / negligent misrepresentation

## Main Record Gaps

To make these claims harder to attack, the next most important missing items are:

1. The actual HACC-Quantum management / owner-participation / HAP documents.
2. Quantum's intake log or CRM record for the packet.
3. A household-specific relocation plan or case-management record from HACC.
4. OTCA notice proof if a negligence claim against HACC is going to be pressed.
5. More concrete reliance-loss proof, including fees, mailings, missed alternatives, or timing prejudice.
