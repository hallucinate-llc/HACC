## Preliminary Statement

This FED action arises out of an incomplete demolition/redevelopment relocation process, not a clean and ordinary tenancy default. HACC's own records show that by September 19, 2024 the Hillside Park Section 18 / redevelopment relocation process had been triggered, that replacement housing opportunities such as Blossom and Hillside Manor were part of the same replacement-housing structure, and that Quantum Residential received but did not transmit the household's intake packet to HACC. Plaintiff now seeks possession while its own relocation duties remained incomplete and while the replacement-housing pathway had been materially obstructed. Plaintiff's own prior breaches and the related interference of Quantum Residential bar or reduce the relief sought here.

## Affirmative Defenses

### First Affirmative Defense: Prior Material Breach

1. Plaintiff materially breached obligations arising from the operative relocation process before seeking eviction.
2. Those breaches include failure to complete relocation counseling, failure to commit or pay relocation expenses, failure to furnish comparable replacement housing, and pursuit of eviction before relocation was complete.
3. Plaintiff's prior material breach bars or limits the relief sought.

### Second Affirmative Defense: Prevention / Hindrance

1. Plaintiff and/or Plaintiff's associated relocation agents hindered Defendants' ability to complete the relocation process now cited as a basis for eviction.
2. Plaintiff directed Defendants into replacement-housing paths involving Parkside Heights and Quantum-side packet handling.
3. Defendants submitted the Parkside Heights application after HACC identified that path and its next steps.
4. Parkside Heights acknowledged receipt of the application but advised that the requested two-bedroom units were still under construction.
5. The Blossom-side packet was also received but not transmitted onward to HACC.
6. The present record also supports an inference that this was a recurring delay problem within the same Quantum-managed relocation network, not a single isolated intake error.
7. Plaintiff also attempted to route the household toward a one-bedroom third-floor Easton Ridge placement that was facially incompatible with Jane Cortez's use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter and with her inability to safely manage stairs.
8. Plaintiff also failed to timely provide the accommodation package that the provider verification supported: a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver sleeping arrangement, and accommodation for Sarah as an emotional support dog. Exhibits 2 and 3.
9. Plaintiff also treated Julio's household status inconsistently by taking or being accused of taking lease-side action in December 2025, relying on paperwork effective January 1, 2026, then stating on January 12, 2026 that Julio was still a household member unless court papers were submitted, and then reducing the household to one-bedroom eligibility by January 27, 2026. Exhibits 4 and 10.
10. Plaintiff may not obtain possession based on a failure that Plaintiff and its relocation pathway materially caused, aggravated, partially recognized, or left unresolved beyond the vacate deadline.

### Third Affirmative Defense: Failure of Conditions Precedent

1. Lawful displacement required completion of the relocation steps triggered by the redevelopment approval.
2. Those conditions included counseling, relocation expense support, and a comparable replacement path.
3. Plaintiff proceeded without satisfying those conditions.

### Fourth Affirmative Defense: Unclean Hands

1. Plaintiff seeks equitable and possession-based relief while acting inequitably in the same transaction.
2. Plaintiff pursued eviction while relocation remained incomplete, while a one-bedroom third-floor unit at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, was treated as a replacement option for Jane Cortez despite her inability to safely climb stairs and despite her documented use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter, while extensive documentation burdens and voucher delays remained unresolved, while Julio household-status treatment remained internally inconsistent, while the household alleges a restrained non-household actor continued influencing HACC's lease handling, and while hearing access for Julio Cortez remained impaired. Exhibits 4, 7, 10, and 12.

### Fifth Affirmative Defense: Accessibility / Accommodation Failure

1. Plaintiff knew or should have known of Jane Cortez's stair and mobility limitations, that she could barely walk, and that she used a cane and often needed a walker or mobility scooter.
2. Plaintiff also knew or should have known from the accommodation record that provider verification supported a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, that Benjamin Barber functioned as the needed live-in aide or caregiver with a separate sleeping arrangement, and that Sarah served as an emotional support dog alleviating disability-related symptoms. Exhibits 2 and 3.
3. Plaintiff failed to provide an accessible and effective relocation path and instead proposed a one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, despite those limitations.
4. Plaintiff cannot base possession relief on alleged relocation failure where the proposed path was not reasonably accessible or where the accommodation was only partially recognized but not actually provided in practice.

### Sixth Affirmative Defense: Setoff / Recoupment

1. Defendants suffered delay, housing instability, administrative burden, and related damages caused by Plaintiff's relocation failures.
2. To the extent Plaintiff seeks any monetary recovery, Defendants assert setoff and/or recoupment.

### Seventh Affirmative Defense: Retaliatory or Pretextual Escalation

1. Defendants complained to HACC and Quantum about discrimination, including race discrimination and housing steering, service-animal issues, housing administration failures, and nonprocessing of the Blossom application.
2. Those complaints included written assertions that the Blossom opportunity was being handled on unequal terms and that civil-rights violations were occurring in the housing-access and contracting path.
3. After those complaints, HACC and Quantum continued or escalated adverse actions including notices, documentation burdens, delay in voucher processing, reversal of the two-bedroom voucher path, and continued obstruction of relocation.
4. Plaintiff may not obtain possession based on a sequence materially infected by retaliatory, pretextual, complaint-driven, or unlawfully discriminatory escalation.

### Eighth Affirmative Defense: Denial of Meaningful Hearing and Process

1. Plaintiff and its associated housing-administration process affected the household's rights, obligations, welfare, and continued occupancy without providing a meaningful and accessible opportunity to be heard.
2. The present record supports the allegation that Julio Cortez requested a hearing orally, that HACC insisted on a written request despite knowing he could not write in any language, and that no meaningful hearing was provided before threatened housing loss. Exhibits 4, 8, 11, and 12.
3. The same process was further clouded by HACC's inconsistent treatment of whether Julio remained part of the household, even while HACC was already acting on changed household composition for lease and voucher purposes. Exhibits 4 and 10.
4. The eviction should therefore be denied, stayed, or otherwise limited because the underlying process was not lawfully completed.

## Counterclaims Against Plaintiff

### Counterclaim One: Breach of Relocation Obligations

1. Defendants reallege the foregoing paragraphs.
2. By reason of the approval-driven relocation process, Plaintiff undertook enforceable relocation obligations to the affected household.
3. Those obligations included counseling, relocation-expense support, and comparable replacement housing before displacement was completed.
4. Plaintiff materially breached by:
   - failing to provide relocation counseling;
   - failing to commit or pay relocation expenses;
   - failing to provide comparable replacement housing;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported two-bedroom, first-floor accommodation needed for Jane Cortez's disability-related mobility and caregiving needs;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported live-in aide or caregiver arrangement with a separate sleeping area;
   - failing to accommodate Sarah as an emotional support dog in the replacement-housing pathway despite provider verification that Sarah alleviated disability-related symptoms;
   - offering a non-accessible one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, despite knowing Jane Cortez could barely walk and used a cane and often needed a walker or mobility scooter; and
   - pursuing eviction before relocation was complete.
5. Defendants suffered damages and equitable prejudice as a direct result.

### Counterclaim Two: Promissory / Equitable Estoppel

1. Plaintiff represented that relocation pathways existed and would be administered through its relocation system.
2. Plaintiff should reasonably have expected Defendants to rely on those representations.
3. Defendants did rely on them by pursuing the identified pathways, including the Parkside Heights application path and the Blossom packet process.
4. Defendants substantially performed the Parkside application-submission step, and Parkside Heights acknowledged receipt.
5. Plaintiff's later failure to complete the promised relocation path, while still seeking eviction, caused foreseeable harm.

### Counterclaim Three: Failure to Provide Comparable and Accessible Replacement Housing

1. Plaintiff had a duty to provide a replacement path suitable to the household's actual needs and limitations.
2. Plaintiff knew or should have known that provider verification supported a comparable replacement path consisting of a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver setup with a separate sleeping area, and space and permission for Sarah as an emotional support dog. Exhibit 2, with partial recognition in Exhibit 3.
3. Plaintiff also knew or should have known that the one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments was infeasible because Jane Cortez could barely walk, could not safely climb stairs, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter.
4. Plaintiff breached by offering that inaccessible Easton Ridge placement and failing to provide an accessible ground-floor or otherwise usable alternative that actually met the accommodation record.

### Counterclaim Four: Wrongful Delay, Documentation Burden, and Process Interference

1. Defendants reallege the foregoing paragraphs.
2. Plaintiff controlled or materially influenced the voucher, relocation, document-review, notice, and hearing-access process needed for the household to avoid displacement.
3. Plaintiff imposed extensive and escalating documentation demands even after substantial materials had already been produced.
4. Plaintiff also delayed voucher processing, reversed the earlier two-bedroom voucher path, partially recognized but did not fully provide the requested accommodation, failed to timely honor the live-in aide and emotional-support-animal components of the accommodation request, attempted to direct the household toward the inaccessible Easton Ridge one-bedroom third-floor unit, treated Julio's household status inconsistently across lease, relocation, and voucher communications, allegedly acted on lease-side pressure from a restrained non-household actor, and failed to provide a timely and meaningful hearing process for Julio Cortez. Exhibits 3, 4, 7, 8, 10, 11, and 12.
5. Those acts wrongfully burdened and delayed the household's ability to complete relocation and avoid eviction, caused confusion about who Plaintiff regarded as part of the household and on what effective date, and forced the household to respond to housing options that were never realistically usable by Jane Cortez or consistent with the provider-supported accommodation record.

### Counterclaim Five: Retaliation / Interference With Protected Complaints and Housing Rights

1. Defendants reallege the foregoing paragraphs.
2. The household complained to HACC and Quantum about discrimination, including race discrimination and housing steering, service-animal issues, unlawful housing administration, and failure to process the Blossom application.
3. Those complaints included written assertions that Blossom had refused to process the household's housing application for months, that the opportunity was being handled on unequal terms, and that the household intended to enforce civil-rights protections.
4. Plaintiff knew of those complaints and nevertheless continued or escalated notice activity, documentation burdens, voucher delay, and relocation obstruction.
5. The household was thereby burdened, delayed, and exposed to eviction in part because it complained and attempted to enforce housing rights.

## Motion for Leave to Pursue Related Third-Party Claims Against Quantum Residential

Defendants move for leave to pursue related third-party claims against Quantum Residential, and for joinder as necessary, because complete adjudication of the FED-related defenses and causation issues cannot occur without Quantum's participation.

### Grounds

1. HACC's own records place Quantum inside the same replacement-housing and RAD/PBV portfolio implicated by the Hillside Park relocation process.
2. Defendants submitted packet materials through Quantum-side staff in that relocation path.
3. HACC later acknowledged that Quantum received the packet but did not provide it to HACC.
4. HACC then instructed Quantum staff to transmit the packet.
5. The same record also suggests recurrence: the household describes a months-long Blossom processing failure, and the later Parkside Heights application route, also tied to a Quantum-managed property, remained delayed and conditional under the same relocation deadline pressure.
6. The same record further includes allegations that Quantum refused or failed to accommodate service-animal-related tenancy issues in the Blossom path and continued obstructive conduct after the household complained about discrimination and nonprocessing.
7. The same record further includes allegations that Quantum obstructed a housing-related contracting opportunity on unequal terms after written race-discrimination and steering complaints were communicated.
8. The policy environment also included published racial-equity and BIPOC-focused housing language, which Defendants use as context and notice rather than as the sole proof of liability.
9. Those facts directly bear on prevention, causation, completion of conditions precedent, retaliation, discriminatory obstruction, and the fairness of granting possession in this action.
10. Separate litigation would risk incomplete factfinding and inconsistent rulings about who prevented completion of the relocation pathway.

If leave is granted, Defendants allege:

1. Quantum participated in the intake, processing, review, and/or transmission steps of the replacement-housing path.
2. Quantum received the household's packet but failed to transmit it onward in a timely manner.

4. The present record also supports a reasonable inference that similar intake or processing delay was likely to recur within Quantum-managed relocation properties, not merely at one intake handoff.

### Proposed Related Third-Party Allegation Three: Race-Based Interference With Housing Opportunity / Contracting Path

1. The Blossom application and placement process was a housing-related contracting and tenancy opportunity tied to the household's displacement and voucher-backed relocation path.
2. Quantum controlled or materially participated in the intake, application, processing, and placement steps for that opportunity.
3. Defendants allege that the household submitted the application, that no timely approval, denial, ineligibility determination, or lawful written deficiency notice was ever issued, and that written complaints then asserted race discrimination, housing steering, and unequal treatment in that same housing opportunity.

### Proposed Related Third-Party Allegation Four: Service-Animal and Housing-Access Obstruction

1. Quantum participated in the Blossom-side housing-access process for a HACC-linked relocation opportunity.
2. The household alleges that Quantum not only failed to process the application, but also refused or failed to accommodate service-animal-related tenancy issues in that housing path.
3. That conduct further obstructed the household's access to replacement housing and contributed to loss of housing opportunity and eviction exposure.
