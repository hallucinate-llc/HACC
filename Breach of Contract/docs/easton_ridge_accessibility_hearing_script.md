## 1. Core Theme

The short version is:

- HACC cannot base eviction on failure to relocate where the replacement path it offered was not actually accessible to Jane Cortez.

## 2. Very Short Opening

Your Honor, the key problem with Easton Ridge is simple. HACC was on notice that Jane Cortez could barely walk, used a cane, often needed a walker or mobility scooter, and could not safely climb stairs. HACC also had accommodation material supporting a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement. Yet the record shows HACC still tried to route the household toward a one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge. That was not a realistic or accessible replacement path.

## 4. Best Bench Narrative

The Court can treat this as a direct accessibility problem:

1. HACC knew Jane Cortez had serious mobility limitations.
2. HACC had accommodation materials supporting a first-floor, two-bedroom arrangement.
3. HACC nevertheless proposed or maintained a one-bedroom third-floor Easton Ridge option.
4. That option was facially incompatible with Jane Cortez's no-stairs and mobility-device needs.
5. HACC therefore cannot fairly blame the household for not completing relocation through an option that was not actually usable.

## 5. Best Legal Framing

If the Court asks for the legal point, the answer is:

- a housing provider or housing authority cannot rely on a replacement path that is not reasonably accessible to the disabled household member;
- partial recognition is not enough if the offered path still does not meet the disability-related need in practice;
- and eviction should not proceed based on failure to accept a unit that was not actually feasible for the household.

## 6. Best One-Sentence Ask

The Court should not grant possession based on a supposed relocation failure where HACC's own proposed Easton Ridge path was not accessible to Jane Cortez.

## 7. If The Court Pushes Back

If the Court asks `but wasn't another unit later discussed`, the answer is:

- yes, other possibilities were discussed later;
- but that does not cure the fact that Easton Ridge itself was not a realistic accessible option, and the overall accommodation process still remained partial, delayed, and unresolved.

## 8. Short Closing

Easton Ridge matters because it shows that the household was not refusing a workable replacement. The problem was that the replacement being pushed was not actually accessible to Jane Cortez, and HACC should not benefit from that mismatch in an eviction case.
