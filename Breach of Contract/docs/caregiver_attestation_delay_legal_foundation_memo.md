# Caregiver Attestation Delay Legal Foundation Memo

This memo addresses a narrow question:

- whether the financial-attestation and income-verification process should have been simpler and faster once HACC had notice that Benjamin Barber was being presented as Jane Cortez's live-in caregiver or functionally equivalent caregiver, while county-side representative-payee and Money Management personnel were also involved in Jane Cortez's rent handling.

## Bottom Line

The strongest legally grounded version of the claim is:

- HACC was allowed to request some verification.
- But HACC was not allowed to turn the accommodation or caregiver issue into an open-ended financial dragnet untethered from what was actually necessary to evaluate disability-related need, live-in-aide status, or current countable family income.
- The record now supports a narrower but substantial argument that HACC imposed a verification process that was overbroad, stale, and delay-producing, especially by continuing to demand records tied to businesses or entities said not to have existed for about 10 years while voucher, relocation, and accommodation processing remained unresolved.

The cleanest legal framing is therefore not:

- `no income verification was allowed at all`

but instead:

- `once HACC had notice of disability-related caregiver needs and county-side money-management involvement, it was required to seek the least information necessary, keep the inquiry tied to the actual disability-related and income-calculation issues, and process the matter promptly rather than expanding it into a prolonged and burdensome document hunt.`

## 1. Governing Legal Foundation

### A. Live-in aides are a special category, not just ordinary counted family members

HUD's HCV regulation provides:

- a family may request that the PHA approve a live-in aide; and
- the PHA `must approve` a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation.

See:

- [24 C.F.R. § 982.316](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/part-982/section-982.316)

HUD's June 2025 HCV guidebook further explains that:

- a live-in aide is defined by 24 C.F.R. § 5.403,
- a live-in aide is not considered a member of the assisted family, and
- the PHA must approve a live-in aide if needed as a reasonable accommodation.

See:

- [HUD HCV Guidebook, Housing Search and Leasing, June 2025](https://www.hud.gov/sites/default/files/PIH/documents/HCV-Guidebook_Housing-Search-and-Leasing-Chapter_June-2025.pdf)

### B. Once disability-related need is known or reasonably raised, the PHA must seek only limited necessary information

HUD's April 2025 Fair Housing chapter states:

- if the disability and disability-related need are obvious, readily apparent, or already known, the PHA cannot request additional information about the disability or disability-related need;
- if they are not obvious, the PHA may seek only limited information;
- the PHA must seek the `least amount of information needed`;
- the PHA must not request detailed information about the nature or severity of the disability; and
- it may violate Section 504 and the Fair Housing Act to require additional disability-related information that is unnecessary.

See:

- [HUD HCV Guidebook, Fair Housing and Nondiscrimination Requirements, April 2025](https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/PIH/documents/HCV_Guidebook-Chapter_Fair-Housing_April-2025.pdf)

The same HUD chapter also says:

- requests can be made orally or in writing,
- a request cannot be refused because a standard form was not used, and
- the PHA must work through the reasonable-accommodation process before taking adverse action when disability-related information comes to light in the course of an alleged compliance problem.

See the same guidebook above.

### C. DOJ/HUD guidance uses the same necessity-limited approach

The HUD/DOJ joint statement says:

- a housing provider may request only reliable disability-related information that is necessary to verify disability and the relationship between the disability and the accommodation;
- in most cases, detailed medical records are not necessary; and
- once disability is established, the provider should seek only information necessary to evaluate whether the accommodation is needed because of a disability.

See:

- [DOJ/HUD Joint Statement on Reasonable Accommodations Under the Fair Housing Act](https://www.justice.gov/crt/us-department-housing-and-urban-development)

### D. HUD's 2025 PIH notice reinforces the same rule for unit-related accommodation review

HUD's PIH Notice 2025-12 states:

- if the disability or disability-related need is obvious, readily apparent, or already known, the PHA cannot request additional information;
- if not, the PHA may only request information necessary to evaluate the disability-related need;
- the PHA must seek the `least amount of information needed`; and
- location and accessibility issues such as ground-level access, elevators, proximity to services, and proximity to caregivers are proper disability-related considerations.

See:

- [HUD PIH Notice 2025-12](https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/OCHCO/documents/2025-12pihn.pdf)

## 4. Strongest Legal Assessment

### A. Strong claim

The strongest current claim is:

- HACC had authority to seek some targeted verification,
- but the record supports the inference that HACC went beyond the least-information-necessary standard and into a broad, stale, and delay-producing audit posture,
- even though caregiver, disability, and county-side payment-management issues were already on the table.

### B. Why this is stronger than a blanket objection

This framing is stronger than saying:

- `they were never allowed to ask for financial records`

because the law does allow some screening and some income verification in the right circumstances.

But the current record supports the narrower proposition that:

- the scope, age, and persistence of the demands became disproportionate,
- the demands appear to have treated Benjamin Barber more like an ordinary counted household-income source than like a proposed live-in aide or caregiver,
- and the demands coincided with and allegedly contributed to continued delay in voucher, relocation, and accommodation processing.

### C. Where the argument is strongest in time

The argument is strongest in two phases:

1. `By early February 2026`, HACC had already been told that county-side Money Management was affecting Jane Cortez's rent handling, which undermines any suggestion that the household's financial situation could only be understood by escalating scrutiny of Benjamin Barber alone.
2. `By late March 2026`, once provider verification and accommodation documentation were in place, the legal footing for continued expansive probing was weaker and the expectation of a prompt, targeted, interactive accommodation response was stronger.

## 5. Best Litigation Framing

The best litigation framing is:

- HACC could request information reasonably necessary to determine current eligibility, current countable household income, and whether Benjamin Barber qualified as a live-in aide or disability-related caregiver.
- But HACC should have simplified, narrowed, and accelerated the process once it knew:
  - Jane Cortez had longstanding disability-related limitations,
  - Benjamin Barber was handling her affairs,
  - county-side representative-payee and Money Management personnel were already involved in rent handling,
  - and the accommodation request was tied to caregiving, accessibility, privacy, and ADL/IADL support.
- Instead, the present record supports the claim that HACC expanded the process into repeated demands for business, crypto, banking, and stale entity documents, including materials tied to entities said to have been defunct for about 10 years, while voucher and relocation timing kept slipping.

## 6. Best Next Proof Targets

To harden this claim further, the best next proof targets are:

1. HACC policies or training materials on what may be requested from a proposed live-in aide.
2. HACC records showing whether Benjamin Barber was classified as counted income, caregiver, or live-in aide at each stage.
3. HACC internal notes explaining why decade-old company records were thought relevant.
4. HACC time logs or task histories showing how much staff time was spent on these document demands.
5. County Social Services / Money Management records showing what HACC was told about Jane Cortez's rent handling and whether a county payment was expected.
