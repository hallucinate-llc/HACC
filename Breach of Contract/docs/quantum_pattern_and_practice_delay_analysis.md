# Quantum Delay Pattern Analysis

This note addresses whether the current record supports the argument that delayed or failed processing at Blossom is not an isolated event, but part of a recurring pattern likely to reappear at Quantum-managed properties involved in the same relocation network.

## Bottom-Line Assessment

The current record does not yet prove an adjudicated company-wide "pattern and practice" in the strictest sense. It does, however, support a narrower and still useful inference: repeated processing-delay problems appear across more than one Quantum-linked property in the same HACC relocation ecosystem, making recurrence reasonably foreseeable.

## Record Support for the Recurrence Inference

1. The Blossom episode is not framed in the record as a one-day misunderstanding. In the March 26, 2026 email thread, Benjamin Barber stated that in December he "placed an application to the blossom" and that "they sat on and did not send" it to HACC. That statement aligns with HACC's later written acknowledgment that the intake packet had in fact been submitted to Quantum staff but had not been forwarded to HACC.

2. The same relocation record separately shows a second Quantum-linked property, Parkside Heights, participating in the same HACC relocation thread. The joinder evidence file identifies Parkside Heights at `parksideheights@quantumres.com` as "another Quantum property" and shows HACC relocation staff copied in the same thread.

3. The Parkside path also reflects delay or incompleteness rather than a clean placement. After Defendants submitted the Parkside application on March 29, 2026, Parkside acknowledged receipt on March 30, 2026 but advised that the relevant two-bedroom units were still under construction and that occupancy was not expected until the end of April.

4. The March 30, 2026 Parkside thread also contains Benjamin Barber's statement that "the last time I submitted an application to the blossom it sat unprocessed for several months," tying the earlier Blossom delay to the later Parkside effort and showing the household itself understood the problem as recurring.

5. The timing matters. The same general property-management network appears in both episodes while HACC was pressing a move or eviction timeline. That overlap supports an argument that delay in application handling within Quantum-managed relocation properties was not merely hypothetical; it had already happened once and remained likely to recur.

## Strongest Litigation Use of This Point

The safest and strongest way to use this evidence is:

- not to allege as a proven fact that Quantum had an established unlawful corporate-wide policy;
- but to argue that the record supports a reasonable inference of recurring delay, repeated intake friction, and foreseeable reoccurrence within Quantum-managed properties involved in HACC's relocation pathways.

That framing strengthens the following theories:

- prevention or hindrance of performance;
- substantial performance by the household;
- causation as to why relocation remained incomplete by the eviction deadline; and
- the need to join or keep Quantum in the case because the problem appears to extend beyond a single isolated handoff failure.

## Proof Still Missing if a Broader "Pattern and Practice" Theory Is Desired

To elevate this from a recurrence inference to a stronger pattern-and-practice showing, the most useful missing proof would be:

1. intake logs or CRM records from Blossom, Hillside Manor, and Parkside showing receipt dates, review dates, and forwarding dates;
2. internal Quantum communications about HACC relocation referrals or delayed processing;
3. comparable records from other former Hillside Park households routed through Quantum-managed properties; and
4. any owner/agent participation agreements or workflow documents showing who was responsible for receipt, screening, and transmission of relocation applications.

## Present Conclusion

On the current record, it is fair to argue that the Blossom processing delay was part of a broader recurring problem in the same Quantum-managed relocation network, and that the same type of delay was likely to recur at Parkside or other Quantum properties unless the court accounted for that interference when evaluating the eviction and the relocation defenses.
