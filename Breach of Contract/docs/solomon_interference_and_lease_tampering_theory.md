## 3. Current inference theory

- The present theory is not that there is already a preserved HACC email saying, by name, `Solomon asked us to remove Benjamin from the lease.` I have not found that document in the reviewed record.
- The present theory is instead an elimination-based inference:
  - Benjamin Barber did not request his own removal.
  - Jane Cortez did not request Benjamin's removal.
  - Julio Cortez was already being treated by HACC as the person whose status was disputed, removed, or subject to bifurcation.
  - the federal-complaint narrative states that HACC removed Benjamin and restored `the restrained party`.
- On that logic, the current pleading theory is that the remaining plausible source of contrary pressure or request was Solomon Samuel Barber.

## 4. Why the Solomon texts matter to that inference

- The Solomon texts strengthen the inference because they show:
  - Solomon had actual notice of the restraining-order filing and grant;
  - Solomon adopted the position that he did not need to comply unless and until he accepted service on his own terms;
  - Solomon believed he could simply have the judge `overturn` the order rather than obey it.
- That matters because the lease-removal theory depends in part on the idea that a restrained non-household actor continued trying to affect housing or lease consequences despite the order and despite the household's bifurcation / safety requests.

## 5. Best current pleading formulation

The safest current formulation is:

- `On information and belief, after notice of the restraining-order filing and grant, Solomon Samuel Barber continued communicating with HACC about household or lease matters and sought to influence HACC to remove Benjamin Barber from the lease.`

That is stronger and safer than stating as an already-proved fact:

- `HACC has admitted that Solomon asked to have Benjamin taken off the lease.`

I do not currently have that direct admission.

## 7. Bottom line

- `High confidence`: Solomon had notice of the restraining-order filing and grant.
- `High confidence`: Solomon later expressed that he was not `incentivized to cooperate`, would comply only once he thought he had been served, and believed he could have the judge `overturn` the order.
- `Moderate confidence`: those texts support an inference that Solomon was willing to ignore or evade the order rather than comply in good faith.
- `Low-to-moderate confidence as a direct factual claim, but moderate as a pleading inference`: Solomon was the person who communicated with HACC to have Benjamin taken off the lease.

So the current best use is as an `on information and belief` interference / lease-tampering theory, backed by direct Solomon noncompliance texts and the otherwise unexplained lease-removal sequence.
