## Preliminary Statement

This eviction action cannot be fairly decided as a simple noncompliance or holdover case because the alleged basis for eviction arose in the middle of an uncompleted Section 18 relocation process triggered by demolition/redevelopment of Hillside Park. HACC's own records show that the relocation process was activated no later than September 19, 2024, that replacement housing opportunities such as Blossom and Hillside Manor were part of the same demolition-replacement pathway, and that Quantum Residential received but did not transmit the household's intake packet to HACC. Defendants therefore assert that Plaintiff's own prior material breaches, and the related interference of Quantum Residential in the same relocation sequence, bar or reduce the relief sought in this FED action.

## Affirmative Defenses

### First Affirmative Defense: Prior Material Breach by Plaintiff

1. Before Plaintiff sought eviction, Plaintiff had already materially breached duties arising from the operative relocation process.
2. Those breaches included failure to complete relocation counseling, failure to commit or pay relocation expenses, failure to furnish comparable replacement housing, and pursuit of eviction before relocation was complete.
3. A party in prior material breach may not obtain relief based on alleged downstream nonperformance caused or aggravated by its own breach.
4. Plaintiff's claims are therefore barred in whole or in part.

### Second Affirmative Defense: Prevention / Hindrance of Performance

1. Plaintiff and/or its relocation agents prevented or hindered Defendants' ability to complete the relocation path now invoked as a basis for eviction.
2. HACC directed the household into replacement-housing paths involving Parkside Heights and Blossom/Quantum-side packet handling.
3. Defendants submitted the Parkside Heights application after HACC identified that option and stated the required next steps.
4. Parkside Heights acknowledged receipt of the application but advised that the requested two-bedroom units were still under construction.
5. Quantum also received the Blossom-side packet, but HACC later acknowledged that the packet had not been transmitted to HACC.
6. The present record also supports an inference that this was not a one-off breakdown, but a recurring delay problem within the same Quantum-managed relocation network.
7. Plaintiff also attempted to route the household toward a one-bedroom third-floor Easton Ridge placement that was facially incompatible with Jane Cortez's use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter and with her inability to safely manage stairs.
8. Plaintiff also failed to timely provide the accommodation package that the provider verification supported: a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver sleeping arrangement, and accommodation for Sarah as an emotional support dog. Exhibits 2 and 3.
9. Plaintiff also treated Julio's household status inconsistently by taking or being accused of taking lease-side action in December 2025, relying on paperwork effective January 1, 2026, then stating on January 12, 2026 that Julio was still a household member unless court papers were submitted, and then reducing the household to one-bedroom eligibility by January 27, 2026. Exhibits 4 and 10.
10. Plaintiff may not rely on noncompletion of a process that Plaintiff and its associated intake pathway materially obstructed, delayed, partially recognized, or left conditional beyond the stated vacate timeline.

### Third Affirmative Defense: Failure of Condition Precedent

1. Completion of statutorily and administratively required relocation steps was a necessary condition precedent to lawful displacement.
2. Those conditions included, at minimum, counseling, relocation expense support, and a comparable replacement path.
3. Plaintiff sought eviction without satisfying those conditions precedent.
4. The action is therefore premature and should be dismissed or denied.

### Fourth Affirmative Defense: Unclean Hands / Equitable Bar

1. Plaintiff seeks equitable or possession-based relief while acting inequitably in the same transaction.
2. Plaintiff's inequitable conduct includes pursuing eviction while relocation remained incomplete, while a one-bedroom third-floor unit at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, was being treated as a replacement option for Jane Cortez despite her inability to safely climb stairs and despite her documented use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter, while excessive documentation demands were still being used in the relocation and voucher path, while Julio household-status treatment remained internally inconsistent, and while hearing access for Julio Cortez remained impaired. Exhibits 4, 7, and 10.
3. Plaintiff is barred by unclean hands from obtaining the equitable relief sought, including immediate possession.

### Fifth Affirmative Defense: Disability / Accessibility Failure

1. Plaintiff knew or should have known that Jane Cortez had serious stair and mobility limitations, could barely walk, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter.
2. Plaintiff also knew or should have known from the accommodation record that provider verification supported a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, that Benjamin Barber functioned as the needed live-in aide or caregiver with a separate sleeping arrangement, and that Sarah served as an emotional support dog alleviating disability-related symptoms. Exhibits 2 and 3.
3. Plaintiff nevertheless failed to provide an accessible and effective relocation path and instead offered a one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, that was not feasible for her given those limitations.
4. Plaintiff cannot base eviction relief on alleged failure to relocate where the replacement path was not reasonably accessible or where the accommodation was only partially recognized but not actually provided in practice.

### Sixth Affirmative Defense: Setoff / Recoupment

1. Defendants have suffered damages, delay, housing instability, administrative burden, and other compensable harm caused by Plaintiff's relocation failures.
2. To the extent Plaintiff claims rent, holdover damages, or other monetary relief, Defendants assert setoff and/or recoupment in an amount to be proven.

### Seventh Affirmative Defense: Retaliatory or Pretextual Escalation

1. Defendants complained to HACC and Quantum about discrimination, including race discrimination, housing steering, service-animal issues, housing administration failures, nonprocessing of the Blossom application, and related legal violations.
2. Those complaints included written assertions that the Blossom opportunity was being handled on unequal terms and that civil-rights violations were occurring in the housing-access and contracting path.
3. After those complaints, HACC and Quantum continued or escalated adverse actions including notice activity, documentation burdens, delay in voucher processing, reversal of the two-bedroom voucher path, and continued obstruction of the relocation process.
4. Plaintiff may not obtain possession based on a sequence materially infected by retaliatory, pretextual, complaint-driven, or unlawfully discriminatory escalation.

### Eighth Affirmative Defense: Denial of Meaningful Hearing and Process

1. Plaintiff and its associated housing-administration process affected the household's rights, obligations, welfare, and continued occupancy without providing a meaningful and accessible opportunity to be heard.
2. The present record supports the allegation that Julio Cortez requested a hearing orally, that HACC insisted on a written request despite knowing he could not write in any language, and that no meaningful hearing was provided before threatened housing loss. Exhibits 4 and 8.
3. The same process was further clouded by HACC's inconsistent treatment of whether Julio remained part of the household, even while HACC was already acting on changed household composition for lease and voucher purposes. Exhibits 4 and 10.
4. The eviction should therefore be denied, stayed, or otherwise limited because the underlying process was not lawfully completed.

## Counterclaims Against HACC

### Counterclaim One: Breach of Relocation Obligations / Breach of Relocation Agreement

1. Defendants reallege the preceding allegations.
2. By reason of the Phase II approval and demolition-related relocation process, HACC undertook enforceable relocation obligations to the affected household.
3. Those obligations included relocation counseling, relocation-expense support, and furnishing comparable replacement housing before displacement was completed.
4. HACC materially breached those obligations by:
   - failing to provide relocation counseling;
   - failing to commit or pay relocation expenses;
   - failing to furnish comparable replacement housing;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported two-bedroom, first-floor accommodation needed for Jane Cortez's disability-related mobility and caregiving needs;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported live-in aide or caregiver arrangement with a separate sleeping area;
   - failing to accommodate Sarah as an emotional support dog in the replacement-housing pathway despite provider verification that Sarah alleviated disability-related symptoms;
   - offering a non-accessible one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, despite knowing Jane Cortez could barely walk, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter; and
   - pursuing eviction before relocation was complete.
5. As a direct result, Defendants suffered housing instability, delay, loss of relocation protections, litigation harm, and related damages.

### Counterclaim Two: Promissory Estoppel / Equitable Estoppel

1. HACC represented that relocation pathways existed and would be administered through its relocation process.
2. HACC should reasonably have expected Defendants to rely on those representations.
3. Defendants did in fact rely on those representations by pursuing the identified pathways, including the Parkside Heights application path and the Blossom track.
4. Defendants substantially performed the next identified step in the Parkside path by submitting the Parkside Heights application, which Parkside acknowledged receiving.
5. HACC's later failure to complete the relocation process, while still pursuing eviction, caused foreseeable injury.
6. Injustice can be avoided only by denying eviction relief unless and until the relocation obligations are completed, and by awarding such additional equitable or monetary relief as allowed by law.

### Counterclaim Three: Breach of Duty to Provide Comparable and Accessible Replacement Housing

1. HACC had a duty to provide a comparable replacement path suitable to the household's actual limitations.
2. HACC knew or should have known that provider verification supported a comparable replacement path consisting of a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver setup with a separate sleeping area, and space and permission for Sarah as an emotional support dog. Exhibit 2, with partial recognition in Exhibit 3.
3. HACC also knew or should have known that the one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments was infeasible for this household because Jane Cortez could barely walk, could not safely climb stairs, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter, and because the household had documented mobility limitations and scooter access issues.
4. By offering that inaccessible Easton Ridge unit and failing to provide an accessible ground-floor or otherwise usable alternative that actually met the accommodation record, HACC breached its replacement-housing obligations and caused compensable harm.

### Counterclaim Four: Wrongful Delay, Documentation Burden, and Process Interference

1. HACC realleges the preceding allegations.
2. HACC controlled or materially influenced the voucher, relocation, document-review, notice, and hearing-access process needed for the household to avoid displacement.
3. HACC imposed extensive and escalating documentation demands even after substantial materials had already been produced.
4. HACC also delayed voucher processing, reversed the earlier two-bedroom voucher path, partially recognized but did not fully provide the requested accommodation, failed to timely honor the live-in aide and emotional-support-animal components of the accommodation request, attempted to direct the household toward the inaccessible Easton Ridge one-bedroom third-floor unit, treated Julio's household status inconsistently across lease, relocation, and voucher communications, and failed to provide a timely and meaningful hearing process for Julio Cortez. Exhibits 3, 4, 7, 8, and 10.
5. These acts wrongfully burdened and delayed the household's ability to complete relocation and avoid eviction, caused confusion about who HACC regarded as part of the household and on what effective date, caused compensable harm, and forced the household to respond to housing options that were never realistically usable by Jane Cortez or consistent with the provider-supported accommodation record.

### Counterclaim Five: Retaliation / Interference With Protected Complaints and Housing Rights

1. Defendants reallege the preceding allegations.
2. The household complained to HACC and Quantum about discrimination, including race discrimination and housing steering, service-animal issues, unlawful housing administration, and the failure to process the Blossom application.
3. Those complaints included written assertions that Blossom had refused to process the household's housing application for months, that the opportunity was being handled on unequal terms, and that the household intended to enforce civil-rights protections.
4. HACC knew of those complaints and nevertheless continued or escalated notice activity, documentation burdens, voucher delay, and relocation obstruction.
5. The household was thereby burdened, delayed, and exposed to eviction in part because it complained and attempted to enforce housing rights.
6. Defendants seek all relief allowed by law for that retaliatory or interfering conduct.

## Counterclaim / Third-Party Theory Against Quantum Residential

### Counterclaim / Third-Party Claim One: Breach of Intake-Processing and Relocation-Path Duties

1. Defendants reallege the preceding allegations.
2. Quantum was not a stranger to the relocation process; HACC's own records place Quantum inside the same replacement-housing and RAD/PBV portfolio used for Hillside Park relocation.
3. Defendants submitted intake/application materials through Quantum-side staff in the relocation pathway.
4. HACC later acknowledged that the packet had been submitted to Quantum staff but had not been provided onward to HACC.
5. The present record further supports an inference that the delay problem was likely to recur within Quantum-managed relocation properties, because the household describes a months-long Blossom nonprocessing episode and the later Parkside Heights application path, also involving a Quantum-managed property, remained delayed and conditional during the same relocation crisis.
6. Quantum thereby failed to process, transmit, review, and/or respond to the household's packet in the manner required by the operative intake path.
7. That failure materially obstructed the relocation process and contributed to the circumstances now used to justify eviction.

### Counterclaim / Third-Party Claim Two: Negligent or Intentional Interference With Relocation Performance

1. Quantum knew or should have known that packet receipt and transmission affected whether the household could complete the replacement-housing path.
2. By receiving but not transmitting the packet, and by allegedly denying receipt after submission, Quantum interfered with the household's performance of the relocation path.
3. Defendants were harmed by the resulting delay, lost placement opportunity, and exposure to eviction while relocation remained incomplete.

### Counterclaim / Third-Party Claim Three: Race-Based Interference With Housing Opportunity / Contracting Path

1. Defendants reallege the preceding allegations.
2. The Blossom application and placement process was a housing-related contracting and tenancy opportunity tied to the household's displacement and voucher-backed relocation path.
3. Quantum controlled or materially participated in the intake, application, processing, and placement steps for that opportunity.
4. Defendants allege that they submitted the Blossom application, that it was accepted in person, and that no timely approval, denial, ineligibility determination, or lawful written deficiency notice was ever issued.
5. Defendants further allege that they complained in writing that the Blossom path was being handled on unequal racial terms and that other displaced households moved forward while their application never did.
6. Defendants further allege that the county-linked housing policy environment expressly used racial-equity framing and BIPOC-focused outcome language, but Defendants rely on that policy environment only as context and notice, not as the sole proof of liability.
7. Defendants rely on the circulated DEI- and retaliation-related review material only as context for why those race-discrimination concerns were communicated, not as a direct admission by Quantum.
8. Quantum's alleged refusal to process or honor the housing opportunity on unequal terms further impaired the household's ability to make and enforce the housing-related arrangement needed to avoid displacement.

### Counterclaim / Third-Party Claim Four: Service-Animal and Housing-Access Obstruction at Blossom

1. Quantum participated in the Blossom-side housing-access process for a HACC-linked relocation opportunity.
2. The household alleges that Quantum not only failed to process the application, but also refused or failed to accommodate service-animal-related tenancy issues in that housing path.
3. That conduct further obstructed the household's access to replacement housing and contributed to the loss of housing opportunity and resulting eviction exposure.

## Additional Performance Allegation Relevant to All Defenses and Counterclaims

1. Defendants deny that they failed to pursue available relocation options in good faith.
2. The current record shows that after HACC identified Parkside Heights as an available option, Defendants completed and submitted the Parkside Heights application.
3. Parkside Heights then acknowledged receipt of that application.
4. To the extent no Parkside tenancy was finalized, the current record indicates that the process remained subject to further approval, unit availability, construction completion, and related conditions not solely within Defendants' control.
5. Defendants therefore allege substantial compliance with the application-submission step of the Parkside pathway and deny that the failure of that path can be attributed solely to Defendants.
