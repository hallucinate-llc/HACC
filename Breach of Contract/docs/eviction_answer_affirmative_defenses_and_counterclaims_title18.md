## Preliminary Statement

This eviction action cannot be fairly decided as a simple noncompliance or holdover case because the alleged basis for eviction arose in the middle of an uncompleted Section 18 relocation process triggered by demolition/redevelopment of Hillside Park. HACC's own records show that the relocation process was activated no later than September 19, 2024, that replacement housing opportunities such as Blossom and Hillside Manor were part of the same demolition-replacement pathway, and that Quantum Residential received but did not transmit the household's intake packet to HACC. Defendants therefore assert that Plaintiff's own prior material breaches, and the related interference of Quantum Residential in the same relocation sequence, bar or reduce the relief sought in this FED action.

## Affirmative Defenses

### First Affirmative Defense: Prior Material Breach by Plaintiff

1. Before Plaintiff sought eviction, Plaintiff had already materially breached duties arising from the operative relocation process.
2. Those breaches included failure to complete relocation counseling, failure to commit or pay relocation expenses, failure to furnish comparable replacement housing, and pursuit of eviction before relocation was complete.
3. A party in prior material breach may not obtain relief based on alleged downstream nonperformance caused or aggravated by its own breach.
4. Plaintiff's claims are therefore barred in whole or in part.

### Second Affirmative Defense: Prevention / Hindrance of Performance

1. Plaintiff and/or its relocation agents prevented or hindered Defendants' ability to complete the relocation path now invoked as a basis for eviction.
2. HACC directed the household into replacement-housing paths involving Parkside Heights and Blossom/Quantum-side packet handling.
3. Defendants submitted the Parkside Heights application after HACC identified that option and stated the required next steps.
4. Parkside Heights acknowledged receipt of the application but advised that the requested two-bedroom units were still under construction.
5. Quantum also received the Blossom-side packet, but HACC later acknowledged that the packet had not been transmitted to HACC.
6. The present record also supports an inference that this was not a one-off breakdown, but a recurring delay problem within the same Quantum-managed relocation network.
7. Plaintiff also attempted to route the household toward a one-bedroom third-floor Easton Ridge placement that was facially incompatible with Jane Cortez's use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter and with her inability to safely manage stairs.
8. Plaintiff also failed to timely provide the accommodation package that the provider verification supported: a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver sleeping arrangement, and accommodation for Sarah as an emotional support dog. Exhibits 2 and 3.
9. Plaintiff also treated Julio's household status inconsistently by taking or being accused of taking lease-side action in December 2025, relying on paperwork effective January 1, 2026, then stating on January 12, 2026 that Julio was still a household member unless court papers were submitted, and then reducing the household to one-bedroom eligibility by January 27, 2026. Exhibits 4 and 10.
10. That inconsistency was preceded by written HACC notice in late November 2025, when Ashley Ferron had already forwarded Julio-related issues to HACC Public Housing and Relocation and asked for legal documentation concerning who was and was not in Jane Cortez's household. Exhibit 4.
11. Plaintiff may not rely on noncompletion of a process that Plaintiff and its associated intake pathway materially obstructed, delayed, partially recognized, or left conditional beyond the stated vacate timeline.

### Third Affirmative Defense: Failure of Condition Precedent

1. Completion of statutorily and administratively required relocation steps was a necessary condition precedent to lawful displacement.
2. Those conditions included, at minimum, counseling, relocation expense support, and a comparable replacement path.
3. Plaintiff sought eviction without satisfying those conditions precedent.
4. The action is therefore premature and should be dismissed or denied.

### Fourth Affirmative Defense: Unclean Hands / Equitable Bar

1. Plaintiff seeks equitable or possession-based relief while acting inequitably in the same transaction.
2. Plaintiff's inequitable conduct includes pursuing eviction while relocation remained incomplete, while a one-bedroom third-floor unit at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, was being treated as a replacement option for Jane Cortez despite her inability to safely climb stairs and despite her documented use of a cane, walker, and mobility scooter, while excessive documentation demands were still being used in the relocation and voucher path, while Julio household-status treatment remained internally inconsistent, while the household alleges a restrained non-household actor continued influencing HACC's lease handling, and while hearing access for Julio Cortez remained impaired. Exhibits 4, 7, 10, and 12.
3. Plaintiff is barred by unclean hands from obtaining the equitable relief sought, including immediate possession.

### Fifth Affirmative Defense: Disability / Accessibility Failure

1. Plaintiff knew or should have known that Jane Cortez had serious stair and mobility limitations, could barely walk, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter.
2. Plaintiff also knew or should have known from the accommodation record that provider verification supported a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, that Benjamin Barber functioned as the needed live-in aide or caregiver with a separate sleeping arrangement, and that Sarah served as an emotional support dog alleviating disability-related symptoms. Exhibits 2 and 3.
3. Plaintiff further knew or should have known that Jane Cortez's disability-related limitations were longstanding and substantial because Jane had a representative payee for years, Benjamin Barber had been handling her affairs, and the guardian ad litem order for Benjamin Barber was part of the same Jane Cortez v. Julio Cortez court-process materials through which HACC was receiving notice of Jane's welfare, safety, and household-composition issues. Exhibits 4 and 13.
4. Plaintiff nevertheless failed to provide an accessible and effective relocation path and instead offered a one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, that was not feasible for her given those limitations.
5. Plaintiff cannot base eviction relief on alleged failure to relocate where the replacement path was not reasonably accessible or where the accommodation was only partially recognized but not actually provided in practice.

### Sixth Affirmative Defense: Overbroad Verification / Accommodation Delay

1. Even if some targeted screening or income verification was permissible, Plaintiff was required to keep any information demands reasonably tied to current countable income, live-in-aide or caregiver status, and the disability-related accommodation request.
2. Defendants allege that Plaintiff instead expanded the process into repeated and escalating demands for tax, banking, crypto, entity, and business records, including records tied to companies Defendants stated had not existed for about 10 years. Exhibits 7, 8, and 9.
3. Defendants further allege that Plaintiff continued those demands even after being told that county-side representative-payee and Money Management personnel were affecting Jane Cortez's rent and payment handling, and even while voucher, relocation, and accommodation issues remained unresolved. Exhibits 4, 7, 8, and 9.
4. Plaintiff may not rely on delay or noncompletion that Plaintiff materially caused by imposing overbroad, stale, or unnecessarily burdensome verification demands during the accommodation and relocation process.

### Seventh Affirmative Defense: Setoff / Recoupment

1. Defendants have suffered damages, delay, housing instability, administrative burden, and other compensable harm caused by Plaintiff's relocation failures.
2. To the extent Plaintiff claims rent, holdover damages, or other monetary relief, Defendants assert setoff and/or recoupment in an amount to be proven.

### Eighth Affirmative Defense: Retaliatory or Pretextual Escalation

1. Defendants complained to HACC and Quantum about discrimination, including race discrimination, housing steering, service-animal issues, housing administration failures, nonprocessing of the Blossom application, and related legal violations.
2. Those complaints included written assertions that the Blossom opportunity was being handled on unequal terms and that civil-rights violations were occurring in the housing-access and contracting path.
3. After those complaints, HACC and Quantum continued or escalated adverse actions including notice activity, documentation burdens, delay in voucher processing, reversal of the two-bedroom voucher path, and continued obstruction of the relocation process.
4. Plaintiff may not obtain possession based on a sequence materially infected by retaliatory, pretextual, complaint-driven, or unlawfully discriminatory escalation.

### Ninth Affirmative Defense: Denial of Meaningful Hearing and Process

1. Plaintiff and its associated housing-administration process affected the household's rights, obligations, welfare, and continued occupancy without providing a meaningful and accessible opportunity to be heard.
2. The present record supports the allegation that Julio Cortez requested a hearing orally, that HACC insisted on a written request despite knowing he could not write in any language, and that no meaningful hearing was provided before threatened housing loss. Exhibits 4, 8, 11, and 12.
3. The same process was further clouded by HACC's inconsistent treatment of whether Julio remained part of the household, even while HACC was already acting on changed household composition for lease and voucher purposes. Exhibits 4 and 10.
4. That process was preceded by written HACC notice in late November 2025, when Ashley Ferron had already forwarded Julio-related issues to HACC Public Housing and Relocation and asked for legal documentation concerning who was and was not in Jane Cortez's household, yet Plaintiff still failed to maintain a consistent position thereafter. Exhibit 4.
5. The eviction should therefore be denied, stayed, or otherwise limited because the underlying process was not lawfully completed.

## Counterclaims Against HACC

1. Defendants reallege the preceding allegations.
2. By reason of the Phase II approval and demolition-related relocation process, HACC undertook enforceable relocation obligations to the affected household.
3. Those obligations included relocation counseling, relocation-expense support, and furnishing comparable replacement housing before displacement was completed.
4. HACC materially breached those obligations by:
   - failing to provide relocation counseling;
   - failing to commit or pay relocation expenses;
   - failing to furnish comparable replacement housing;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported two-bedroom, first-floor accommodation needed for Jane Cortez's disability-related mobility and caregiving needs;
   - failing to provide the provider-supported live-in aide or caregiver arrangement with a separate sleeping area;
   - failing to accommodate Sarah as an emotional support dog in the replacement-housing pathway despite provider verification that Sarah alleviated disability-related symptoms;
   - offering a non-accessible one-bedroom unit on the third floor at Easton Ridge Apartments, another Quantum-managed property, despite knowing Jane Cortez could barely walk, used a cane, and often needed a walker or mobility scooter; and
   - pursuing eviction before relocation was complete.

2. HACC should reasonably have expected Defendants to rely on those representations.

HACC knew or should have known that provider verification supported a comparable replacement path consisting of a two-bedroom, first-floor arrangement, a workable live-in aide or caregiver setup with a separate sleeping area, and space and permission for Sarah as an emotional support dog.
