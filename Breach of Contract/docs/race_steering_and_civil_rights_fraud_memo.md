# Race Steering and Civil Rights Fraud Memo

## Purpose

This memo separates three different issues that should not be blended together in court papers:

1. current federal enforcement positions about race-based discrimination in federally funded programs,
2. local SHS / RLRA / Metro / HACC policy language in the workspace,
3. how those theories should be used in the state FED case versus a broader federal civil-rights case.

## Bottom Line

The strongest filing-safe theory is not that every use of the word `DEI` is automatically unlawful. The stronger and more defensible theory is:

- HACC, Quantum, and related county-linked housing actors were operating inside a publicly stated race-equity policy environment;
- you made written complaints about race discrimination, race steering, and unequal treatment in the Blossom housing path;
- the Blossom path was a housing-related application / placement / contracting opportunity;
- you allege that opportunity was withheld, delayed, or obstructed on unequal racial terms;
- after those complaints, the same actors allegedly escalated notices, documentation burdens, nonprocessing, and relocation obstruction.

If a federally funded housing recipient knowingly used race-based preferences, steering, exclusions, or unequal treatment while certifying compliance with federal law, that is where current DOJ `civil rights fraud` framing may become relevant.

## Current Federal Authority

### DOJ position on unlawful discrimination in programs labeled DEI

The current DOJ position is that a program is not lawful merely because it is labeled `DEI`, `equity`, or similar terms. If a federally funded program uses protected traits such as race in a discriminatory way, DOJ says federal antidiscrimination law still applies.

- On May 19, 2025, DOJ announced the `Civil Rights Fraud Initiative`, stating it would use the False Claims Act to pursue recipients of federal funds that knowingly violate federal civil-rights laws.
  Source: https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-establishes-civil-rights-fraud-initiative

- On July 30, 2025, DOJ released guidance for recipients of federal funding stating that federal antidiscrimination laws apply to programs involving discriminatory practices, including programs labeled `DEI`.
  Source: https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-releases-guidance-recipients-federal-funding-regarding-unlawful

- DOJ Civil Rights Division currently provides a reporting portal specifically for people alleging illegal discrimination resulting from `diversity, equity, and inclusion` programs.
  Source: https://civilrights.justice.gov/diversity-equity-inclusion-illegal-discrimination

### What this does and does not prove

These federal materials support the proposition that:

- race-based discrimination remains unlawful even when wrapped in `equity` language,
- federal funding recipients can face enforcement consequences for knowingly maintaining unlawful discriminatory practices,
- and the federal government currently treats some race-based DEI practices as possible civil-rights violations and, in some settings, potential False Claims Act problems.

These materials do not by themselves prove that every race-conscious policy statement is illegal, nor do they by themselves prove that Clackamas County, Metro, SHS, HACC, or Quantum committed fraud. For that, you still need the facts showing actual race-based treatment, steering, preference, exclusion, or false certification.

### HUD / housing-specific relevance

HUD's fair-housing materials remain directly relevant to housing steering and unequal access.

- HUD's Housing Choice Voucher fair-housing guidance remains a primary housing source for the proposition that steering and unequal treatment in housing access are unlawful.
  Source: https://www.hud.gov/sites/dfiles/PIH/documents/HCV_Guidebook-Chapter_Fair-Housing_April-2025.pdf

- HUD also announced on September 15, 2025 that it was investigating Boston's race-based housing plan under the Fair Housing Act and Title VI. That is useful as current evidence that HUD is treating race-based housing allocation or preference questions as active federal enforcement issues.
  Source: https://www.hud.gov/news/hud-no-25-147

## Best Legal Characterization of the Local Policy Language

The local SHS / RLRA language is strong evidence of a race-conscious policy environment. It is not yet, by itself, proof of a facially unlawful race quota or explicit race bar in your individual housing placement.

The safest characterizations are:

- `race-conscious policy language`
- `racial-equity-directed program design`
- `BIPOC-focused targeting language`
- `policy environment encouraging race-aware housing administration`

The riskier characterizations, unless supported by more contract text or implementation records, are:

- `explicit race quota`
- `minority-only eligibility`
- `direct facial exclusion of non-BIPOC applicants`

So the current record supports saying that the local program language openly prioritized racial-equity objectives and BIPOC-centered outcomes. It does not yet conclusively establish that your specific denial was required by a formal race-exclusive rule.

## What Belongs in the FED Filing

The FED filing should stay narrow and practical. The best use there is:

- you complained about race discrimination and housing steering,
- those complaints were part of the protected-activity sequence,
- the Blossom path was a housing-related contracting and tenancy opportunity,
- you allege it was handled on unequal terms,
- and adverse action escalated after those complaints.

That use is already reflected in:

- [eviction_answer_affirmative_defenses_and_counterclaims_title18.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Breach%20of%20Contract/docs/eviction_answer_affirmative_defenses_and_counterclaims_title18.md)
- [fed_combined_answer_counterclaims_and_joinder_package.md](/home/barberb/HACC/Breach%20of%20Contract/docs/fed_combined_answer_counterclaims_and_joinder_package.md)

The FED court does not need a broad abstract argument that all DEI is unconstitutional. It needs a concrete explanation of why possession should be denied:

- the relocation path was incomplete,
- the replacement path was obstructed,
- the housing opportunity was allegedly handled on unequal terms,
- and the eviction sequence was infected by retaliation, discrimination complaints, and procedural unfairness.

## What Belongs in a Separate Federal / Administrative Theory

The broader theory can go much further, but it should usually live in:

- a federal complaint,
- a HUD / DOJ / OCR / Title VI complaint,
- a False Claims Act / civil-rights-fraud research memo,
- or discovery targeted at SHS / Metro / HACC contracts and certifications.

That broader theory would ask:

- Did Metro, SHS, HACC, or county contractors use race as a selection, targeting, referral, or allocation criterion?
- Did contracts or grant certifications represent compliance with Title VI, the Fair Housing Act, or equal-protection principles while implementation materially departed from those obligations?
- Did local housing actors use race-explicit goals in a way that became race-explicit placement or access decisions?
- Did those policies shape Blossom, referral, or relocation outcomes in your case?

## What Would Strengthen the Bigger Theory

The best missing evidence would be:

- SHS / Metro / HACC contracts with nondiscrimination and certification clauses
- referral or prioritization matrices showing race-coded or BIPOC-targeted scoring
- internal training, implementation guidance, or dashboards showing race-based selection rules
- communications showing that race affected placement, interview priority, or application handling
- funding certifications or grant assurances that can be compared against actual race-based program operation

## Recommended Phrasing

Use this in a careful way:

`The local SHS/RLRA materials expressly adopt a racial-equity framework centered on BIPOC outcomes. Under current DOJ guidance, federal funding recipients may not use race in a discriminatory manner merely because the practice is labeled equity or DEI. The present record does not yet prove every element of a broader civil-rights-fraud theory, but it does support a narrower allegation that plaintiffs complained about race discrimination and steering in a housing-contracting path and then faced continued obstruction and adverse action.`

Avoid this stronger phrasing unless you have more direct proof:

`The contract itself conclusively proves a race quota or fraud.`

## Practical Conclusion

Right now, the best litigation posture is:

- in the FED case: use race discrimination, race steering, and `1981` only as part of the protected-complaint, unequal-treatment, and prevention/retaliation story;
- in the broader civil-rights case: preserve the stronger argument that race-targeted housing administration under SHS / Metro / HACC may violate federal law and, if knowingly certified as lawful, may implicate DOJ's current civil-rights-fraud framework.
