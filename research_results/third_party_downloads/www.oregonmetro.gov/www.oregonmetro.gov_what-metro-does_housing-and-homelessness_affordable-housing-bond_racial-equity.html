Racial equity and housing

Due to many decades of racist policy and practice, Black and Indigenous and other people of color have long faced unequal access to quality, affordable housing. Due to these inequities, thousands of people throughout the region have not been and are still not able to access quality education, career jobs, public transit, parks, health care and other things they need to thrive. That’s why advancing racial equity is a core value of Metro’s affordable housing bond program.

Increasing the stock of affordable housing, gathering community input in the pre-development process, and ensuring this new housing is equitably distributed to those who need it most, is crucial to remedying the history of harm to Black, Indigenous and other communities of color.

Metro advances racial equity in the following key areas.

  • Promoting fair and equitable access

    All bond projects are required to develop an outreach plan to ensure communities of color — and other groups who have been harmed by past policy and practice — are aware of new housing opportunities. For example, by placing ads in publications that are written for specific cultural audiences or translating marketing information into different languages.

    Bond partners also must make sure they are taking steps to reduce barriers in the application process. This might look like changing screening criteria to eliminate credit scores, limiting criminal background checks to only consider major crimes, or not charging application fees.

    Data from developments that have reached full occupancy suggest that these strategies are working: there are a higher percentage of people of color living in bond-funded homes than the percentage of households that are people of color in the surrounding neighborhoods and the region as a whole.

  • Culturally specific and culturally responsive resident services

    All services provided in bond-funded projects are expected to be culturally responsive. Culturally responsive services are respectful of, and relevant to, the beliefs, practices, culture and linguistic needs of diverse resident populations and communities. Connections to services provided by culturally specific organizations are also prioritized.

    Culturally specific organizations are nonprofits that serve a particular community of color, where the majority of staff and members/clients are from the community being served. Bond partners contract with organizations that have a track record of successful community engagement and involvement with the community being served.

  • Geographic distribution of housing

    Metro’s housing location strategy aims to create affordable homes all over the region instead of concentrating it in specific neighborhoods — a common practice in the past.

    Across the United States, for many years, local governments and other entities used policies like zoning regulations and legally binding language in property deeds to exclude people of color — especially Black people — from living in many neighborhoods.  That’s why the Metro bond is bringing affordable housing to neighborhoods that are rich with amenities and have traditionally excluded people of color — whether intentionally or due to a lack of options for low-income households and renters.

    In other parts of the region, communities of color are threatened with or have already experienced displacement in neighborhoods where they have established roots, due to urban renewal and gentrification. In response, Metro’s partners are using bond funding to create affordable homes in places where communities of color live today, to ensure long-term housing stability.

    This strategy includes partnering with the Portland Housing Bureau to create more affordable housing in Portland’s historically Black neighborhoods, so those who have been displaced, and their family members, have the opportunity to return.

  • Increasing economic opportunity through construction

    The bond program aims to create business and workforce opportunities for people who have traditionally been underrepresented in the construction trades, ensuring at least 20% of construction funding goes to women and minority-owned businesses. By December 2024, the program has exceeded this goal, with 29% ($165 million) of construction contract funds going toward these businesses.

    In addition to contracting goals, the bond program encourages partner jurisdictions to set goals for and track workforce diversity. While only Portland has established specific goals, 68% of bond-funded projects have committed to reporting on workforce diversity. This data will help establish a baseline on which future goals can be established.
