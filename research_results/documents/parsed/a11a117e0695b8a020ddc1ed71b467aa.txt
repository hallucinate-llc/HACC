Business Oregon is undertaking a strategic assessment of incentives to 1) examine how well
our legacy incentive programs are aligned with our current strategic priorities, 2) review the
effectiveness and efficiency of the state’s incentive management practices, and 3) provide
recommendations for improvement.

We have contracted with Smart Incentives to conduct this assessment. Smart Incentives, a
national leader on state and local incentive policies, works with economic development
organizations to help them manage their incentive programs effectively and responsibly.

Would you be available for a 30 minute confidential conversation with Ellen Harpel, Smart
Incentives founder and president, to share your perspective on how Oregon’s incentive portfolio
performs and affects our state’s competitiveness? We would value your thoughts on how well
incentives work now and how they might be improved.

If you are willing, below are some suggested dates and times for either a telephone call or video
conference, depending on your preference. If these dates are not convenient, please feel free to
suggest others and we will do our best to make them work.

It is likely that small and middle market companies use the Enterprise Zone to help
finance their growth plans. If “small and middle market companies’ can be defined by
employment and possibly NAICS code, then it might be technically possible for Business
Oregon to use data already collected to report on the number and percentage of
recipients in this category.
It may also be possible to enhance reporting to provide a more complete picture of
Enterprise Zone activities in rural areas that cultivate rural economic stability, including
metrics for rural small businesses. This approach would require rethinking forms and
procedures through which data are gathered and would need to be pursued in
consultation with local and state partners involved in Enterprise Zone reporting. It would
likely require additional staff, funding, and data management resources.
Business Oregon already works to enhance local economic development capacity
regarding incentive use through in-person workshops and, lately, via webinar offerings.
Stakeholder interviews suggest that local organizations may also benefit from technical
assistance on strategic approaches to incentives that would help communities market
the program, better link their Enterprise Zone to other economic development initiatives,
and leverage the opportunity the program provides to incorporate conditions beyond the
essential statutory requirements into their agreements with recipients. Additional
resources would be needed to provide more regular, rigorous and sophisticated
technical assistance and support.
Lack of follow-up on FSHA agreements executed as part of the approval process
represents a gap in the system. While the agreements may be in place, there is no data
suggesting how often companies actually work with FSHA partners or how many
individuals have been hired as a result of the agreements. More insight into this element
of the program could support the priorities to grow small and middle market companies
and advance economic opportunity for underrepresented people.
Similarly, it should be possible, if not easy, to share outcomes and good practices from
the communities that include additional conditions in their agreements. Insights from this
source may illuminate ways Oregon communities are working toward several of the
priorities and supporting the strategies.
It does not appear that data is collected to indicate the ownership characteristics of
participating businesses. Business Oregon could consider collecting demographic data
to determine whether the program assists underrepresented entrepreneurs and small
business owners.
An impressive amount of transactional and compliance data is collected and reported for
the Enterprise Zone program. Stakeholders also expressed interest in understanding the
longer term contributions that the program makes to state and local economies. Some
questions to consider could include: How have area economic conditions changed since
zone designation, and how have the incentivized investments fared over time once they
exit the program? Do they continue to generate economic and fiscal benefits for the
community?
The reporting process is labor intensive and appears to rely heavily on one person’s
knowledge of the procedures. Should reporting processes be codified and should
information systems for managing data be improved (via an online portal, for example) ?
What resources (funds, expertise, time) might be available? Can resources and support
be obtained via legislation?
The 2009 study suggested that the program does not seem to be working for “rural
remote’ areas. Is there an opportunity to compare successful and non-successful zones
(or zones with at least one project vs. those with none), perhaps as part of the capacity
building strategy?

Gaps, Data Shortcomings and Opportunities for Improvement

   e    This incentive program lacks a specifically stated purpose in statute. The objective
        should be specified.
   e        Companies may be willing to share some facility-wide data on their workforce that could
            be combined and reported at an aggregated level to understand whether
            underrepresented people are participating in the opportunities these investments
            provide. However, any such data request would be voluntary, and companies may
            choose not to provide it. The potential value of the data would need to be weighed
            against the time and resources required to ask for, obtain, and manage the information.
   e    There are a small number of program participants, so case studies may provide one way
        to demonstrate how incentivized investments help cultivate rural economic stability over
        time.

The SRF application and approval process may be streamlined to work more efficiently
for Business Oregon and incentive applicants by removing snags, bottlenecks,
redundancies that have accumulated as the program has aged and to provide more
speed and clarity in the application review procedures. Such a review should strive to
maintain the accountability measures that have been incorporated into the program over
time.
Business Oregon may consider expanding its public reporting for the SRF program so
that both costs and benefits become clearer. It would be valuable to be able to state
more definitively that SRF-funded projects support Business Oregon's strategic
priorities, rather than suggesting that they might do so, but that we are not sure. More
insight on the outcomes associated with the negotiated benefit agreements that are
considered a critical element of program accountability would also be useful for
understanding the full value of SRF-funded activities. As with the other incentive
programs considered in this analysis, the time and resources needed to make more
information available would need to be balanced with confidentiality requirements, staff
capacity, and level of interest for additional information among stakeholders.
The SRF statute and administrative rules indicate a broad economic development
mandate, and the program's discretionary, forgivable loan structure make it a unique and
potentially attractive option for a variety of business development initiatives. There may
be an opportunity to deploy SRF in new ways to support Business Oregon’s strategic
priorities, especially regarding innovation, growth of small and medium enterprises, and
rural economic stability. One option would be to convene a working group of partners
within Business Oregon, local economic development partners and allies, and the
Governor's office to assess the potential for using SRF for additional purposes.

The Rural Technical Assistance Program (RTAP), formerly known as Colorado Blueprint 2.0,
provides free technical assistance, consulting, and funding to help rural communities create
economic development strategies.

Employee Ownership Network (EON) seeks to keep rural firms facing a generational shift in
ownership (i.e., owner retiring) from relocating under new ownership or shutting down due to
lack of a buyer. EON is supported by a $3 million revolving loan fund that makes loan up to
$10,000 to assist with legal, accounting, and business advisory costs associated with
transitioning to 100% employee ownership. Firm revenues must be less than $5 million to
access the RLF.

The Colorado Rural Jump-Start Zone program provides tax relief to businesses and employees
of these businesses that are located in designated economically distressed areas of rural
Colorado. 14 companies are participating in the Rural Jump-Start Zone program as of June 30,
2020, and 16 counties have formed Rural Jump-Start zones, and 24 more counties were eligible
to form zones but have not done so.                        The program is only open to newly created businesses
that do compete with an existing business in that Rural Jump-Start Zone.                      Participating firms
receive relief from all state and local business taxes for a four year period.

the myriad of certifications available as a minority, veteran or woman-owned business.
Businesses learn how to best market themselves as a certified business and secure
government contracts. In 2020 MBO assisted 113 businesses, with 42 attaining MBE
certification and winning $167.4 million in contract awards.

Strategic Fund Job Growth Incentive
              Basic benefit for business firms: Performance-based cash grant for competitive
              projects with significant job creation. Cash payments range from $3,000 — 6,500
              per new job created, for a negotiated period of up to five years.
              Geography: statewide, however larger per job created awards possible in
              economically disadvantaged counties.
              Business eligibility: no quantitative requirement beyond 1:1 required local match;
              project must be multi-state and/or internationally competitive, entail significant
              capital investment, and create permanent, net new full-time jobs. A company
              may receive either the Strategic Fund Job Growth Incentive or the Job Growth
              Incentive Tax Credit for any group of new jobs, not both.
              Minimum employment requirement: none.
              Employee compensation requirement: jobs must pay at least 100% of average
              county wage.
              Local hiring or workforce cooperation requirement: none.
              Local government role: must provide 1:1 match and must be done in a manner
              that is not commonly offered to all businesses in the jurisdiction.   Common forms
              are cash, sales or property tax abatement, reduction in permitting fees.     Private
              contributions acting on behalf of the local government are acceptable.
              Use/Outcomes reported:       The Economic Development Commission (EDC)
              approved six new Strategic Fund projects in FY20, with anticipated job total job
              creation of 971. Total Strategic Fund commitment to the six projects was

Idaho Global Entrepreneurial Mission (IGEM) — IGEM is a three- prong program that invests
public funds in advanced university research, capacity building and commercialization initiatives
to further economic development efforts across the state. By connecting Idaho businesses with
university research capabilities, IGEM is helping to develop new products, grow companies and
attract investment, while elevating research capacities at Idaho’s universities.   The IGEM-
Commerce program receives $1 million annually for commercialization grants, and has funded
36 projects, providing $7.7 million over its eight years in existence. Idaho has 5% research tax
credit.




Idaho Commerce does not heavily emphasize small and middle market companies in its
presentation of services, but several of the incentive and finance programs would be accessible
to small and mid-sized businesses. All businesses are eligible for a 3% investment tax credit on
all new tangible personal property (machinery and equipment) and a personal property tax
exemption on the first $100,000 of personal property.




The Rural Economic Development Program was created to support economic development
projects in rural communities. The program awards grants, used in conjunction with local
funding, to hire full-time economic development professionals. These experts are managed by a
local board of directors and work on initiatives including business retention, expansion, export
assistance, tourism, and entrepreneurial development. This State-funded grant is a component
of the Idaho Rural Initiative. 18 professionals funded in FY 2020 at annual rates ranging from
$17,500 to $30,000; total program budget was $500,000. 30 of Idaho’s 44 countries are eligible
for this program; some of the 18 economic development professionals cover only one county,
while other cover a multi-county area.

Real Property Tax:                          Minnesota has an extremely complicated system for determining a
property’s annual property tax. First, the property’s market value is multiplied by the “class rate”
to determine its “tax capacity” for that year. Importantly, the system discriminates against
commercial and industrial property as the class rates for residential property are 1.0% up to
$500,000 in market value, and 1.25% over $500,000; by comparison, the class rates for
commercial and industrial are 1.5% for the first $150,000 in market value, and 2.0% over
$150,000.

Once the tax capacity is determined by the calculation above, state and local levy rates are
applied to determine the ultimate property tax bill. Thus, while Minnesota ranks only 21° per
Tax Foundation for state and local property tax collections, with an average per capita collection
of $1,599, the business levy is likely to rank much higher.

Economic Development Tax Increment Financing (EDTIF)
             Basic benefit for business firms: tax rebate program of up to 30%, over the life of
             the project, of new state taxes paid for up to 20 years. Typical length of project
             agreement is five to ten years. No more than 50% of overall project credit may
             be claimed in any one year.
             Geography: statewide
             Business eligibility: business must be in operation for at least three years and
             consistently profitable; project must compete with another state; business must
             be in one of the state’s target industries; retail businesses ineligible.
             Minimum employment requirement: create at least 50 new jobs in urban
             counties (no minimum in rural counties).
             Employee compensation requirement:         pay at least   110% of average county
             wage.
             Local hiring or workforce cooperation requirement: none
             Local government role: commitment from local government to provide local
             incentives and establish an Economic Development Zone.
             Use/Outcomes reported:     in FY 2020, 21 companies received approval from the
             GOED Board to participate in the EDTIF program. These participants are
             collectively projected to create 13,364 new jobs, $1,133,129,906 in capital
             investment, over $590,977,656 in new state revenue, and $9,738,589,715 in new
             state wages.
             Every three years, the EDTIF program is required to undergo a third-party review
             to ensure its meeting and accurately providing information for its statutory,
             ethical, and reporting obligations. The most recent report, completed by the
             Tanner CPA firm in the summer of 2020, shows the EDTIF program continues to
             provide a positive return on investment to the state of Utah, and that outcomes
             for new incremental and high-paying jobs are reported accurately and according
             to legislative intent. For FY 17, 18, and 19, new State of Utah revenues as a
             percentage of program expenses & tax credits/rebates were 339%, 360%, and
             383%, respectively.

Business & Occupation (B&O) Tax Incentives for Aerospace
            Basic benefit for business firms: Package of tax incentives targeted at
            aerospace industry (both OEM, i.e., Boeing, as well as manufacturing and
            services portions of supply chain). Most/all date back to successful effort in the
            2000s to secure production of the Boeing 787 series airliners in Washington (a
            second production site has since been opened outside Charleston, SC).
            Credits for preproduction development expenditures; property/leasehold taxes
            paid on aerospace business facilities; retail sales/use tax exemption for
            construction materials used in commercial aircraft wing, fuselage, and final
            assembly; and aircraft MRO (maintenance repair & overhaul) activities remain in
            place. Sales tax exemption for computer hardware/software/peripherals used by
            aerospace businesses remains in place. Preferential B&O tax rate for aerospace
            businesses repealed 4/1/2020 at Boeing’s request to head off adverse World
            Trade Organization ruling that would have permitted retaliatory sanctions by
            European Union member nations against a wide range of U.S. (not just
            Washington State) exports.
            Geography:    statewide
             Business eligibility: operate within defined aerospace industry codes
             Minimum employment requirement: none noted
             Employee compensation requirement:         none noted
             Local hiring or workforce cooperation requirement:      none noted
            Local government role: none noted
            Use/Outcomes reported: 371 companies benefited from the B&O preferential
            rate as of 2018. Boeing was the biggest beneficiary by an overwhelming margin,
            with an estimated tax savings of $100 million that year; the next closest firm had
            a tax savings of $884,000.    The third through tenth ranking firms reported
            savings of $854,000 to $322,000; the remaining 361 firms collectively reporting
            savings of $10,689,095 (average of $29,609 per firm — in aerospace enough to
            fund probably 4 to 1/3 FTE).     Ending the preferential rate is estimated to yield an
            additional $229 million in revenue during the 2021-23 biennium.
            General comment on B&O tax incentives — since the B&O tax, as a gross
            receipts tax, impacts virtually all for profit firms, it can be an effective vehicle for
            providing meaningful benefits to a broad spectrum of firms.        Contrasted with a
            corporate income tax scheme, where unprofitable (e.g., many/most start-ups)
            firms receive no benefit unless the state allows them to monetize losses (typically

The California Competes Tax Credit is an income tax credit available to businesses that want to
locate in California or stay and grow in California. The income tax credit is non-refundable but
allows for a 6 year carryover. Over $231 million in tax credits were available for allocation in the
2020-21 fiscal year. No more than 20% of the allocation may go to any one applicant per fiscal
year.

Businesses apply online for the tax credit and go through a two-phase review and approval
process. California typically offers three application periods per fiscal year, with 90 days from
application to award. Tax credit agreements are negotiated by the Governor’s Office of
Business and Economic Development (GO-Biz) and approved by the California Competes Tax
Credit Committee. The GO-Biz California Competes team includes a deputy director, assistant
deputy director, tax credit specialist, and four tax credit analysts. The Committee members
include the State Treasurer, Director of the Department of Finance, two legislative appointees,
and the Director of GO-Biz. Documentation is shared with the Franchise Tax Board (FTB),
which must review records to ensure compliance with the terms of the agreements (this review
is discretionary for businesses with receipts under $2 million) and notify GO-Biz in case of
breach of agreement.

All businesses are eligible to apply. Phase | includes a quantitative analysis and assessment of
the cost-benefit ratio. Phase Il is a more comprehensive evaluation of the factors listed below
plus the Phase | ratio. The cost-benefit ratio is the amount of the credit requested divided by the
sum of aggregate employee compensation and aggregate investment. In general, applications
with the most advantageous (lowest) ratio move to Phase II but exceptions are allowed. The
ratios that allow projects to move forward are not established in advance and vary by application
period. Recent historical ratios have ranged from 0.224 for one period in 2019-20 to 0.0366 in
the first period of 2020-21.

New Employment Credit

The New Employment Credit is an income tax credit available to companies that hire full-time
employees within designated geographic areas (DGA). The credit amount is roughly 35% of
qualified wages. The designated geographic areas are defined by high unemployment/high
poverty census tracts.

Qualified employers must operate within a DGA, hire qualified employees, obtain a credit
reservation for the qualified employees, pay qualified wages (portion of wages that are between
150% and 350% of CA minimum wage), and have a net increase in jobs. An employee can
generate qualified wages for 60 months from the date of hire. Temporary help, retail, theater,
food and drink establishments, and casinos are generally excluded unless they are small
businesses.


                                                                                                 60
Qualified employees are those hired on or after the location’s designation as a DGA, perform at
least 50% of work in the DGA, receive wages that exceed 150% of California minimum wage,
work at least 35 hours per week, were previously unemployed, are veterans, received the
Earned Income Credit the previous year, are ex-offenders, or are current recipients of
CalWORKS     or county general assistance.

Employment Training Panel

The Employment Training Panel (ETP) provides funding to employers for training that upgrades
the skills of their workers. The program was created in 1982 to retain businesses and jobs in the
state, increase the competitiveness of California companies, and enhance workforce skills. Its
primary focus has been high-wage, high-skilled, secure jobs in priority industries. Construction
and manufacturing are the dominant industry recipients, followed by technical services, high
tech and healthcare.

The program works by reimbursing employers for approved training for incumbent workers and
for training to help unemployed workers re-enter the workforce. It is funded through a special
payroll tax. Only employers that are subject to the Employment Training Tax are eligible.
Businesses earn ETP funds after a trainee completes training and is retained for a minimum
period of time at a required wage in a job using the skills learned in training. Training for
unemployed workers Is provided principally through distributions from the Employment
Development Department and California Energy Commission.

The ETP is governed by an eight member panel with representatives from labor, business, and
government. Three members are appointed by the Governor, two by the Speaker of the
Assembly, and two by the Senate Pro Tem. The director of GO-Biz is an ex officio voting
member. The organization touts its partnerships with the California Workforce Development
Board and California Workforce Association.

The ETP mission appears to have expanded since its inception to adapt to changing workforce
needs. ETP programs and funds may be used to support “initiatives important to the Governor
and the Panel,” and they also implement incentive programs to support workforce needs in the
Clean Transportation Program, to support job creation in cooperation with GO-Biz, for
apprenticeship programs, and to provide training for veterans. In FY 2018-19, ETP reports $8.4
million was used to support creation of over 5000 jobs. For example, DreamWorks Animation
received ETP funds to “further develop a high performance work environment and maintain a
competitive edge in the animation marketplace.”

Further, the Vision and Mission have been updated in the 2020-21 Strategic Plan. The previous
vision stated, “The Employment Training Panel will support economic development in California
through strategic partnerships with business, labor and government.” The new vision reads:



                                                                                                 61
The Employment Training Panel will support future-oriented, sustainable economic development
and job training in California that is equitable and inclusive through strategic partnerships with
business, labor, and government.

A new element of its mission is to “Support high-wage, career track workforce training efforts for
disproportionately affected communities.” ETP also implemented a COVID Rapid
Reemployment and Retraining Pilot program in FY20-21.

The 2018-19 Annual Report notes that over three decades, the ETP has provided “$1.69 billion
for the successful training and employment retention of 1.3 million workers employed by over
87,628 California businesses.” For FY 2018-19, ETP completed 409 contracts totaling $77
million serving over 85,000 trainees. In addition, 365 contracts engaging 95,000 trainees were
active, and 432 new contracts for 97,000 trainees were approved. ETP’s FY 18-19 budget was
$92.3 million. The average post-retention wage for retrainees was $33.45/hour, and the average
for workers previously unemployed was $15.57/hour.

A July 2020 external assessment found that ETP’s “pay for performance approach to reimburse
employers that train their employees ... had positive and significant impacts on company sales
and firm size,” but with variations by firm size, age and sector.

Research and Development Tax Credit

The California Research and Development Tax Credit reduces the income or franchise tax for
businesses or individuals who paid or incurred qualified research expenses while conducting
qualified research in California. The credit is equal to the sum of 15% of qualified expenses that
exceed a base amount and 24% of basic research payments (available to corporate taxpayers
only). Qualified expenses include wages, supplies, rental or lease costs of computers, and in-
house or contract research expenses. Qualified expenses do not include payments for tangible
personal property eligible for specified sales or use tax exemption. Basic research refers to
certain types of research activities conducted by selected outside entities such as universities



                                                                                                   62
and independent research institutions on behalf of the taxpayer. Unused credits can be carried
over until the credit has been exhausted, but the carryover must be applied to the earliest tax
year possible. The tax credit is not refundable. Businesses do not have to claim the federal
research credit to claim the California credit.

Taxpayers may choose to take an alternative incremental credit “in which taxpayers are
assigned a smaller three-tiered fixed-base percentage and a reduced three-tiered credit rate
(1.49%, 1.98%, and 2.48%),” according to the Franchise Tax Board. According to the
Legislative Analyst's Office, this option allows otherwise ineligible corporate taxpayers to
participate in the program and “benefits those firms whose R&D expenses have not kept pace
with their gross receipts.”

For taxable years between January 1, 2020, and January 1, 2023, there is a $5 million limitation
on the application of business credits for taxpayers. Credits that are disallowed for this reason
may be carried over. This limitation does not apply to the Low Income Housing Credit.

The 2020-2021 Tax Expenditures Report estimated the revenue loss to the state general fund
from the R&D tax credit to be $2.4 billion in 2020-21. The same report noted that over 17,000
personal income tax returns claimed this credit in 2017, and over 4,300 corporate tax returns
claimed the credit in 2016.

The last in-state evaluation of this credit we were able to identify was performed by the
Legislative Analyst's Office in 2003. The revenue loss associated with the tax credit in 2003-04
was $519 million. Most that loss was associated with the corporate tax as opposed to the
personal income tax and represented about 5.3% of total corporate tax revenues that year.
Manufacturers accounted for the bulk of claims and value of the tax credit claimed.
Nonmanufacturing accounted for 43% of claims and 26% of the amount of tax credit claimed.

Findings are mixed from academic research and other state evaluations examining the impact
and effectiveness of research tax credits. It is difficult to draw clear conclusions from the
research because studies use different time periods, measures of impact and effectiveness, and
a wide variety of methodologies.

A literature review in a 2016 lowa study on Research Activities Tax Credits stated that the
 impact of state level incentives on outcomes such as migration patterns of research scientists
or contribution to variation in R&D spending among states is not clear, but that studies generally
find the federal tax credit does increase R&D expenditures. A 2017 analysis from the
 Department of Legislative Services in Maryland found that tax policies and the tax credit in
 particular “do not appear to be the most important factor in explaining the difference in R&D and
 innovation across states” and questioned whether the tax credit boosted long-term economic
growth through increased innovation. Pennsylvania’s Independent Fiscal Office in 2020 states,
“Research finds that the tax credit incentivizes state R&D expenditures, but evidence of that
effect cannot be seen using statewide jobs or R&D expenditure data.”

A recent Smart Incentives literature review on incentives for entrepreneurial firms (forthcoming)
also identified mixed effects on entrepreneurial activity. While some analyses of R&D tax credits
note various negative impacts on entrepreneurship as measured by new market entry and
startup creation, others suggest that R&D tax credits indirectly lead to new firm formation when
increased R&D is correlated with increases in employee departure to venture capital-backed
startups. Another analysis found that startups increased after the third year of tax credit




                                                                                                    63
implementation with increases continuing up to year 14, with accompanying improvements in
the “quality” (growth orientation) of entrepreneurship.

How California affects Oregon’s economic development activities and
incentive programs

California remains important to Oregon’s economic development activities as a source of
opportunities. Oregon still offers several cost advantages over California, including utilities, cost
of living (including housing), labor, and effective tax rates, although these advantages would
vary according to which communities are being compared. Local building codes and permitting
processes in Oregon also appear to continue to provide operating advantages to companies. In
this context, wnen Oregon competes with California, it is rarely about competing incentive
offers.

California’s incentive offerings have not had much effect on opportunities for Oregon, but in
recent years other states have become more aggressive and several have updated their
incentive programs as they increasingly compete with Oregon for opportunities coming out of
California. California itself may also be increasingly aggressive in keeping opportunities at
home, with a long list of incentives designed to help in-state businesses and a major proposed
expansion to the California Competes Tax Credit.



                                                                                                    64
There is some debate over the volume of activity leaving California and moving to other states.
During a March 2020 webinar, Bob Hess, Vice Chairman of Newmark’s Global Corporate
Services practice, said, “/WWhile some companies are very publicly shifting headquarters, most
are not fully exiting California.” Further, the Newmark team noted that even if companies move a
headquarters, for example, in many cases jobs and people remain in California. Businesses are
not completely abandoning the state. That said, more companies are looking outside of
California for additional options and second operations. The same is true for individuals. While
the media often mention the exodus from the Bay Area (and other large cities in the US), data
show that people who are moving tend to relocate to areas just outside the Bay Area or
elsewhere in California. Only a small percentage leave the state.

Three other major GO-Biz initiatives may affect the way economic development is practiced in
California, with potential implications for Oregon and other states. California’s focus on issues
like the future of work, job quality, worker empowerment, and the economic development
intersection with transportation, housing and community development all presage changes in
how state-level economic development will be practiced and perceived in the rest of the country.
All three initiatives got underway pre-COVID, so in some cases their work plan shifted in
response to the pandemic, especially for the Entrepreneurship Task Force. Themes of climate
mitigation, the shift to a carbon neutral economy, and clean energy technologies run throughout
these and much of California’s economic development work.

Effectiveness and Efficiency Topic # 3


In what new ways can Business Oregon present or market incentive programs to businesses
and local partners?




                                                                                                  70
Explaining incentives is one of the top communication challenges economic developers face.
Business incentives often involve financial transactions or tax strategies that elude easy
understanding for laypersons. Knowledgeable businesses and economic development
practitioners may also struggle to understand the eligibility and compliance criteria that vary
across programs (and that may be further complicated by political or legislative choices) as they
try to apply those criteria to their specific circumstances. Communication challenges are
compounded whenever administrative guidelines and statutory requirements change.
Information imbalances may exist between local officials and the lawyers or consultants of
certain businesses.

The problems this meeting is intended to address include: How can Business Oregon assist and
facilitate the confidence of especially understaffed local partners with necessary marketing and
managing of programs. How can differentiated needs or circumstances of users be categorized
(e.g., remote rural locations), and such user segments served appropriately with interactive
means to learn and promote programs or drawing on special assistance? How can various
types of cooperation between county assessors and economic development practitioners be
fostered and enhanced?

In today’s meeting, the steering group will discuss “what could be done differently” to share
information through outreach and technical assistance to help businesses and local partners to
better understand and make use of Oregon’s incentive programs in a manner consistent with
the state’s strategic priorities.

   1.   What incentive information do businesses and local partners need? (brief Smart
        Incentives presentation)
   2.   How might incentive program information be better presented or taught? Are the typical
        stages of a development project (as described in the presentation) an appropriate
        framework for organizing information? What other examples are there for organizing
        schemes?
   3.   What are the available channels for sharing incentives information with businesses and
        local partners? What format (FAQs, videos, one-pagers, infographics, case studies, in-
        person briefings, training curricula, etc.) is appropriate for each channel?
   4.   Is there potential to encourage and facilitate local communities, practitioners and county
        assessor offices to share techniques, best practices and lessons learned among
        themselves, including for local coordination and processes? What are the options for
        enabling peer learning among local partners?
   5.   Can incentives be integrated explicitly with Business Oregon’s strengths at marketing or
        with regionally based service provision?
   6.   What are the drivers and next steps for communication, marketing and technical
        assistance initiatives?
           a.   To what extent will state agency leadership, elected officials, and other
                stakeholders value Business Oregon’s ability to improve its communication
                around incentive programs?
           b.   Who within Business Oregon and other government offices or organizations
                should participate in future conversations about marketing and technical
                assistance initiatives?
           c.   Which options are most doable? What resources (staff, time, partnerships,
                systems) would be needed to implement them? Are these resources obtainable?
           d.   What roadblocks might be encountered? How can they be overcome?
           e.   Which, if any, of the options discussed should Business Oregon pursue? Who
                within Business Oregon should lead tasks related to the most promising options?
