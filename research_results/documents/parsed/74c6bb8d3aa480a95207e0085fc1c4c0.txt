Re:            2025 ORCA     Framework

Motion: Housing Stability Council adopts the following framework elements for the
ORCA and Low-Income Housing Tax Credits as presented [or will note revisions].

Overview

In this memo, we provide Housing Stability Council (HSC) with the final framework
elements to allow for updates in the 2025 Oregon Centralized Application (ORCA) and Low-
Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC) for adoption.          These components will be implemented
through a finalized Qualified Allocation Plan as well as the updated ORCA.

The Affordable Rental Housing division has been working to make updates to the ORCA
over the past several months. In October, we provided HSC with the draft framework
related to incorporating the 9% and 4% LIHTC programs into the ORCA process.         In
November, we provided HSC with the draft 2025 ORCA framework. Now, we are seeking
adoption of the final 2025 ORCA framework, which would become effective in February
2025.

At the January Housing Stability Council we will review key details of the framework
alongside a summary of public feedback gained through public comment period which
concluded on December 31*, 2024.




Summary of Final 2025 ORCA Framework

1.    ORCA-LIHTC Integration
Previously, OHCS    used Notices of Funding Availability to award LIHTC resources. For 2025,
we are integrating both 9% and 4% LIHTC into the ORCA application process.




                                       Jan 10, 2025 HSC   Material                           14
2.     ORCA Set-aside updates

There are two set-aside updates that would apply to all ORCA projects (including LIHTC
projects).

CSO & Rural set-asides *updated from previous recommendation*

     2024 ORCA         25% Culturally Specific Organization or Nonprofit organizations
                       building in rural Communities.
     2025 ORCA         Retain 2024 set-aside parameters through June 2025.
                       In the next biennia resource allocations, update the set-aside to
                       20% exclusively for Culturally Specific Organizations alongside a
                       target for rural projects that is responsive to ensuring rural access
                       and opportunity.
     Rationale         Based on public feedback, including as received from the HSC, we
                       are recommending
                                     that we retain the current set-asides instead of
                       changing mid-biennia. This lessens the shifts and uncertainty for
                       development teams and communities that have invested in project
                       pipelines. As we receive additional resource investments for the
                       next biennia, our recommendation is to bifurcate the strategies for
                       ensuring CSO access from those in rural communities in order to
                       ensure policies are responsive and effective.

                       Why: While the ORCA was initially launched with set-asides for all
                       funding resources to have 25% of resources to be available to
                       Culturally Specific Organizations and nonprofit organizations
                       building in Rural Communities. However, the set-aside approach
                       doesn’t ensure regional diversity that supports rural access to
                       funding. Response to concerns from rural communities regarding
                       access to project development & permanent financing; desire to
                       ensure that communities that need more time to get project
                       development strategies retain access to resources in a way thatis
                       more effective than shared set-aside funds.

ORCA Regional set-aside calculations

     2024 ORCA         Apply regional set-asides across all resources.
     2025 ORCA         Apply regional set-aside to development capital for resources
                       that do not have prescribed eligibility.
     Rationale         By doing this, we are striving to ensure that the Permanent
                       Supportive Housing development funds dedicated to Supportive
                       Housing Institute projects are able to be available to them and that
                       Preservation funds are effectively able to be prioritized through the
                       Preservation Framework.



                                    Jan 10, 2025 HSC   Material                                15
                          Why: The conceptual approach to looking at all funding resources in
                          aggregate was intended to ensure that the right types of resources
                          could serve the communities they work best in, i.e. if there was a
                          greater need for preservation resources in Eastern Oregon than new
                          construction, a set aside wouldn’t hinder an outsized portion of
                          those specific resources going to that region. However, as both PSH
                          and Preservation have other processes to prioritize investment,
                          including those resources within the regional set-asides has
                          created unnecessary risk that set-asides won’t be able to deliver
                          resources where needed to meet legislative intent for those funds.
                          By updating the approach, that risk is eliminated.

In addition to the above regional set-asides, the 9% LIHTC resources will continue to
dedicate 25% to fund preservation projects, and 10% to fund projects on tribal trust land.




3.   Rural Definition Update
With the launch of the ORCA, ARH worked with our Research Team to align the definition of
rural with the creation of the State of the State’s Housing Report. While the overall
definition will still calculate rural based on a combination of factors including density, the
update to the rural definition currently within the ORCA seeks to better recognize that some
more densely populated small cities may be within a larger metropolitan area but still far
enough away from the major cities in the MSA. The resulting addition to the rural definition
is that a city/town cannot have census tracts that are considered urban if the city/town is
located farther away than 30 miles, based on standard commutes, from the border of the
nearest respective Metropolitan Statistical
                                         Area (MSA).




4.   9% LIHTC Project Selection Process
Projects that apply for 9% LIHTC will be evaluated against the listed criteria to determine
the projects that will be eligible for the resource through the ORCA.

Mandatory:

     1-   Project includes features in the design, services, site location, or other project
          considerations that are tailored to the population being served (e.g. appropriate
          levels of supportive services for chronically homeless households, co-located ECE
          facility or supports for families with young children,      features for older adults and
          people with disabilities, transportation opportunities or unique design features for

        veterans, unique features or access to locations of cultural significance for
        communities of color, etc.).

   2-   Project demonstrates alignment with the Oregon Housing Needs Analysis and local
        jurisdiction housing production strategies. Small cities without an OHNA
        requirement are exempt from this.

   3-   Project has supporting documentation from a local Housing Authority
        demonstrating that      a commitment is in place to market available units to their wait

Supplemental (must meet 3):

5.   4% Housing Authority Owned set-aside
OHCS will work annually with the Housing Authorities of Oregon to establish a Housing
Authority Owned    (HAO) Private Activity Bond (PAB) Set-Aside. This HAO PAB Set-Aside will
be eligible for projects that are owned by Housing Authorities and do not require any state
loan or grant funding. The 2025 HAO PAB Set-Aside will be $100 million.




6.   4% PAB Timeline Prioritization rights
Where there is a risk that OHCS will not be able to substantially commit all available PAB to
projects in a given year, OHCS    reserves the right to reassign resources within the funded
pipeline or direct resources to projects on the ORCA waitlists based on the development
timeline.




7.   4% LIHTC Subsidy Limit Updates
While 9% LIHTC will be able to leverage a max of $3 million in gap subsidy per project, the
4% LIHTC will use per unit subsidy through the ORCA.

Subsidy limits are intended to:

     e   Scale according to bedroom size and AMI served;

     e   Respond to lower rent rates in rural communities with significantly lower AMI, which
         reduces the ability for projects to cover fixed costs of development;

     e   Include an additional building-level subsidy for projects that incorporate unique
         building design functions to serve the target population; and

     e   Allow for reasonable increases (up to 10% above subsidy limits, or in alignment with
         the maximum    PAB cap) when projects approach the final commitment of resources,
         upon approval by the OHCS     Finance Committee.

Note that these subsidy limits differ from those projects receiving only gap resources via
the ORCA process. All projects receiving federal tax credits must adhere to the limits listed
