Dear Co-Chairs:

In response to your request, attached is our submission of reduction options for the Higher
Education Coordinating Commission’s 2025-27 Legislatively Adopted Budget (LAB), as well as a
list of new and expanded HECC programs since July 1, 2021.

The HECC is the Oregon state agency responsible for access to and success in higher education
and workforce training. Through strategic coordination, funding, outreach, services, reporting,
and leadership for systems change, the HECC:

   e    Drives equitable postsecondary access and success for all Oregonians;

   e   Anticipates and meets workforce and economic needs; and

   e   Strengthens Oregon’s collective future by fostering research, innovation, learning, civic
       engagement, and cultural contributions.

The HECC’s mission has grown more urgent in the face of acute workforce needs that require
postsecondary education, particularly in health care, advanced manufacturing, K-12 and early
childhood education, and other sectors.

As a $4.4 billion agency, primarily General Fund (GF) and Lottery Fund (LF), that dedicates
almost all of its funding to colleges, universities, workforce organizations, and students, it is
difficult to meaningfully cut HECC’s budget without impacting access, opportunity, and support
for learners seeking postsecondary education and training.

That said, we took a deliberate approach to our submission for potential 2.5% and 5.0%
reductions. Consistent with the values and priorities expressed in the HECC’s Strategic Plan and
Equity Lens, we attempted to focus on reductions that met one or more of the following

To help inform our submission, we consulted directly with public university and community
college financial officers, financial aid directors, and other partners. Early in October, we shared
a list of potential reductions with those entities and received their feedback. This was
particularly helpful for assessing the likely impacts associated with cuts to the public university
and community college support funds. A high-level version of that information is included in the
“impacts” column of our submission.

Because the majority of HECC’s General Fund budget is dedicated to support for public
universities and community colleges, those funds necessarily bear the brunt of the reductions
here (at the 5% level, approximately $54 million and $41 million respectively). There are several
points worth noting. First, the actual impact of those reductions — how, for example, they would
affect tuition, program availability, staffing levels, employee compensation, and reserve fund
balances -- would be determined by college and university administrations and governing
boards. The HECC is not authorized to direct those decisions, and each institution would likely
strike a different balance between those options.

Second, cuts to state funding — at any level — would come at a particularly challenging time for
colleges and universities. While the 2025-27 LAB for the PUSF and CCSF is at the Current Service
Level (CSL), the actual cost of maintaining programs and services at last biennium’s level has
outpaced the State’s CSL estimate. And, because many institutions are unable or unwilling to
increase tuition revenue at the same rate as their expenses have been increasing, many are
making budget cuts this year -- resulting in hundreds of eliminated positions statewide along
with other reductions -- even under their 2025-27 LAB.

Finally, the impact of the college and university funding cuts contained in our submission would
primarily be felt in the second year of the biennium. Assuming the Legislature acts during its
regular session in February-March 2026, colleges and universities would primarily reflect those
reductions to state revenue in their 2026-27 institutional budgets. Thus, a 5% reduction in the
biennium’s budget would appear as an approximately 10% reduction in an institution’s state

Applying the principles described earlier in this letter resulted in the following key features of
our submission:

    1.   Our submission fully shields state need-based financial aid, the Oregon Opportunity
         Grant (OOG), from cuts to LAB. This reflects a longstanding priority by the HECC to focus
         the state’s postsecondary resources on Oregonians who have the least means to afford
         college.
    2.   Our submission fully shields the Oregon Tribal Student Grant (OTSG) from cuts to LAB.
         Maintaining the State’s commitment to our nine federally-recognized tribes seems
         particularly important given our nation’s history of unkept promises to tribal nations.
    3.   Even after preserving OOG and OTSG at or near LAB, we ensured that Oregon
         community college and public university support funds were not cut by more than the
         overall targets.

To accomplish the above while achieving the reduction targets, we were required to propose a
number of painful reductions. Some of the most notable include:

    e    The HECC agency’s capacity would be reduced by more than 10% in the second year of
         the biennium. Some specific activities that we would slow or eliminate are described in
         our submission. Additionally, likely outcomes would include reduced reporting to the
         Legislature, lower levels of responsiveness to partners, students, and job-seekers, less
         inclusive and robust processes for policy and coordination decisions, and diminished
         progress towards HECC’s strategic goals of increasing the relevance, alignment,
         affordability, inclusivity, and economic impact of our postsecondary education system.
    e    Important and targeted workforce programs through which HECC makes grants to local
         partners, including Industry Engagement, Work Experiences, Local Competitiveness, and
         Maritime Workforce Development, have been reduced below their 2023-25 funding
         level.
    e    Career Pathways funding for community colleges, which was recently expanded greatly
         through the Legislature’s investment in Future Ready Oregon, would return closer to
         pre-pandemic levels. To sustain career-oriented education, colleges would need to draw
         from other general revenues.

    e   Atthe 5% reduction level, the HECC’s Childcare Grant for student parents would not
        make awards to new students in the second year of the biennium. Many student parents
        would continue to be eligible for childcare subsidies under Oregon’s Employment
        Related Day Care (ERDC) program.
    e   Atthe 5% reduction level, the Oregon Promise would no longer accept new students,
        starting in 2026-27. We would continue to fund current (2025-26) Oregon Promise
        students for as long as they remain eligible. This would generate modest savings in the
        current biennium and considerably more in 2027-29. For most lower-and middle-income
        community college students, tuition would remain partly or fully covered by the Pell
        Grant and the Oregon Opportunity Grant.

As a State agency with one of the largest GF/LF budgets, we appreciate that HECC will likely
incur reductions in connection with any significant budget rebalance. While we have attempted
to describe an approach that would minimize the direct impacts on Oregon students and others
we serve, cuts of this magnitude — most of which would be experienced by institutions and
students during the second year of the biennium — would cause harm. Especially given the
severe challenges already facing the sector due to federal funding recissions, demographic
changes, and the growing needs of students, the cuts described here would put postsecondary
education further out of reach for some Oregonians. Still, we recognize that the Legislature and
Governor must make difficult decisions to balance the State’s 2025-27 budget, and appreciate
the opportunity to help inform that work.
